Lesson 0004
Loop 3: The External Feedback Loop

The only loop where humans talk to other humans. Slow, ugly, and the only one that can tell you whether you're building the right thing.

What this loop is

"This includes a wide range of tactics like asking a few friends for feedback, launching to alpha testers, or putting the code into production with A/B testing. These tactics are usually slow, rarely taking less than hours and sometimes taking days or even weeks."

— Andrew Ng, The Batch Issue 359

Loop 3 is the only loop that involves other humans with context you don't have. Friends, alpha testers, paying customers, strangers on Twitter. They use your software in ways you didn't anticipate, with needs you didn't encode into the spec.

The honesty test: if you removed all external users from your product, would you still know what to build next? If yes, you don't have a Loop 3 problem — you have a Loop 2 problem (you're not using your context advantage). If no, you've discovered why Loop 3 exists.

The cadence is hours to weeks

Andrew gives the cadence as "rarely taking less than hours and sometimes taking days or even weeks." Why so slow?

  • Real users need real context — they have to discover your product, try it, form an opinion, and articulate it. That takes days, not minutes.
  • Sample size — a single friend's reaction is anecdote. Five friends is signal. Fifty users is data. Each tier takes longer.
  • Production loops are slower still — A/B tests need statistical power, which means traffic, which means time.

Don't fake it. "Loop 3 in 5 minutes" — sending the agent to scrape Twitter and cluster complaints — is a Loop 2 activity wearing a Loop 3 costume. Real Loop 3 involves real humans you cannot fully simulate.

How Loop 3 feeds back into the others

Andrew is explicit about the feedback chain:

"This data informs the developer vision, which in turn continues to drive the detailed product spec, which in turn drives the coding agent."

— Andrew Ng, The Batch Issue 359

The flow is one-directional:

Loop 3external
feedback → Loop 2vision →
spec → Loop 1agent
codes

External feedback updates your vision. The vision becomes an updated spec. The spec drives the agent. The whole 3-loop system is one big loop that runs at the speed of its slowest part — Loop 3.

The leverage insight: improving Loop 3's feedback quality — not its speed — is where the real gains are. A weekly call with five users who can articulate what they want beats a daily survey of fifty users who can't.

The "partial PM" role

Andrew closes the Loop 3 section with this:

"With coding agents speeding up software development, more engineers are starting to play a partial product management role. For many engineers who are growing into this role, the hardest part is shaping the product vision and striking a balance between building (bridging the gap between vision and spec) and getting user feedback to evolve the vision."

— Andrew Ng, The Batch Issue 359

Three implications for solo devs:

1Your role expands, it doesn't shrink

The agent handles typing. You handle vision + spec + external feedback. The total cognitive load doesn't decrease — it shifts toward higher-leverage work.

2"Building" and "listening" are both required

Andrew: "It is important to do both!" Most solo devs under-invest in Loop 3 because listening is harder than shipping. The discipline is to schedule both.

3The hardest part is the vision

Engineers moving into PM roles get stuck on shaping the product vision, not on writing the spec. grill-me is your friend here.

How AI can — and can't — help Loop 3

Andrew is balanced here:

"AI-native teams are increasingly using AI to help shape product direction, for example, automating the gathering and analysis of usage data, summarizing written and verbal customer feedback, or carrying out competitive analysis."

— Andrew Ng, The Batch Issue 359

But also:

"For pretty much all the products I'm involved in, I see humans as having a significant context advantage over current AI systems… thus humans play a critical role."

— Andrew Ng, The Batch Issue 359

Loop 3 activity Can AI help? Where humans still own it
Gather usage data Yes — log everything, summarise with an agent. Decide what to log in the first place.
Summarise written feedback Yes — cluster complaints, extract themes. Read the rare, articulate complaint yourself.
Summarise verbal feedback (call transcripts) Yes — transcribe, summarise, extract action items. Be on the call. You cannot delegate presence.
Competitive analysis Yes — scrape competitor sites, summarise positioning. Decide which competitors matter.
Decide what to build next No — that's vision. Always human.
Decide who to talk to No — that's context advantage. Always human.

Setting up Loop 3 cheaply

You don't need fifty users to start Loop 3. Andrew's typing app started with his daughter. A minimal Loop 3 has three components:

1A feedback channel

A shared doc, a Slack channel, a form, a Twitter DM list, a Signal group with 3 friends. Doesn't matter which.

2A weekly ritual

Block 60 minutes on your calendar. Read the feedback. Write down 1-3 things to change in the spec. That's the whole loop.

3A vision-update trigger

After the weekly ritual, edit your VISION.md (the file Steinberger uses as the agent's anchor — see Agentic Engineering). The next Loop 1 run reads the updated vision and produces accordingly.

The trap: collecting feedback but never acting on it. A doc full of complaints that nobody reads is just a graveyard. The weekly ritual is non-negotiable.

Mapping to Matt Pocock skills

Loop 3 is where Matt's framework thins out. Most of his skills serve Loop 1 and Loop 2. But two patterns apply:

Action Items

  1. Pick 3 humans for your first Loop 315 min Friends, colleagues, domain experts. People who will use the thing or know people who will. Reach out today.
  2. Create a VISION.md30 min Anchor file at the project root: what the product is, who it's for, what it's not. The next Loop 1 run reads it. (See Agentic Engineering for the VISION.md pattern.)
  3. Schedule the weekly 60-minute Loop 3 ritual2 min Block it on your calendar. Recurring. Non-negotiable. Without this, feedback collects but nothing happens.
  4. Run grill-me on your vision20 min After one cycle of external feedback, use Matt's grill-me skill to test whether your vision has actually updated. If you can't state the change in one sentence, your Loop 3 isn't working.
