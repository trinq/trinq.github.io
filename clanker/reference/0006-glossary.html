Glossary

Farhan's vocabulary, with operational definitions.

This is a reference document, not a lesson. Use it the way you'd use any glossary: look up a term when it shows up in a conversation, when onboarding a new hire, or when writing a doc that needs to use a shared word the same way the rest of the team does.

Terms are organized into four sections matching the structure of the talk:

  • Frame — the meta-claim about what the work is now
  • Vocabulary — the terms Farhan uses to describe the new shape
  • Levers — the policies and structures for managing the transition
  • Strategies — the hiring and growth moves

Each term has: a one-line definition, the section it belongs to, the lesson it appears in, and a "use when" note that tells you when to reach for it.

Frame

FRAME
The Bottleneck
bot-uhl-neck
The claim that the rate-limiting step in software development moved, in 2024–2025, from executing (typing, recalling syntax, writing functions) to framing (deciding what to build, validating that it was built right, owning the outcome). The bottleneck is what determines what an engineer's job is. When the bottleneck moves, the job moves.
Use when
Explaining why any specific change in the job is happening. "X used to be the bottleneck; now it's not."

Vocabulary

VOCABULARY
Clanker engineer
klang-ker
A shorthand for "the engineer who knows how to direct AI work, frame problems, and own outcomes." The name comes from the Star Wars droids — unfeeling working-class machines that get the job done. In this curriculum's usage, "clanker engineer" is not derogatory; it is descriptive. The job is closer to managing capable but unthinking labor than to traditional craft.
Use when
Naming the role when writing JDs, leveling guides, or team-charter docs. Useful in opposition to "craft engineer" (the old ideal).
Tone
Descriptive, not pejorative. Adjust if your team is not onboard.
VOCABULARY
Centaur
sen-tor
A half-human, half-AI collaboration shape. The human does the typing-orchestration, the AI does the typing. Borrowed from post-Deep-Blue human + engine chess pairs (1997–2017) that beat either human or engine alone. In coding terms: the dominant 2022–2024 model, where the human was the driver and the LLM was the autocomplete. The shape is now obsolete because typing is no longer the bottleneck.
Use when
Naming what the team is moving away from. "We used to work in centaur mode."
See also
Lesson 0004, Augmented engineer
VOCABULARY
Augmented engineer
awg-men-ted
The new shape of the role. The human frames problems at a high level (spec, intent, validation criteria), the AI executes at scale, the human owns the outcome. Distinct from "centaur" because the human is no longer at the keyboard orchestrating keystrokes. Distinct from "pure human" because the AI is doing most of the typing.
Use when
Naming what the team is moving toward. "We're trying to be augmented engineers."
Synonyms
"Clanker engineer" (this curriculum's preferred term), "AI-native engineer"
VOCABULARY
Pure human
pyoor hyoo-muhn
The pre-2020 mode. No AI in the loop. Human writes, reviews, ships. Useful as a foil for the augmented model, but the term is not nostalgic — almost no real engineering work operates in pure-human mode today.
Use when
Contrasting the three collaboration shapes: pure-human, centaur, augmented.
See also
Lesson 0004
VOCABULARY
Reflexiveness
ri-flek-siv-nes
Farhan's term for the meta-skill of writing prompts, scripts, and tests, and validating AI outputs. "Reflexive" here means: the loop of frame → execute → validate is now the worker's primary craft, not a side activity. An engineer with high reflexiveness can point AI at a problem, observe the output, course-correct, and converge on a working result.
Use when
Naming the skill you are hiring or training for. "We're looking for reflexiveness, not just execution speed."
Related
Leverage
VOCABULARY
Leverage
lev-er-ij
A person with high leverage is one whose decisions and direction get multiplied by AI processes. A senior engineer with strong leverage might direct ten parallel AI tasks to a working system. A mid-level engineer with low leverage is bottlenecked by their own typing. The interview signal for leverage is whether the candidate can scale their judgment beyond their own execution speed.
Use when
Describing what you are hiring for at the senior level. "We need a principal with leverage."
See also
Reflexiveness, Lesson 0005
VOCABULARY
Collateral learning
kuh-lat-er-ul lur-ning
The learning that used to happen as a side effect of doing the work — picking up the codebase by reading, fixing, and shipping it. The curriculum's claim is that the centaur model left junior engineers with rich collateral learning; the augmented model leaves them with thin collateral unless deliberate training is added. The name borrows from "collateral damage" — unintended side effects — and is not moralizing; it is a description of a pattern that needs to be replaced with intention.
Use when
Naming the gap when a junior appears to be shipping but not internalizing. "Their collateral learning is thin."
See also
Lesson 0002
VOCABULARY
Council of LLMs
koun-sil ov el-el-emz
A pattern of running multiple AI processes in parallel and synthesizing their outputs. Example: a spec is given to three different LLMs, each produces an implementation, the human reads all three and selects / merges / improves. Farhan describes this as a way to surface options and reduce single-model bias. The cost is time and attention; the benefit is higher quality and faster learning by comparison.
Use when
Describing a deliberate strategy for high-stakes work, not a default. "Let's council this — three models, then I'll pick."
Risk
Reviewer fatigue. Reserve for work where the cost of being wrong is high.

Levers

LEVER
Ownership of responsibility
oh-ner-ship
A principle for accountability under AI work: responsibility for an outcome rests with the human whose name is on the ship decision, regardless of who or what produced the artifacts. The human cannot delegate understanding of the work to the AI that produced it. This is a policy lever — it sets the line that the team operates on, and is the single most important thing to be explicit about.
Use when
Postmortems, ship decisions, promotions, and any conversation where someone says "the AI wrote that."
See also
Lesson 0003
LEVER
Spec authoring
spek aw-ther-ing
The new site of human craft. A spec is a written artifact that frames a problem — user-facing behavior, edge cases, API shape, scope boundaries. The author commits to the spec; the implementation is delegated. The skill is in the framing: precise enough that AI can build it, opinionated enough that the result is on-bounds, and explicit about what is out of scope.
Use when
Naming the artifact that replaces the keyboard as the collaborative site. "Pair on the spec."
See also
Lesson 0004, Three-mode interview model

Strategies

STRATEGY
Thousand interns (analogy)
thou-zend in-terns
Farhan's analogy for the new working relationship with AI. "It's like having a thousand interns" describes the characteristics of the work: cheap to spawn, needs a clear assignment, output requires review, course-correct cheaply. It is not a slur on interns, and it is not a comment on AI capability. It is a description of the management discipline the work now requires.
Use when
Naming the operating model. Useful for senior engineers who resist being called "managers" — the role has shifted to one with management discipline, even if the title hasn't changed.
See also
Lesson 0005
STRATEGY
Three-mode interview model
three-mohd
A framework for interview design that maps roles to the mode of working they'll actually do. Mode 1: open book with AI (velocity signal). Mode 2: spec authoring with AI as builder (framing signal). Mode 3: learning loops with AI as tutor (trajectory signal). The model is not "use all three on every candidate" — it is "use the mode that matches the role."
Use when
Designing or auditing interview loops. Especially useful when your current loop is mostly "whiteboard without AI" — that is tuned for the pre-bottleneck-shift era.
