Responsibility Can't Be Delegated

The one policy lever you actually control as a lead.

This is the most concrete lesson in the series. Of everything Farhan says, this is the part that translates directly into something you can write down on Monday and ship on Tuesday: a code review policy. No vendor evaluation. No tool rollout. Just a rule about whose name goes where.

The principle

Farhan is brief at 20:46: "We do not let AI take responsibility at Shopify. Responsibility can be shared, but it cannot be given away. Your name on the PR."

Responsibility can be shared,
but it cannot be given away.
Farhan Thawar, paraphrased at 20:46

This is not a slogan. It is a precise position with a precise consequence: when something goes wrong in production — an outage, a security incident, a customer-visible regression — there is a person who answers for it. That person is the one whose name is on the PR. The LLM is not a person. It cannot answer. So it cannot own the outcome.

Why this is the lever you actually control

Most of the shifts in the talk are descriptive — they describe where the SDLC time goes now, what bottlenecks look like, why learning is the collateral. Useful framing. Not directly actionable.

Responsibility is prescriptive. You can write it down. You can enforce it in your team's PR template. You can audit it in retros. It is the single sentence Farhan gives you that can become a policy without buying a new tool.

The leadership gap: most AI rollouts stall because the lead asks "should we adopt Cursor?" or "which model?". Both are tools questions. The harder question — and the one your team is waiting for you to answer — is "who owns what the AI ships?" This is that answer.

The shared-but-not-given distinction

Farhan's wording matters: shared, not given away. The principle permits:

  • Tagging the LLM in the PR description (e.g., Co-authored-by: cursor[bot]) — transparency about how the code was made
  • Using AI for review, with a human reviewer still required to merge
  • Pair-programming with AI as the second pair — the human is still the pilot
  • Distributing review load across multiple humans — each owns their approval

The principle forbids:

  • Pushing AI-authored code to production without a human signer
  • Claiming AI made the change when something breaks ("the bot did it")
  • Treating the LLM as a co-owner of the outcome
  • Letting AI-only PRs merge on weekends when no human is around to sign

What the policy looks like in practice

Here is a draft PR template you can adapt. The required fields are the ones that enforce the principle; the optional fields are transparency improvements.

/.github/pull_request_template.md
[Short summary of change]
Author & Responsibility
Required: human signer Optional: AI tool used (cursor / claude-code / copilot / other)

By opening this PR, I confirm that I have reviewed every line of the diff, understand its behavior in production, and accept responsibility for the outcome. AI assistance is permitted and will be tagged in the description, but ownership of this change is mine.

AI assistance (optional, encouraged)
  • Tool(s) used: ____________
  • What AI did: ____________ (e.g., initial draft, refactor, test scaffolding, review)
  • What I did: ____________ (e.g., problem framing, validation, architecture choice)
Reviewers
  • Human reviewer(s): required for merge
  • AI review: encouraged (Council of LLMs, Cursor review, etc.) but does not replace human signoff

Notice what is not in the template: a section for "AI approval." There is no such thing. AI can review. A human must sign.

The "LGTM" trap and how this policy closes it

In lesson 0001 we saw that humans were not actually reviewing code well — the default "LGTM" was rubber-stamping. In lesson 0002 we saw that code review wasn't really producing learning either. The responsibility principle closes that loop in one move:

If you must put your name on every PR, "LGTM" becomes a heavier word. You cannot casually approve code you have not read, because you now own it. The policy forces the human to actually read, because the human is now actually accountable.

The compounding effect: when humans are accountable, they read more carefully. When they read more carefully, they catch more. When they catch more, AI-generated bugs surface earlier. The policy doesn't make the AI better — it makes the human's attention load-bearing again.

Edge cases

Some situations deserve explicit handling before you publish the policy:

  • Auto-generated dependency bumps. Dependabot-style PRs have no human signer by default. Add a rule: every auto-PR must be co-signed by an owner of the affected module before merge.
  • Hotfixes during an incident. The pressure to skip review is highest here. The rule: hotfixes still need a human signer, even if the human is the on-call. Sign on-call, not zero signers.
  • Generated migrations. AI migrations can be large and look correct. The rule: any migration touching >N rows or schema needs an explicit "I have read this migration" line in the PR. Farhan does not specify N; pick one your team is comfortable with and write it down.
  • AI-only commits in a shared repo. Some teams want AI to open drafts with no human author. The rule: drafts are fine. Merge requires human signer.

What you should not write into the policy

Avoid these — they sound responsible but erode the principle:

  • "AI must be cited in every commit" — useful for transparency, but the policy lever is who owns the PR, not who wrote the lines. Conflating them leads to theater.
  • "Every PR must be reviewed by AI before a human" — encourages rubber-stamping in the other direction. Review load is the human's choice.
  • "If AI confidence is below X%, require human review" — there is no well-defined AI confidence score that maps to a code property. False precision.

Exercise: Write your team's policy

  1. Open your team's PR template (or create one if it doesn't exist).
  2. Add the "Author & Responsibility" section from the draft above. Adjust the wording so it sounds like your team, not like Farhan.
  3. Decide the answer to the four edge cases above. Write each answer down in your team wiki — don't leave them implicit.
  4. Pick a date to flip the template on. Two weeks is usually right: long enough to socialize, short enough to not slip.
  5. At the rollout date, walk through one example PR with the team and demonstrate how the new fields get filled in. Don't just enable the template and hope.

What you should walk away with: a one-sentence policy ("the human whose name is on the PR owns the outcome") and a draft PR template that enforces it. Two documents. Monday morning. That's the lever.

Ask follow-up questions

  • "How do I get buy-in for this from a senior engineer who thinks it's bureaucratic?"
  • "What if my team is too small for a real PR template — is there a lighter version?"
  • "How does this interact with the Council of LLMs idea from Farhan — does AI review count?"
  • "Show me how this would look different in a regulated industry (finance, health) vs a startup."
