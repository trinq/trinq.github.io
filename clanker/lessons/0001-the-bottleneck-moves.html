The Bottleneck Always Moves

The single idea that explains every other shift in Farhan Thawar's talk.

If you watch the whole 26 minutes of Farhan's talk, you will hear about coding, reviewing, hiring, infrastructure, prototypes, and interns. Underneath all of it is one idea borrowed from Eliyahu Goldratt's The Goal: the bottleneck always moves.

This is the first lesson because if you can explain this idea in your own words, you can explain the rest of the talk in your own words. It is the seed.

The SDLC, before

For most of software history, the time-consuming part of building software was the coding. The SDLC looked roughly like this:

where the bulk of time went other stages
Plan
small
Code
bulk
Test
small
Review
small
Ship
small

Farhan calls this out at 00:55: the "big blue box" was always the coding step.

The SDLC, now

When AI writes most of the code, the box that used to be huge shrinks. Time has to go somewhere — and it goes to the stages around the coding:

where the bulk of time goes now
Plan
bigger
Code
smaller
Validate
bigger
Review
bigger
Ship
same

The coding didn't disappear. It moved — into a different shape (parallel agents, sequential critique loops) — and it stopped being the bottleneck.

What is a bottleneck, exactly?

Farhan borrows from Goldratt's The Goal. In a factory, the bottleneck is the single stage that determines how much product can come out. You can speed up every other stage and the factory doesn't get faster. Only the bottleneck determines throughput.

The punchline of the book: every time you fix the bottleneck, a new bottleneck appears somewhere else. The constraint doesn't disappear; it relocates.

Farhan's claim: the AI step of writing the code is moving the bottleneck to other parts of the SDLC. As you fix those, new bottlenecks appear. That's fine — that's the job. Find the bottleneck, move it, find the next one.

Why this is the seed idea

Once you accept that the bottleneck always moves, the rest of the talk becomes a list of which bottlenecks are showing up now:

  • Code review is now the bottleneck 20:46 — more code is being generated than humans can review. Shopify's answer: a Council of LLMs.
  • Planning is now the bottleneck — when you can build in a day, the question "what should we build?" dominates the calendar.
  • Hiring calibration is now a bottleneck 19:39 — what does it mean to evaluate an engineer when the AI is doing the typing? Three modes: no-AI, AI-optional, AI-mandatory.
  • Responsibility is a permanent bottleneck — your name on the PR. The LLM tags along but the human owns it.

Each of these becomes legible as "the bottleneck moved here next."

The lesson for leading a team

When a team is resisting the shift — saying "AI writes sloppy code" or "we can't trust what it generates" — they are usually pointing at the old bottleneck. The code. That bottleneck is shrinking whether they engage with it or not.

The leadership question is not "should we use AI?" — that bottleneck is being outsourced by the clanker regardless. The question is: which new bottleneck is now strangling us, and who owns unblocking it?

"If the AI is better at writing code, should you write code anymore? It's an open question. But the bottleneck moves. That's the job." — Farhan Thawar

Exercise: Map your team's current bottlenecks

  1. List the five stages of your team's SDLC: Plan, Code, Test, Review, Ship.
  2. For each stage, write one sentence: "Where is time actually going right now?" Not where it should go — where it does.
  3. Mark the stage that, if you doubled its capacity tomorrow, would change your shipping rate the most. That's your current bottleneck.
  4. Ask: did this stage become the bottleneck after the team started using AI, or was it always the bottleneck? If the former, you have evidence the bottleneck has moved.
  5. Bring the map to your next 1:1 with a senior engineer. Ask: "Which of these is the new bottleneck you'd pick if you had to fix one?" Compare their answer to yours.

What you should walk away with: a one-line answer to the question "why is my job different now?" — because the bottleneck moved. Everything else in the talk is a downstream consequence.

Ask follow-up questions

This lesson is short by design. If anything is unclear — or if you want to push back on the framing — ask the agent. Useful follow-ups:

  • "Show me a real example of a bottleneck moving in our team's workflow."
  • "What's the opposite case — when has the bottleneck not moved after AI was introduced?"
  • "How do I explain this to an engineer who thinks AI hype is overstated?"
