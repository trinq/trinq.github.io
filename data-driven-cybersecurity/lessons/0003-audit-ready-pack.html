Lesson 0003 · Data-driven cybersecurity · Arc 1: The metric discipline

The Audit-Ready Metrics Pack

A board pack satisfies the board. An audit-ready pack satisfies UBCK, ISO 27001, PDPD, and the next examiner who walks in. You need both.

Why this lesson third

Lessons 0001 and 0002 gave you the board pack (10 metrics) and the per-metric runbook (seven-step lifecycle). Both are excellent. Neither survives a regulatory examination on its own.

A Vietnamese securities firm does not get to choose between satisfying the board or satisfying the regulator. You must satisfy both — with the same metric set. The board sees the same numbers the regulator sees; the difference is the framing. Lesson 0003 is the lesson that makes this work.

Mariano Mattei, Data-Driven Cybersecurity ch. 1.5 This book uses NIST CSF V2.0 as a foundation for its discussions. The focus is not on NIST itself but on the broader application of cybersecurity metrics across frameworks. This approach allows readers to benefit from the insights provided here, no matter which framework they rely on.

Mattei is right that NIST CSF is the lingua franca. But for your firm, NIST CSF alone is insufficient. UBCK (Ủy ban Chứng khoán Nhà nước) sets VN-securities-specific expectations; ISO 27001 is increasingly required for VN brokers; PDPD (Nghị định 13/2023) is in force since 1 July 2023 with a 72-hour breach-notification clock. Each framework asks for slightly different evidence from the same data. The cross-reference matrix is the answer.

For your 9-entity firm, the lesson lands with three specific pressures the ciso-playbook course did not address:

  1. The UBCK examination is coming. Whether the firm has been audited before or not, the next circular review will ask "where is your evidence?" The audit-ready pack is the answer file. It is the artifact you hand to the examiner, not the dashboard you keep internally.
  2. PDPD 72-hour clock is binary. Unlike NIST CSF or ISO 27001, which are maturity-model frameworks, PDPD has a hard 72-hour breach-notification deadline. The metric "time-from-incident-confirmed-to-MPS-notification (P95)" must be ≤ 72 hours, full stop. This is a different category of metric: a regulatory ceiling, not a maturity indicator.
  3. ISO 27001 surveillance audits require continuous evidence. If the firm has or wants ISO 27001 certification, the surveillance auditor will ask for 12 months of evidence per Annex A control. The metric pack must map each board-pack metric to the relevant Annex A control(s) so evidence is auto-collected, not assembled at audit time.

By the end of this lesson you will have produced the audit-ready cross-reference matrix (in Vietnamese) — 10 board-pack metrics × 5 frameworks (UBCK ICT risk circular, ISO 27001:2022 Annex A, PDPD, NIST CSF 2.0, CIS Top 18 v8) — showing which metric satisfies which control, what evidence the examiner will request, and how the evidence is auto-collected. This matrix is the artifact you keep on file and bring out the morning the examiner arrives.

The five frameworks — what each one actually demands

Mattei's chapter 1.5 introduces three frameworks (HITRUST, CIS Top 18, NIST CSF) and references ISO 27001 as the international standard. For your firm, the set is five frameworks, layered:

1. UBCK ICT Risk Circular (Ủy ban Chứng khoán Nhà nước)

What it is: the specific circular from UBCK governing ICT risk management at VN securities firms. Search thuvienphapluat.vn for the latest version (circulars update; the ciso-playbook course's RESOURCES.md flagged this as a moving target).

What it demands: documented ICT risk management programme; named accountable officer; annual ICT risk assessment; incident-response readiness; business continuity arrangements; periodic reporting to UBCK. The circular sets the floor — compliance is non-negotiable, not aspirational.

What it does not demand: a specific metric set or specific framework (NIST CSF / ISO 27001 are accepted as evidence of compliance, not mandated as the framework itself). This is the regulator's gift: you can use ISO 27001 controls to satisfy UBCK expectations.

For your firm: UBCK is the primary regulator. The audit-ready pack must show, for each board-pack metric, the UBCK circular clause it satisfies. If the circular requires "incident-response readiness," the metric "tabletop exercise participation by executive team" is the evidence.

2. ISO/IEC 27001:2022 (Information Security Management Systems)

What it is: the international management-system standard for information security. Increasingly required for VN securities firms (and certainly expected if the firm has any foreign counterparty or parent).

What it demands: a documented ISMS (information security management system) covering all Annex A controls (93 controls in 4 themes — Organizational, People, Physical, Technological); risk treatment plan; statement of applicability; internal audit programme; management review; continuous improvement. Annex A control 5.30 (ICT readiness for business continuity) is the one most directly relevant to your recovery metrics.

What it does not demand: a specific metric for every control — it demands evidence of effectiveness, which a metric can provide but is not the only form of evidence.

For your firm: if you have or want ISO 27001 certification, every board-pack metric must map to at least one Annex A control. The mapping is the audit defence.

3. PDPD (Nghị định 13/2023/NĐ-CP — Personal Data Protection)

What it is: Vietnam's Personal Data Protection Act, in force since 1 July 2023. Governs processing of personal data by any organisation operating in Vietnam or processing VN residents' data.

What it demands: personal data inventory and classification; lawful basis for processing; consent management; data subject rights (access, correction, deletion); impact assessment for sensitive processing; breach notification to MPS (Ministry of Public Security) and affected data subjects within 72 hours of incident confirmation; cross-border transfer controls. Penalties include fines up to 5% of prior year's revenue.

What it does not demand: a specific framework or maturity model. PDPD is regulation, not a control framework.

For your firm: PDPD has two specific metric implications. First, the personal-data inventory metric (% of data assets classified for PDPD scope) is a board-pack candidate from lesson 0001's metric #7. Second, the 72-hour breach-notification clock is a regulatory ceiling — the metric is binary (compliant or non-compliant), not a maturity indicator.

4. NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0

What it is: the US National Institute of Standards and Technology's voluntary cybersecurity framework. Globally the de facto vocabulary for cybersecurity program design. CSF 2.0 (released 2024) added Govern as a top-level function (previously grouped under Identify in CSF 1.x).

What it demands: nothing mandatory (it's voluntary), but everything language-defining. Every cybersecurity conversation, in any country, expects you to be conversant with the CSF function structure. UBCK examiners will use the CSF vocabulary in their questioning even if they don't cite the framework directly.

CSF 2.0 functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. The book (and your course) maps each chapter and lesson onto these functions.

For your firm: the CSF is your internal organising structure. Every board-pack metric maps to one CSF function; every cross-reference to UBCK / ISO 27001 / PDPD also maps to a CSF function (because the crosswalks are public). The CSF is the hub.

5. CIS Critical Security Controls (CIS Top 18) v8

What it is: the Center for Internet Security's prioritised set of 18 controls (previously 20). Implementation Groups (IG1, IG2, IG3) stratify controls by organisation size and sensitivity.

What it demands: nothing mandatory. But CIS is the most-operationally-actionable framework — if a SOC analyst must implement a control, CIS tells them how. UBCK examiners and ISO 27001 auditors both accept CIS as evidence of operational implementation.

For your firm: CIS Implementation Group 2 (IG2) is the right target for a mid-size securities firm (600 people, 9 entities, sensitive data). CIS maps to both NIST CSF and ISO 27001 Annex A publicly — the crosswalks are free.

Layering, not substituting — the relationship between frameworks

The five frameworks overlap heavily. This is the design feature, not a bug. The same data satisfies multiple frameworks. The cross-reference matrix exploits the overlap.

FrameworkLayerWhat it gives youWhat you give it
UBCK ICT Risk Circular Regulator (VN) Legal authority to operate; examiner expectations Evidence of compliance with ICT risk circular clauses
ISO 27001:2022 Management system (international) Certification if you pursue it; global credibility Documented ISMS + Annex A control evidence
PDPD Regulation (VN) Legal right to process personal data Personal-data inventory + breach-notification compliance
NIST CSF 2.0 Vocabulary (global) Common language with any cybersecurity professional Function-aligned program evidence
CIS Top 18 v8 IG2 Operational implementation (global) SOC-actionable control guidance Implementation evidence per control
The layering principle. Each framework operates at a different altitude. UBCK at 50,000 feet (do you have an ICT risk programme?). ISO 27001 at 30,000 feet (does your ISMS cover the controls?). NIST CSF at 20,000 feet (does your program cover the functions?). CIS Top 18 at 5,000 feet (is each control actually implemented?). PDPD sits orthogonal to all of these (does your personal-data processing comply?). The audit-ready pack shows the same metric at multiple altitudes — the data is the same, the framing is different.

The UBCK examination — what actually happens

The UBCK examination pattern for a securities firm is well-documented in peer conversations (per ciso-playbook RESOURCES.md's wisdom section), even though no public examiner checklist exists. The pattern:

  1. Document request phase (1–2 weeks before the visit). UBCK sends a list of requested documents: ICT risk management policy, ICT risk assessment (last 12 months), incident response plan, business continuity plan, third-party-risk assessment for critical vendors, evidence of awareness training, evidence of penetration testing, evidence of backup testing. The list is the curriculum.
  2. On-site visit (typically 2–5 days). The examiner walks the program with the CISO. They ask: "show me the metric for X." "How do you know it's accurate?" "What did you do when the metric went red?" "Show me the runbook for this metric." "Who owns it?" "When was the threshold last reviewed?"
  3. Findings phase (1–4 weeks after). The examiner issues findings: minor (corrective action within 30–90 days), major (corrective action within 30 days + escalation), or critical (immediate remediation + potential UBCK enforcement action).

The audit-ready pack pre-empts each phase. The document request is satisfied by the runbook cards from lesson 0002 plus the cross-reference matrix from this lesson. The on-site visit is satisfied by the board-pack trend lines + the per-entity owner roster. The findings phase is satisfied by the Improve-step playbooks from lesson 0002.

The examiner's first question. Almost always: "Show me how you measure your incident-response readiness." The answer is the tabletop-exercise metric (board-pack #8). The follow-up is: "When was the last one, who attended, what did you change?" If the answer is "we ran two last year, the CEO attended, we updated the runbook to reflect the gap we found" — you pass. If the answer is "we ran one two years ago and we're planning the next one" — you have a finding.

The PDPD 72-hour clock — a different category of metric

Most cybersecurity metrics are maturity indicators. "Time-to-contain P95" is better at 2 hours than 4 hours, but neither is "compliant" or "non-compliant" in a regulatory sense. PDPD's 72-hour breach-notification clock is different: it is a regulatory ceiling. P95 must be ≤ 72 hours, full stop. Above 72 hours, the firm is non-compliant, with fines up to 5% of prior-year revenue.

This category demands different measurement discipline:

PropertyMaturity indicator (most metrics)Regulatory ceiling (PDPD 72-hour)
Threshold Yellow / red bands; trends matter Binary — within 72 hours or not
Reporting cadence Weekly / monthly / quarterly Per-incident + monthly aggregate
Owner Program owner (SOC, IR Lead, etc.) Data Protection Officer (DPO) + CISO
Failure consequence Program degradation Regulatory fine + reputational damage
Evidence required Trend line + improvement narrative Per-incident timestamp log + notification proof

The 72-hour clock requires its own card in the runbook — not folded into "time-to-contain." Lesson 0007 (Respond + Recover) builds the PDPD-specific metric pack; lesson 0003 only registers the requirement.

The audit-ready evidence packet — what to assemble before the examiner arrives

The packet has six sections. Each section is pre-assembled and updated quarterly. The examiner's document-request phase is satisfied by handing them the packet.

  1. Program overview — the strategic plan from ciso-playbook 0010 (revised quarterly), the board pack from lesson 0001, the per-metric runbook cards from lesson 0002.
  2. Metric catalogue — the full metric set (not just board-pack 10 — the program-owner metrics too), with the cross-reference matrix from this lesson.
  3. Evidence trail per metric — for each board-pack metric, the 12-month trend, the Improve-step playbook, the threshold-basis documentation. Auto-collected where possible (SIEM exports, IR ticket exports, backup-platform exports).
  4. Runbook for the Improve-step — when a metric breaches, what happened. Include Severity-1 incidents from the past 12 months with metric-trigger documentation.
  5. Personnel and ownership — the layered ownership roster from lesson 0002 (group consolidator + 9 entity owners per metric). Includes entity-CEO monthly-call minutes referencing the metric.
  6. Regulatory correspondence — prior audit findings, prior responses, prior remediation evidence. Plus any proactive correspondence (e.g., "we self-reported this incident under PDPD, here is the notification proof").

The packet is not a fire-drill. It is a standing artefact, updated quarterly as part of the Sustain step from lesson 0002. The morning the examiner arrives, you do not assemble it. You print it.

  • Mỗi ô chứa một clause / control / function / control-number cụ thể — không viết "covered" hay "yes". Examiner sẽ hỏi "covered by what exactly?"
  • Mỗi ô có một evidence request — câu hỏi cụ thể mà examiner sẽ hỏi và câu trả lời auto-collected.
  • Cập nhật quarterly cùng với runbook cards từ lesson 0002. UBCK circular update → refresh matrix ngay.
  • Ô "Gap" (M7/UBCK) — flag cho strategic plan (ciso-playbook 0010) để address trong roadmap.

Self-check

Why is the PDPD 72-hour clock treated as a different category of metric than time-to-contain?

Because the 72-hour clock is a regulatory ceiling, not a maturity indicator. Time-to-contain P95 is better at 2 hours than 4 hours, but neither is "non-compliant." PDPD's 72-hour breach-notification clock is binary: within 72 hours of incident confirmation, MPS must be notified. Above 72 hours, the firm is non-compliant, with fines up to 5% of prior-year revenue. Maturity-indicator metrics get a trend line and an improvement narrative; regulatory-ceiling metrics get per-incident timestamp logs and notification proof. Different evidence, different reporting cadence, different owners (DPO + CISO for the ceiling, program owner for the maturity indicator). Conflating the two categories is the most-common audit-readiness gap.

Why does the audit-ready pack layer frameworks rather than substitute one for another?

Because each framework operates at a different altitude, and the same data satisfies multiple altitudes. UBCK at 50,000 feet asks whether you have an ICT risk programme. ISO 27001 at 30,000 feet asks whether your ISMS covers the controls. NIST CSF at 20,000 feet asks whether your program covers the functions. CIS Top 18 at 5,000 feet asks whether each control is actually implemented. PDPD sits orthogonal — it asks whether your personal-data processing complies with the law. The audit-ready pack shows the same metric at all altitudes simultaneously. One number (e.g., 95% backup coverage) satisfies UBCK + ISO 27001 + NIST CSF + CIS at four altitudes. Substituting frameworks — choosing NIST and ignoring ISO 27001 — forfeits evidence the examiner may want. Layering exploits the public crosswalks; substituting forfeits them.

What is the examiner's first question, and how do you pre-empt it?

The examiner's first question is almost always: "Show me how you measure your incident-response readiness." The answer is the tabletop-exercise metric (board-pack M8). The follow-up is: "When was the last one, who attended, what did you change?" The audit-ready pack pre-empts this by having M8's runbook card ready (lesson 0002), the cross-reference matrix showing M8 satisfies UBCK + ISO 27001 + NIST CSF + CIS (this lesson), and the evidence trail showing the 12-month trend of executive-team participation (auto-collected from the tabletop calendar + attendance log). If the answer is "we ran two last year, the CEO attended, we updated the runbook to reflect the gap we found" — you pass. If the answer is "we ran one two years ago and we're planning the next one" — you have a finding.
