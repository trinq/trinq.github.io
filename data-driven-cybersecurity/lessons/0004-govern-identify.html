Lesson 0004 · Data-driven cybersecurity · Arc 2: Measure every program area

Govern + Identify: Strategic Foundation Metrics

You already have the strategic plan and the BIA. Now measure whether they are doing what they were written to do.

Predecessor lesson (ciso-playbook course). This lesson measures the program areas covered by ciso-playbook 0010 (strategic plan) and ciso-playbook 0006 (continuity & BIA matrix). The Vietnamese artifacts from those lessons are the input — specifically the strategic plan's mission/vision/governance structure (lesson 0010) and the 8-process × 7-column BIA matrix (lesson 0006). This lesson adds the measurement layer to both.

Why this lesson fourth

Arc 1 (lessons 0001–0003) gave you the metric discipline: which 8–12 metrics, the seven-step lifecycle, the cross-framework evidence pack. Arc 2 starts the per-CSF-function work. We begin with Govern and Identify because they sit at the top of the program — if you measure the foundation, everything downstream inherits the discipline.

Mattei (chapter 5) frames Govern as "how authority is exercised and accountability rendered for managing organizational risk" — policies, processes, procedures, organizational structure, defined roles. Identify (chapter 6) is the function that maps what you have (assets), what could go wrong (vulnerabilities, threats), and how you prioritise (risk assessment). Together, Govern + Identify are the strategic foundation. Without them measured, the rest of the program operates on assumptions.

Mariano Mattei, Data-Driven Cybersecurity ch. 5.2 When cybersecurity initiatives are misaligned with board expectations, organizations risk underfunding critical security measures or overinvesting in areas that do not directly mitigate the most pressing threats.

This is the lesson's spine: misalignment is invisible until you measure it. The ciso-playbook course designed the strategic plan (lesson 0010) and the BIA matrix (lesson 0006). Both are design artifacts. This lesson is the measurement layer: are the design artifacts actually doing the work they were written to do?

For your 9-entity Vietnamese securities firm, Govern + Identify lands with three pressures:

  1. Multi-entity governance is the hard problem. One strategic plan can serve nine entities only if governance is consolidated where it should be and specialised where it must be. Mattei (chapter 5.6) introduces governance metrics — the kind that measure "are the right decisions being made by the right people at the right cadence?" Without measurement, multi-entity governance is either uniform-but-misaligned (group policy forced on entities that don't fit) or specialised-but-uncontrolled (each entity doing its own thing). The lesson gives you the metric set that distinguishes healthy multi-entity governance from drift.
  2. Asset inventory accuracy is the unknown unknown. A typical mid-size firm's asset inventory is 30–50% wrong on a good day. Subsidiaries, contractor laptops, cloud workloads, shadow IT — none of it shows up in the inventory. UBCK examiners know this. The asset-management metric (chapter 6.2) is the leading indicator that determines whether every other Identify metric is real.
  3. Risk-assessment cadence is the lagging trap. A firm that does a risk assessment once a year and files it is not managing risk; it's documenting compliance. The risk-assessment metric (chapter 6.3) measures whether risk is being re-assessed at the cadence the threat environment demands, not just at the cadence the calendar demands.

By the end of this lesson you will have produced the Govern + Identify metric catalogue (in Vietnamese) — 8–10 metrics, each cross-referenced to the ciso-playbook strategic-plan artifact and the BIA matrix it measures. This catalogue is what you present when the board asks "is the strategic plan working?" and when the UBCK examiner asks "show me your risk-management cadence."

Govern metrics — the strategic-foundation layer

Govern metrics answer one question: is the program being run by the right people, with the right authority, at the right cadence, against the right objectives? Mattei (chapter 5.6) frames oversight as the spine of governance. The metric set falls into four categories.

Cadence & oversight Cadence adherence: strategic-plan review (quarterly), audit-committee briefing (quarterly), entity-CEO cyber call (monthly) Whether the governance cadence is actually held (not just calendared) Strategic plan — cadence section

Of these 8 metrics, only 2–3 belong on the board pack. The rest are program-owner metrics. Specifically, the board pack gets: cadence adherence (you either had the audit-committee briefing or you didn't), % strategic-plan initiatives funded vs planned (the plan is being delivered or it isn't), and % policy exceptions consolidated (the multi-entity governance is working or drifting).

The "is the plan working" metric. The single most important Govern metric is the one the ciso-playbook course did not name explicitly: strategic-plan initiative delivery rate. Of the initiatives committed in the strategic plan (ciso-playbook 0010), how many were funded, started, and delivered on schedule in the past 12 months? This metric is the answer to the board's question "is the cybersecurity plan working?" It is also the metric that forces honest governance — if delivery rate is < 60%, the strategic plan is aspirational, not operational, and the board should know.

Identify metrics — the asset-and-risk layer

Identify metrics answer the question: do we know what we have, what could go wrong, and what we are doing about it? Mattei (chapter 6) frames this as the function that produces the inputs every other function consumes. The metric set falls into three categories.

Continuous improvement (ch. 6.4) % of strategic-plan objectives with measured progress (vs aspirational only) Whether the strategic plan is being measured at all — the structural check that prevents the plan from drifting into a binder on a shelf Strategic plan — objectives tracker

Of these 8 metrics, 2 belong on the board pack: % in-scope assets in authoritative inventory (asset-management discipline) and risk-assessment refresh cadence adherence (risk-management discipline). The rest are program-owner and entity-level metrics.

The strategic-plan-vs-reality metric — the one UBCK will ask first

Of all Govern + Identify metrics, the single most audit-defensible one is the strategic-plan-vs-reality metric. It has four components, each a separate metric that feeds the same audit defence:

  1. Plan exists. Documented strategic plan, last reviewed within 12 months, approved by the board / CEO. (Leading: yes/no.)
  2. Plan is funded. % of strategic-plan initiatives with allocated budget for the current fiscal year. (Leading indicator of execution capacity.)
  3. Plan is in motion. % of initiatives with documented progress against milestones (not just "in progress" but actual milestone dates hit/missed). (Leading indicator of execution discipline.)
  4. Plan is delivered. % of initiatives completed within the planned quarter, and aggregate delivery rate over the past 12 months. (Lagging indicator of execution capability.)

The four metrics tell the same story at four altitudes. UBCK examiner asks "is your strategic plan being executed?" The answer is the four-metric dashboard. If all four are green, the plan is alive. If any one is yellow or red, the plan has a problem at that altitude.

The audit-defence pattern. A UBCK examination of the strategic plan typically asks three questions, in this order: (1) "Show me your strategic plan." (Documented artifact from ciso-playbook 0010.) (2) "Show me progress against the plan." (The four-metric dashboard above.) (3) "When a milestone slipped, what did you do?" (The Improve-step playbook from lesson 0002.) If all three answers are clean, the audit passes. The fourth question — "what did you change in the plan as a result?" — is the follow-up that distinguishes a mature program from a compliant one.

The asset-inventory accuracy problem — the unknown unknown

Mattei (chapter 6.2) treats asset inventory as the foundation of Identify. The book's framing is straightforward: you cannot protect what you do not know you have. The reality at most firms is harder: the inventory is incomplete in ways that are systematically biased toward missing the assets that are most at risk.

Three patterns dominate the inventory-accuracy problem at mid-size firms:

PatternWhat's missingWhy it's missingHow to measure the gap
Cloud-shadow Cloud workloads spun up by business units without IT involvement Self-service cloud provisioning; no central gatekeeper Cloud-provider billing export vs inventory — the delta is the shadow
Contractor-blind Contractor laptops, contractor-managed systems, vendor-owned devices with access to firm data Contractor onboarding bypasses IT; vendor devices are out of scope by default Active Directory / IAM joiners report vs inventory — the delta is the contractor gap
Entity-silo Assets held at the entity level but not surfaced to group IT 9 entities have different IT teams, different tooling, different reporting cadences Group IT asset count vs sum of entity IT asset counts — the delta is the silo gap

For your firm, the multi-entity pattern is the most acute. A 9-entity portfolio has at least three different IT operating models: entities with their own IT team, entities that outsource to a shared service, and entities that operate on group IT infrastructure. The asset inventory must reconcile across all three. The metric % of in-scope assets in authoritative inventory (with entity attribution) is the integrity check.

Lesson 0006 (Detect) revisits asset inventory in the context of detection coverage — you cannot detect threats to assets you don't know you have.

The risk-assessment cadence question — annual vs continuous

Mattei (chapter 6.3) frames risk assessment as a recurring process, not an annual event. NIST SP 800-30 (which Mattei cites as the formal reference) supports both cadences — a comprehensive annual assessment and continuous updates as the threat environment changes. The metric is which one your firm actually does.

CadenceWhat it looks likeWhat the metric saysWhat UBCK sees
Annual-only Big assessment in Q1, filed, not revisited until next Q1 Risk-assessment refresh cadence adherence: 100% on the annual cycle, 0% on the continuous cycle Risk assessment is a compliance artifact, not a management tool
Triggered Annual comprehensive + ad-hoc reassessments on material changes (new product, new vendor, new regulation) Risk-assessment refresh cadence adherence: 100% annual + N triggered per year (documented) Risk assessment is operational but uneven — the trigger list must be documented
Continuous Risks are tracked continuously; quarterly review of every risk register; monthly review of top-10 risks Risk-assessment refresh cadence adherence: 100% annual + 100% quarterly + continuous monitoring Risk assessment is a management discipline — the gold standard

For your firm, the realistic target is triggered in year one, continuous by year two. The metric risk-assessment refresh cadence adherence tells the story regardless of which cadence you adopt.

Self-check

Why is asset inventory accuracy treated as the foundation of Identify?

Because every other Identify metric is built on the inventory. If the inventory is 70% accurate, then "100% of critical assets are protected" is a meaningless claim — 30% of the critical assets are unknown and therefore unprotected by definition. The asset-inventory-accuracy metric is the integrity check for the entire Identify function. Mattei (chapter 6.2) is direct on this: you cannot protect what you do not know you have. The hard part is not building the inventory; it is keeping it accurate as cloud workloads spin up, contractor laptops join the network, and entity-level IT teams operate outside the group perimeter.

Why does annual-only risk-assessment cadence fail the audit?

Because the threat environment does not wait for the calendar. A firm that assesses risk once a year in Q1 and files the document is documenting compliance, not managing risk. The metric risk-assessment refresh cadence adherence exposes this: if the firm assesses annually but never triggered a re-assessment when a new vendor came online, a new product launched, or a new regulation came into force, the cadence adherence is 100% on the annual cycle and 0% on the triggered cycle. The audit-defensible pattern is "annual comprehensive + documented trigger list + triggered re-assessments on material change" (or continuous, in mature programs). The trigger list must be documented — "we re-assess when something changes" is not enough; "we re-assess when any of these 8 triggers fire" is.

What is the single most audit-defensible Govern metric, and why?

Strategic-plan initiative delivery rate: of the initiatives committed in the strategic plan (ciso-playbook 0010), how many were funded, started, and delivered on schedule in the past 12 months. This metric is the answer to the board's question "is the cybersecurity plan working?" It is also the metric that forces honest governance — if delivery rate is < 60%, the strategic plan is aspirational, not operational. The four-component version (plan exists / funded / in motion / delivered) tells the same story at four altitudes. UBCK examiners ask this in three questions: "show me your plan," "show me progress," "when a milestone slipped, what did you do?" If all three answers are clean, the audit passes.
