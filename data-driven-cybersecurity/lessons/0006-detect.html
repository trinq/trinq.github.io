Detect: FAR/FRR/EER + ATLAS Coverage

Your SIEM generates 12,000 alerts per day. Analysts triage 200. The other 11,800 either fire too often or never should have fired. That gap — between what the system says and what is real — is what Detect metrics measure.

The Vietnamese SOC playbook artifact (ciso-playbook 0004) is the input; this lesson adds the measurement layer: are the alerts accurate, are the thresholds tuned, is the SOC catching what matters?

Why this lesson sixth

Lessons 0004 and 0005 measured the inputs to detection: governance, asset inventory, IAM, awareness, data security, platform. Lesson 0006 measures the detection function itself — the SOC's alert quality, threshold tuning, and coverage of the threats that matter. Per Mattei (chapter 8), the detection function has three measurable surfaces: the sensor coverage (what we can see), the alert quality (FAR/FRR/EER), and the analyst throughput (TTD/TTR/event volume).

Mariano Mattei, Data-Driven Cybersecurity ch. 8.4 ATLAS is designed to combat the problem of alert fatigue, where analysts are overwhelmed by the volume of alerts, leading to desensitization and potential oversight of critical alerts.

For your 9-entity Vietnamese securities firm, Detect lands with three pressures the ciso-playbook course did not address:

  1. Alert fatigue is the SOC's biggest failure mode. A SOC that generates 12,000 alerts per day and triages 200 has a 98.3% alert-desertion rate. The genuine threats that should have triggered action are buried in the noise. The FAR (false acceptance rate) and FRR (false rejection rate) metrics — borrowed from biometric authentication theory and applied to anomaly detection per Mattei chapter 8.5 — are the way to measure and fix this.
  2. ATLAS is the methodology, not a product. Mattei (chapter 8.4) introduces ATLAS (Alert Threshold Lifecycle Assessment System) as a seven-step methodology: Select, Define, Categorize, Collect, Analyze, Implement, Measure. The output is not a tool — it is a disciplined approach to threshold tuning. Most SOCs have a SIEM; few have a documented ATLAS process. The audit-defensible answer is the ATLAS spreadsheet, maintained as a living document.
  3. Top-10 SOC use cases are the coverage spine. Mattei (chapter 8.3) lists the canonical SOC use cases: ransomware detection, C2 beaconing, crypto-mining, data exfiltration, external IP communication, malware C2, signature-based threats, brute-force, DDoS. A SOC that has tuned thresholds for all 10 has coverage; a SOC that has tuned thresholds for 3 has gaps. The coverage metric is the audit-defensible answer to "do you detect what matters?"

By the end of this lesson you will have produced the Detect metric catalogue (in Vietnamese) — the FAR/FRR/EER detection-quality dashboard, the ATLAS-tuned top-10 SOC use cases with documented thresholds, and the TTD/TTR event-throughput metrics. The three measurable surfaces of Detect

Mattei (chapter 8) organises the detection function into three measurable surfaces:

SurfaceWhat it measuresKey metricsCadence
Sensor coverage Whether the SOC can see the threats — is the data being collected? % asset coverage by EDR/NDR/Cloud/SIEM sensors; % log source online Continuous (real-time dashboard)
Alert quality (FAR/FRR/EER) Whether the alerts the SOC sees are accurate — are the thresholds tuned? FAR, FRR, EER per top-10 use case; alert volume; true-positive rate Daily / weekly review; ATLAS update quarterly
Analyst throughput (TTD/TTR) Whether the SOC can respond — is the team keeping pace? TTD P50/P95; TTR P50/P95; event volume per analyst per shift Weekly / monthly review

The three surfaces feed each other. Sensor coverage without alert quality is high-volume noise. Alert quality without sensor coverage is a tuned SIEM watching nothing. Analyst throughput without either is burnout.

Sensor coverage metrics — can we see?

Sensor coverage is the most basic question: is data flowing from the assets that matter? Mattei's chapter 8.1.2 frames the architecture: sensors on endpoints, servers, network devices, cloud workloads, applications. The metric set has four categories.

MetricWhat it measuresOwnerDefensible threshold
D1 — % in-scope assets with EDR coverage (covered in lesson 0005 as P17) Whether every endpoint has functioning endpoint detection SOC Manager Xanh ≥ 98%; Đỏ < 95%
D2 — % in-scope network segments with NDR coverage Whether network sensors are placed at the segments that matter (DMZ, internal east-west, egress) Network/Security Engineering Lead Xanh 100% critical segments; Vàng ≥ 90% all
D3 — % cloud workloads with detection coverage (CWPP / CSPM) Whether cloud workloads have parity with on-prem detection — the cloud-shadow trap from lesson 0004 Cloud Security Lead Xanh ≥ 95%; Đỏ < 85%
D4 — % critical log sources online in SIEM Whether the SIEM is receiving the logs it expects — the data-pipeline integrity check SOC Manager + Platform Lead Xanh 100% critical; Vàng ≥ 95% all; Đỏ < 90%
The asset-coverage-denominator trap. D1 (EDR coverage) is calculated as endpoints with EDR / total endpoints in inventory. The denominator is lesson 0004's I1 (in-scope assets in authoritative inventory). If the inventory is 70% accurate, D1 is calculated over the wrong denominator — the gap of 30% may have EDR or not; the metric doesn't know. The lesson 0004/0006 dependency is real: fix the inventory first, then fix the coverage metric. Same pattern for D2 (network), D3 (cloud), D4 (log sources).

Alert quality metrics — the FAR/FRR/EER framework

Mattei (chapter 8.5) borrows three metrics from biometric authentication theory and applies them to anomaly detection. The framework is direct: every detection rule has two error types, and the trade-off between them is the core detection-engineering decision.

MetricWhat it measuresWhy it mattersDefensible threshold
FAR (False Acceptance Rate) % of malicious activity that the system fails to flag — the detection miss A high FAR means real threats pass through undetected. The most dangerous failure mode. Xanh ≤ 1%; Vàng 1–5%; Đỏ > 5%
FRR (False Rejection Rate) % of benign activity that the system incorrectly flags — the false alarm A high FRR means alert fatigue. Analysts tune out, and the FAR worsens as a side-effect. Xanh ≤ 5%; Vàng 5–20%; Đỏ > 20%
EER (Equal Error Rate) The point at which FAR = FRR — the balanced operating point The lower the EER, the better the threshold is tuned for the trade-off. Xanh ≤ 2%; Vàng 2–5%; Đỏ > 5%
True-Positive Rate (TPR) % of malicious activity correctly flagged — 1 − FAR The complement to FAR. A more intuitive metric for non-security audiences. Xanh ≥ 99%; Đỏ < 95%
Mariano Mattei, Data-Driven Cybersecurity ch. 8.5 The EER is a critical metric representing the point at which the FRR and FAR are equal. It serves as a balanced measure of the system's overall accuracy. The lower the EER, the more reliable the system is at distinguishing between legitimate and illegitimate activities.

The FAR/FRR trade-off is the central detection-engineering decision. Tightening thresholds lowers FRR (fewer false alarms) but raises FAR (more real threats missed). Loosening thresholds lowers FAR but raises FRR. The EER is the operating point where both error rates are equal — the most-balanced tuning. In practice, SOCs tune slightly above the EER (in the direction of higher TPR), accepting more false alarms to catch more real threats. The exact operating point is a risk-appetite decision documented in the strategic plan.

The ATLAS methodology — seven steps to tuned thresholds

Mattei (chapter 8.4) introduces ATLAS (Alert Threshold Lifecycle Assessment System) as a seven-step methodology for threshold tuning. The output is not a product — it is a disciplined process, with the ATLAS spreadsheet as the living document. For each top-10 use case, ATLAS produces a documented threshold that is reviewed quarterly.

StepWhat it doesOutput
1. SelectIdentify the alert to be added; use a meaningful name (not PROD1AL2)Alert name + use case ID
2. DefineDocument what you are monitoring, why, and how it contributes to the programAlert description (the "why")
3. CategorizeClassify as Zero-tolerance (notify immediately), Policy violation (threshold-based), or Anomaly detection (statistical)Category + initial threshold direction
4. CollectGather the historical log data needed to establish a baseline (typically 6–12 months)Baseline dataset
5. AnalyzeApply statistical analysis: weekly percentiles, mean, standard deviation, slide positivity rate, slope of weekly data on a log scaleRecommended threshold (typically Mean + 2×SD)
6. ImplementDeploy the threshold in the SIEM with documented false-positive trackingProduction threshold + FP-tracking mechanism
7. MeasureContinuously document false positives and adjust; review quarterlyUpdated threshold + ATLAS spreadsheet row updated

The audit-defensible answer is: "Show me your ATLAS spreadsheet for the top-10 SOC use cases." If the spreadsheet exists, is current, and shows the threshold history, you pass. If it doesn't exist, you have a finding: thresholds are tuned ad-hoc, not systematically.

Analyst throughput metrics — the TTD/TTR pair

Mattei (chapter 8.6) frames analyst throughput through three metrics: time-to-detect (TTD), time-to-respond (TTR), and event volume. The first two are the lagging measures of detection quality; the third is the lagging measure of capacity.

MetricWhat it measuresOwnerDefensible threshold
D5 — TTD P50 / P95 (time-to-detect, from event occurrence to alert generation) How quickly the system identifies a real adverse event — the detection latency SOC Manager Xanh P50 ≤ 1h, P95 ≤ 4h; Đỏ P95 > 24h
D6 — TTR P50 / P95 (time-to-respond, from alert to first response action) How quickly the analyst responds to an alert — the response latency SOC Manager Xanh P50 ≤ 30 min, P95 ≤ 4h; Đỏ P95 > 24h
D7 — Daily event volume per analyst per shift Whether the workload is sustainable — the burnout indicator SOC Manager + HR Xanh ≤ 50 events/shift; Đỏ > 100 events/shift
D8 — Incident classification accuracy % of events correctly categorised by severity — the analyst judgement quality SOC Manager Xanh ≥ 90%; Đỏ < 75%
D9 — Root-cause resolution rate % of identified root causes that have been addressed — the closing-the-loop indicator SOC Manager + IR Lead Xanh ≥ 80%; Đỏ < 60%

The alert-fatigue cascade — why FRR matters more than it looks

Alert fatigue is a cascade, not a single failure. The pattern at most SOCs:

  1. High FRR — thresholds are too loose, generating many false alarms.
  2. Analyst desensitisation — after weeks of false alarms, analysts start dismissing alerts by default.
  3. FAR creeps up — because the real threats are buried in the noise, genuine attacks slip through unnoticed.
  4. True-positive detection drops — TPR falls, even though the SIEM is still firing alerts.
  5. Incident response degrades — TTD/TTR worsen because the analysts are now triaging by gut feel, not by alert priority.

Breaking the cascade requires intervention at step 1 (lower the FRR with ATLAS) or at step 5 (improve triage discipline through playbooks + automation). The FAR/FRR/EER metric set is the only way to detect the cascade early.

The SecureData case study. Mattei (chapter 8.6.2) describes SecureData Inc.'s before-and-after: TTD from 6 hours to 1 hour, TTR from 4 hours to 30 minutes, FPR from 60% to 20%, classification accuracy from 70% to 90%, root-cause resolution from 50% to 80%. All five metrics moved together because they were treated as a system, not as individual KPIs. The lesson: detection-quality improvements are multiplicative; addressing one metric without addressing the others produces marginal gains.

Self-check

Why is FAR treated as the more dangerous failure mode than FRR, even though FRR is more visible?

Because FAR measures the threats that pass through undetected — the attacks you don't know about. FRR measures the false alarms — visible, noisy, but ultimately benign. The danger of FAR is that a high FAR looks the same as a working system: the SIEM is firing, alerts are being generated, dashboards are populated. The only way to detect a high FAR is to measure it explicitly — through red-team testing, breach-and-attack simulation (BAS), or retrospective analysis of confirmed incidents that should have triggered alerts. Mattei's SecureData case study shows the inverse: a low FRR with a high FAR is the worst combination, because the system appears quiet and effective while missing real threats. The operating-point decision (above or below EER) is a risk-appetite choice; most SOCs operate slightly above EER, accepting higher FRR to drive FAR toward zero.

Why is the ATLAS spreadsheet the audit-defensible answer, not a SIEM rule configuration?

Because the audit question is not "what thresholds are configured in the SIEM?" — that answer is one SELECT query away and tells the examiner nothing about whether the thresholds are actually tuned. The audit question is "show me your process for setting, reviewing, and updating thresholds." The ATLAS spreadsheet answers this: it documents the Select, Define, Categorize, Collect, Analyze, Implement, Measure steps for each use case; it shows the threshold history (when was it last reviewed, what was changed, why); it shows the false-positive rate over time and the tuning iterations. If the spreadsheet exists, is current, and shows evidence of quarterly review, the audit passes. If the thresholds live only in the SIEM and the rationale lives only in someone's head, the audit has a finding: thresholds are tuned ad-hoc, not systematically. ATLAS is a methodology, not a product — its value is the discipline it imposes, evidenced by the spreadsheet.

Why does PDPD scope matter for the data-exfiltration use case?

Because the data-exfiltration threshold (use case 4) is built on a baseline of "normal" bytes-out per hour per server. If the baseline is built only on non-PDPD data, the most-sensitive assets (customer PII, financial records, employee data) are not in the baseline. Anomalous exfiltration from those assets may not trigger the threshold — because the threshold is calibrated against the wrong population. The fix: scope the baseline to include PDPD-scope assets, then compute the anomaly threshold separately for PDPD and non-PDPD segments. The same principle applies to use case 10 (insider threat / data leakage): the user-behaviour-analytics baseline must be built on the user's normal access patterns against PDPD-scope data, not against generic access patterns.
