Respond + Recover: IR Metrics and the PDPD 72-Hour Clock

Every minute an incident runs past the PDPD notification window is a regulatory finding waiting to happen. The IR metrics exist to make sure that clock never moves.

Predecessor lessons (ciso-playbook course). This lesson measures the IR + Recovery layer designed by ciso-playbook 0004 (SOC playbook) and the continuity layer designed by ciso-playbook 0006 (continuity, backups, RTO/RPO). The Vietnamese IRP + BIA matrix + continuity artifacts from those lessons are the input. This lesson adds the measurement layer: when an incident happens, are the metrics in place to prove the response was fast, effective, and compliant?

Why this lesson seventh

Lessons 0004–0006 measured the strategic foundation, the controls, and the detection layer. Lesson 0007 measures the function that runs when those layers fail — Respond and Recover. Per Mattei (chapter 9), the IR framework has 10 steps: planning, testing, detection, triage, notification, containment, evidence, eradication, recovery, post-incident. Each step has metrics; together they tell the story of whether the firm can recover from an incident without regulatory exposure.

Mariano Mattei, Data-Driven Cybersecurity ch. 9.1 Incident management is a systematic approach to detecting, responding to, and recovering from cybersecurity incidents. It is designed with the primary objective of minimizing the effects of incidents on the organization.

For your 9-entity Vietnamese securities firm, Respond + Recover lands with three pressures:

  1. The PDPD 72-hour notification clock is a regulatory ceiling, not a target. Vietnam's Personal Data Protection Act (PDPD, Decree 13/2023) requires notification to the Personal Data Protection Commission and affected data subjects within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal-data breach. UBCK adds its own notification requirements for securities firms. The 72-hour clock starts at "awareness," which means time-to-detect (lesson 0006's D5) eats directly into the notification window. A firm that takes 48 hours to detect a breach has 24 hours left to notify. The IR metric set must be designed to give the notification team a fighting chance.
  2. IR is the most-tested CSF function by UBCK. Mattei (chapter 9.3) frames tabletop exercises as the discipline for testing the IRP. UBCK examiners ask for evidence of the last 12 months of tabletop exercises, the action plans from each, and the improvements tracked back into the IRP. The IRP-test metric is the audit-defensible answer.
  3. Recovery is the function that gets tested only in a crisis. Mattei (chapter 9.10) is direct: "Communication is key during the recovery phase." But the actual recovery is the function that gets tested only in a crisis — when it's too late to discover that the backup doesn't restore, the RTO is wrong, or the BIA-criticality list missed a system. The recovery metric set is the discipline that prevents the crisis-day discovery.

By the end of this lesson you will have produced the Respond + Recover metric catalogue (in Vietnamese) — the MTTC-I / MTTR-I / IRSR-I containment-and-remediation dashboard, the PDPD 72-hour notification metric, the IR communication metrics (ICT/SSR/CRR), and the recovery validation metrics (RTO/RPO compliance). This is what the IR Lead reviews per-incident, what the CISO presents quarterly, and what UBCK examines under the "incident response and continuity" theme.

The PDPD 72-hour clock — the regulatory ceiling

Vietnam's Decree 13/2023 (PDPD) Article 12.1 establishes a 72-hour notification window: when a personal-data breach is detected, the Personal Data Protection Commission and affected data subjects must be notified within 72 hours. The 72-hour clock is a regulatory ceiling — the absolute maximum, not a target. A firm that uses the full 72 hours has no buffer for the notification to fail, the scope to expand, or the regulator to ask follow-up questions.

The metric design must account for three distinct phases within the 72-hour window:

PhaseDurationWhat happensOwner
Detection → confirmation Variable (typically 1–48h) Confirm the breach is real, scope the affected personal-data records, classify under PDPD Article 2 SOC + DPO + Legal
Confirmation → notification Variable (typically 1–24h) Draft notification to PDPC, draft data-subject notification, coordinate with PR/Comms DPO + Legal + Comms
Notification → regulator response Ongoing (no fixed window) Handle PDPC follow-up, supplement information as scope expands DPO + Legal
The clock starts at "awareness," not "detection." PDPD Article 12.1 uses "phát hiện" (becomes aware), which the PDPC interprets as the moment the firm has sufficient information to confirm a breach has occurred — not the moment the SIEM fires. A firm that detects an anomaly but takes 48 hours to confirm it's a breach has already lost 48 hours of the 72-hour window. The lesson's metric set distinguishes between time-to-detect (TTD, lesson 0006 D5) and time-to-confirm (a new IR-specific metric), and uses both to track the total clock.

Containment + remediation metrics — the IR-quality pair

Mattei (chapter 9.9.1) introduces the three core IR metrics: MTTC-I (mean time to contain), MTTR-I (mean time to remediate), IRSR-I (incident response success rate). These are the lagging measures of IR effectiveness.

MetricWhat it measuresOwnerDefensible threshold
R4 — MTTC-I P50 / P95 (mean time to contain) How quickly containment is achieved once an incident is confirmed — the speed metric IR Lead + SOC Manager Xanh P50 ≤ 4h, P95 ≤ 24h; Đỏ P95 > 72h
R5 — MTTR-I P50 / P95 (mean time to remediate) How quickly the incident is fully resolved — the closure metric IR Lead + SOC Manager Xanh P50 ≤ 24h, P95 ≤ 7 ngày; Đỏ P95 > 30 ngày
R6 — IRSR-I (incident response success rate) % of incidents successfully contained without recurrence within 30 days — the effectiveness metric IR Lead Xanh ≥ 90%; Vàng 80–90%; Đỏ < 80%

Containment time was reduced to an average of 3 hours; recovery time to 6 hours; incident response success rate increased to 85%.

The CyberSecure before/after (MTTC-I from 8h to 3h; MTTR-I from 16h to 6h; IRSR-I from 60% to 85%) shows the realistic target trajectory. The lesson's thresholds (P95 ≤ 24h for MTTC-I; P95 ≤ 7 days for MTTR-I; ≥ 90% for IRSR-I) are calibrated against this benchmark, with the strategic-plan risk-appetite overlay applied per firm.

Reporting & communication metrics — the stakeholder-trust pair

Mattei (chapter 9.11.1) introduces three communication metrics: ICT (incident communication time), SSR (stakeholder satisfaction rate), CRR (compliance reporting rate). These measure the quality of communication during and after an incident.

MetricWhat it measuresOwnerDefensible threshold

Recovery metrics — the validate-by-testing discipline

Mattei (chapter 7.3.5 and 9.10) frames recovery through two validating disciplines: meeting the committed RTO and RPO, and proving the recovery actually works through periodic testing. The metrics cluster around three questions:

MetricWhat it measuresOwnerDefensible threshold
R10 — RTO compliance rate (covered in lesson 0005 as P14) % of recovery tests that meet the committed RTO — the contractual question Infrastructure Lead + BIA owner Xanh ≥ 95%; Đỏ < 85%
R11 — RPO compliance rate (data-loss tolerance) % of recovery tests that meet the committed RPO — the data-integrity question Infrastructure Lead + BIA owner Xanh 100%; Đỏ < 95%
R12 — Recovery test cadence (covered in lesson 0005 as P13) Whether critical assets are tested at the committed cadence (monthly/quarterly/annual per BIA tier) Infrastructure Lead Xanh 100% theo BIA cadence; Đỏ < 80%
R13 — Recovery test success rate (no data integrity issues found) % of recovery tests that fully restore data and services without integrity issues Infrastructure Lead Xanh ≥ 95%; Đỏ < 85%
Why recovery is tested, not assumed. Mattei (chapter 9.10) is direct: "Post-recovery validation and documentation is the final step in the recovery phase." The recovery test is not optional — it is the validation that the recovery plan actually works. The audit-defensible answer is: "Show me the last 12 months of recovery tests for the Tier 1 assets, with the RTO achieved vs committed." If the tests don't exist or the RTO was missed, the firm has a finding: recovery is documented, not validated.

The Respond + Recover board-pack subset — one or two metrics

Of the 15–16 Respond + Recover metrics, one or two belong on the board pack (joining the metrics from lessons 0004–0006):

  • R1 — Total time from awareness → PDPD notification (the regulatory-ceiling metric, the most-defensible IR metric at the UBCK + PDPC table)
  • OR R4 / R5 / R6 — MTTC-I, MTTR-I, IRSR-I (the IR-effectiveness trio, presented together as a single line)

The rest live at the IR Lead's per-incident review and weekly dashboard. The board sees the trend: is IR getting faster? is the success rate climbing? are we staying inside the 72-hour window?

Where Respond + Recover metrics connect to the rest of the program

Respond + Recover metrics are the lagging indicators that validate everything upstream. The R4 (MTTC-I) metric is bounded by D5/D6 (lesson 0006) and by the IAM metrics from lesson 0005 — if de-provisioning is slow, the access-revocation containment step is slow. The R1 (PDPD 72h) metric is bounded by R2 (time-to-confirm) and by the PDPD scope-classification accuracy (lesson 0004/0005).

Self-check

Why is the 72-hour PDPD clock treated as a regulatory ceiling, not a target?

Because it is the maximum, set by Decree 13/2023 Article 12.1, beyond which the firm is in regulatory violation. A firm that uses the full 72 hours has no buffer for the notification to fail, the scope to expand, or the regulator to ask follow-up questions. The clock also starts at "awareness" — the moment the firm has sufficient information to confirm a breach — not at SIEM detection. A firm that detects an anomaly but takes 48 hours to confirm has already lost 48 hours of the window. The metric design must give the notification team a fighting chance: target ≤ 24h for the total clock (a 48h buffer); critical threshold > 72h (regulatory violation). The defensible answer is documented in the metric card with the regulator minimum (Decree 13/2023), the industry benchmark, and the firm risk appetite.

Why is the IRSR-I metric defined as 30-day no-recurrence?

Because incident response success is not "we contained it" — it is "we contained it and it did not come back." A containment that lasts a day and then the threat resurfaces is not a containment; it is a pause. The 30-day no-recurrence window is the audit-defensible test: if the same incident recurs within 30 days, the original response failed to address the root cause. This is consistent with Mattei's chapter 9.9.1 framing: "incidents that were successfully mitigated and did not result in further security breaches." The IRSR-I threshold of ≥ 90% is calibrated against the CyberSecure before/after (60% → 85%); the 90% target is realistic but not easy. The metric must distinguish between recurrence (same threat actor, same vector, same scope) and new incidents (different vector) — only the former counts against IRSR-I.

Why is recovery tested, not assumed?

Because the recovery plan is a hypothesis until it is validated. A backup that has never been restored is not a backup — it is a copy. An RTO of 4 hours that has never been timed is a number on a page, not a commitment. Mattei (chapter 9.10) is direct: "Post-recovery validation and documentation is the final step in the recovery phase." The audit-defensible answer is: show me the last 12 months of recovery tests for the Tier 1 assets, with the RTO achieved vs committed, the RPO achieved vs committed, and the data-integrity check passed. If the tests don't exist or the RTO was missed, the firm has a finding: recovery is documented, not validated. The cadence (monthly/quarterly/annual per BIA tier) is the discipline that prevents the crisis-day discovery.
