Lesson 0001 · Data-driven cybersecurity · Arc 1: The metric discipline

Stop Counting Vulnerabilities. Start Measuring Program Health.

Joseph Steinberg spent three decades watching boards and CISOs use metrics that quietly destroyed them. This lesson is about not being one of them.

Why this lesson first

You just finished eleven lessons designing a CISO program — governance, talent, awareness, monitoring, IR, continuity, recovery, forensics, the strategic plan, the handover. That program produced eleven Vietnamese artifacts that sit on your desk, your CEO's desk, and your board's deck right now.

Every one of those artifacts generates data. Some of it you already measure. Most of what you measure is, by Mattei's foreword-writer's argument, dangerously misleading. You do not know which.

This first lesson is the lesson before any new metric is defined, any new dashboard is built, any new KPI is briefed to the board. It is the deconstruction phase. Until you can look at every metric currently in production at your firm and classify it as useful, lagging-only, leading-only, vanity, or misleading — and act on the classification — every metric you add in lessons 0002–0010 will simply join the pile of metrics that look like measurement but aren't.

Joseph Steinberg, Foreword to Data-Driven Cybersecurity When metrics show that an organization is consistently experiencing a reduced number of breaches with diminishing financial effects on a quarter-over-quarter basis, does that translate to a notable and commendable improvement? Or does it indicate that the organization's cybersecurity management has deteriorated relative to emerging threats and that the organization is increasingly failing to both recognize when it has been breached and properly account for the business effects accumulating beneath the surface?

Steinberg has watched this exact failure mode — the "we're getting better because the dashboards are green" failure — repeatedly over thirty years. The question is not "do we have dashboards?" It is "what would have to be true for our dashboards to mislead us, and how would we know?"

By the end of this lesson you will have produced the single board-facing metric page (in Vietnamese) for your 9-entity securities firm — 8–12 metrics, each with an owner, a source, a cadence, a threshold, and a decision it drives. It is the artifact the auditor, the board, and your successor will look at. Everything else is downstream of it.

What you probably measure today — and why it lies

Most security programs at mid-size financial-services firms measure some subset of these. Mark each one you currently produce:

Metric you probably produceWhat it appears to sayWhy it can mislead
Number of vulnerabilities open "We have fewer vulnerabilities than last quarter." Often a coverage artefact: you scanned fewer systems, or you stopped tracking the long tail. Mattei's recurring point: "fewer vulnerabilities" can mean "we are looking less hard," not "we are more secure."
Number of incidents detected "Our detection is improving." Inverse problem. If you detect more, is that better detection or worse threat environment? If you detect fewer, are you safer or blind? Incidents detected is meaningless without incidents expected.
Number of phishing emails blocked "Our email security is working." Vanity metric. The phish that got through is the only one that matters; you cannot measure the got-through from the block-count.
Mean time to detect (MTTD) "We detect faster than last quarter." MTTD can improve because your detection logic is faster, or because your detection logic now only catches the easy stuff. Pair with mean time to contain (MTTC) and mean time to recover (MTTR) — never alone.
Patch compliance % "95% of systems patched within SLA." Coverage question: 95% of what? The 5% unpatched is where the breach lives. Steinberg's warning made concrete: a 95% patch rate is irrelevant if the 5% contains your most-exposed internet-facing assets.
Phishing simulation click-rate "Click rate dropped from 18% to 12%." Gaming risk. Users learn to recognise the simulation template and ignore it; behaviour on real phishing may not have changed. Pair with a real-phish reporting metric (leading) and post-training behaviour observation.
Number of alerts closed "SOC is processing alerts." Volume metric. Closes 10,000 alerts a week tells you nothing about whether any of them mattered. Pair with detection-quality metrics (chapter 8.5 — FAR, FRR, EER).

The pattern: every metric above is a lagging indicator that, in isolation, can be gamed, can decay silently, or can improve for the wrong reason. Mattei calls these traditional metrics (chapter 1.3) — simple, tangible, but reactive. They measure what already happened. They cannot tell the board whether the program is getting better.

Steinberg's three failure modes — and the Vietnamese-context overlay

Steinberg's foreword identifies three structural failures that produce misleading metrics. Each one lands differently in a Vietnamese securities firm.

1. Operators gaming their own numbers

Steinberg's first failure mode is the team that optimises the metric rather than the underlying risk. Awareness click-rate is the canonical example (above). The Vietnamese-context variant: awareness training completion rate at a securities firm that UBCK expects to be 100%. The completion rate will hit 100%. The behaviour change will not. The metric that satisfies the regulator is the metric that least reflects reality.

The UBCK-induced trap. When a regulator sets a numeric expectation (e.g., 100% awareness training completion), the metric stops measuring security and starts measuring compliance theatre. The fix is to add a leading metric that the regulator did not ask for: behaviour-change rate, phishing-report-rate, suspicious-activity escalation count. Keep the lagging metric for the regulator; lead with the leading metric for the board.

2. Boards obsessing over detailed figures that should be irrelevant

Steinberg's second failure mode is the board that descends into operational detail — arguing about whether the alert count went up by 3% or 5% — while ignoring the strategic question of whether the program is fit for purpose. The Vietnamese-context variant: an audit committee that requests the same three metrics every quarter (patch compliance, awareness completion, incident count) because those are the ones the previous CISO briefed, regardless of whether they map to the firm's actual risk.

The fix is not more metrics. It is fewer, better metrics, framed in business language. The board does not need to see 30 metrics. The board needs to see the 8–12 metrics that map to the firm's business decisions: what products are at risk, what regulators expect, what customers would care about. Detail belongs in the program-owners' dashboards; strategy belongs in the board pack.

3. Communication gap between cyber and business

Steinberg's third failure mode is the metrics that are too technical for the board ("we have 14 unresolved CVSS-9.8+ vulnerabilities") or too abstract for the SOC ("we have 'good' cyber maturity"). The Vietnamese-context variant: the CISO briefs in English or French-cited regulatory acronyms, and the CEO nods politely without understanding what action they just approved.

This is the failure mode that the ciso-playbook course's lesson 0010 (strategic plan) and lesson 0004 (monitoring) tried to address structurally — with the audit-ready dashboard and the reasonable-care doctrine. This lesson inherits both, and adds the measurement discipline that turns "reasonable care" from a doctrine into evidence.

Mattei's framework: leading vs lagging, possible vs direct, derived vs indirect

Mattei (chapter 1.1) gives a categorisation that is more useful than the obvious "leading vs lagging" split. Every metric in your catalog should be classified along all four axes:

AxisQuestionExamples
Quantitative vs Qualitative Is it a number, or a judgement? Q: MTTD, vulnerability count. Qual: training effectiveness, board confidence.
Leading vs Lagging Does it predict the future or describe the past? Leading: phishing reports per 100 users, high-risk-asset coverage. Lagging: incidents detected, breach cost.
Possible vs Direct Is it standard, or custom to your firm? Possible: patch compliance (industry standard). Direct: time-to-provision a brokerage trading account (firm-specific).
Direct vs Derived vs Indirect Is it measured, calculated, or inferred? Direct: failed-login count. Derived: ratio of successful-to-failed logins. Indirect: cyber-incident reduction after awareness training.

The discipline: every board-facing metric must answer all four axes. If you cannot say "this is a leading metric because it predicts X," the metric is lagging-only and belongs on the program-owner dashboard, not the board pack.

The 8–12 board-facing metrics — how to choose

The first job is to choose. The choice is the artifact. Mattei's book is ~80 metric candidates across chapters 5–9. You will produce ~10. The rest will be classified as program-owner metrics (managed by the SOC, the IR lead, the awareness program owner — not by the board).

Three filters apply:

  1. Does the metric drive a business decision? If the only response to "the metric moved" is "interesting," the metric does not belong on the board page. The board needs metrics where the response is action: allocate budget, change a vendor, brief a regulator, approve a hire.
  2. Is the metric leading or lagging? The board pack needs at least 3 leading indicators. Pure-lagging packs are the failure mode Steinberg warns against — the dashboard is always green, until the breach happens.
  3. Does the metric survive a regulator's question? If a UBCK examiner asks "where does this number come from, and how do you know it's accurate," can you answer in one sentence? If not, the metric is not audit-ready. Lesson 0003 builds the audit-ready pack; lesson 0001 just selects the candidates.

Eleven is the right number for a mid-size securities firm; twelve is the upper limit before the board starts ignoring.

The multi-entity test. For a 9-entity firm, every metric above has two layers: group-aggregated (one number, all entities, consolidated) and entity-level (nine numbers, one per entity, with group-aggregated colour-coded against them). Group-level metrics drive group-level decisions (consolidate the backup platform, hire a group CISO). Entity-level metrics drive entity-level decisions (remediate the brokerage's phishing-report rate). The board pack shows the group-aggregated number; the entity CEOs see their own number with peer comparison. This is the structural answer to "how do we measure across 9 entities?"

What to drop — the deconstruction discipline

For every metric currently in production at your firm, classify it. The classification is the artifact:

ClassificationActionExample
Useful, leading Keep on board pack Phishing-report rate (leading behaviour-change metric)
Useful, lagging (paired with a leading) Keep on board pack with a partner metric Time-to-contain paired with tabletop exercise participation
Lagging only, no leading partner Move to program-owner dashboard, not board pack Total vulnerabilities open (no leading partner at the metric level)
Vanity (counts things that don't matter) Drop or replace with the underlying business metric Phishing emails blocked (replace with phish-that-got-through, if detectable)
Misleading (improvement can mean decay) Replace or pair with the inverse metric Patch compliance % (pair with high-risk-asset coverage; report both)
Regulator-required but lagging-only Keep for regulator; lead with a different metric for board Awareness training completion % (required by UBCK; lead with behaviour-change for board)

The discipline: for every metric currently produced, write down its classification. The list of dropped metrics is itself an artifact. When the audit committee asks "why did we stop reporting X?", the answer is on the list.

Self-check

Why is "phishing emails blocked" a vanity metric while "phishing-report rate" is a leading metric?

Phishing emails blocked counts what the system stopped. The breach-relevant quantity is what got through — which is, by definition, unmeasurable from the block count. Phishing-report rate (the number of suspicious emails users actively report to the security team) is a leading indicator of behaviour change: a culture that reports suspicious emails surfaces phishing attempts that the email gateway missed. The metric you cannot measure (phish that got through) becomes visible via the metric you can measure (user reports).

Why does the board need leading metrics if they're harder to interpret than lagging metrics?

Because lagging metrics are always late. By the time a lagging metric tells the board "we had a breach," the breach is six months old and the cost has been incurred. Leading metrics are the board's only signal of whether the program is preventing the next breach. A board pack that is all-lagging is a board pack that learns about cyber risk from the news, not from the CISO. This is exactly Steinberg's second failure mode (boards obsessing over detailed figures that should be irrelevant) inverted: a board pack that is only lagging is a board that is not actually overseeing cyber risk, just acknowledging past damage.

What is the UBCK-induced trap in awareness metrics?

When a regulator sets a numeric expectation (e.g., 100% awareness training completion), the metric stops measuring security and starts measuring compliance theatre. The completion rate will hit 100% because the program is designed to hit 100%, not because behaviour has changed. The fix: keep the lagging completion-rate metric to satisfy the regulator, and add a leading behaviour-change metric (phishing-report rate, suspicious-activity escalation, post-training observation) that the regulator did not ask for. Lead with the leading metric for the board; lead with the lagging metric for the regulator. Same data, two different stories, both honest.
