AI for Metrics: ML Models and Generative Reporting

ML models catch what statistical methods miss. Generative AI drafts what humans would take a week to write. Lesson 0010 teaches the pattern, the guardrails, and the audit-defensible answer for both.

Predecessor lesson. Lesson 0009 taught the statistical spine (correlation, SMA/EMA, Bayesian, Poisson). This lesson adds the two AI layers: (1) unsupervised ML models (DBSCAN, random forest, SVM) for threat detection and behavioural baselining at scale; (2) generative AI for incident-response automation, reporting, and trend summarisation. The ethics chapter (13.10) is the guardrail: human-in-the-loop, data privacy, accuracy validation — the things that make the audit answer defensible rather than embarrassing.

Lessons 0001–0009 built the metric catalogue (lessons 0004–0007), the predictive layer (0008), the statistical spine (0009). Lesson 0010 closes the loop with the AI layer that makes the catalogue scalable. Per Mattei (chapter 12.2), DBSCAN identifies clusters of network behaviour and flags outliers as anomalies — the unsupervised ML pattern that catches what rule-based detection misses. Per Mattei (chapter 13.8), generative AI automates incident-response recommendations and trend analysis — the language-model pattern that scales reporting without scaling headcount.

Mariano Mattei, Data-Driven Cybersecurity ch. 12.2 DBSCAN identifies clusters based on two parameters: Epsilon (eps) — the maximum distance between two points to be considered part of the same cluster; Minimum samples — the minimum number of points required to form a dense cluster. Points that don't belong to any cluster are labeled as noise or anomalies.

For your 9-entity Vietnamese securities firm, the AI layer lands with four pressures the previous lessons did not address:

  1. Rule-based detection has a ceiling. Lesson 0006 ATLAS taught rule-based detection (SIEM correlations) for the top-10 SOC use cases. That works for known patterns — signature, threshold, anomaly. It breaks on novel patterns: an attacker who probes the trading platform at 3am from a residential Vietnamese IP is, by ATLAS rules, within the "unusual login" threshold. Unsupervised ML (DBSCAN clustering of network behaviour, chapter 12.2) catches the pattern: this device's behaviour doesn't cluster with any peer group. The detection surface widens.
  2. Random forest and SVM scale the classification. Mattei (chapter 12.3) introduces random forest for ensemble classification and SVM for separating benign from malicious traffic. Both can scale to the 9-entity firm without scaling the SOC headcount. The trade-off: training data must be labelled, and labelled data is scarce for novel attack types. The audit-defensible answer: only deploy supervised ML where labelled data is sufficient; fall back to DBSCAN + Bayesian + statistical thresholds where it isn't.
  3. Generative AI cuts reporting latency from days to minutes. Mattei (chapter 13.7) shows how generative AI transforms raw incident data into structured summaries. For a firm producing quarterly board packs, weekly SOC dashboards, and incident reports on demand, the time saved is material. The trade-off: GenAI outputs must be reviewed by humans before publication. Chapter 13.10 is direct: "Decisions involving breach response, regulatory compliance, or insider threat detection require human expertise."
  4. The privacy + auditability gap is the dominant risk. Cloud-based GenAI APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic) process the firm's security data on third-party servers — a PDPD compliance issue for a Vietnamese firm. Mattei (chapter 13.10) recommends local models (LM Studio, Ollama) when data sensitivity is high. The audit-defensible answer: "Show me where the data is processed, who has access, and what the data-retention policy is."

By the end of this lesson you will have produced the AI-for-Metrics playbook (in Vietnamese) — three ML model patterns (DBSCAN, random forest, SVM) for threat detection and behavioural baselining; four GenAI patterns (incident recommendation, reporting, scenario generation, trend analysis) with audit-defensible guardrails; and the human-in-the-loop decision policy that keeps the AI assistive, not autonomous. This is what the data-science team and the security-strategy team reference when scaling beyond the rule-based catalogue.

The two AI layers

Mattei (chapters 12 & 13) organises AI for cybersecurity into two distinct layers with different risk profiles:

LayerWhat it doesMattei chapterData sensitivityAudit risk
ML models (unsupervised + supervised) Detects patterns, clusters behaviour, classifies events 12 Medium — processes behavioural data; can run locally Moderate — model outputs are explainable; risk is false positives/negatives
Generative AI (LLMs) Drafts reports, recommends responses, summarises trends 13 High — processes narrative data; cloud APIs are a privacy risk High — outputs are not deterministic; hallucinations possible

The two layers have different deployment patterns and different guardrails. The ML layer can be deployed quietly (model runs, alerts fire, humans review). The GenAI layer must be deployed with humans-in-the-loop (model drafts, humans approve, then publish).

DBSCAN for unsupervised threat detection

Mattei (chapter 12.2) uses DBSCAN to cluster network communication patterns and flag outliers as anomalies. The DBSCAN algorithm doesn't require labelled training data and doesn't require the number of clusters to be specified — it identifies clusters based on density and labels sparse points as noise.

import pandas as pd
from sklearn.cluster import DBSCAN
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Network communication patterns: external vs internal comms
df = pd.DataFrame({
    'Device': ['Laptop_A','Phone_B','Printer_C','Laptop_B','Server_D'],
    'External_Comm': [10, 15, 3, 8, 120],
    'Internal_Comm': [100, 95, 200, 90, 80]
})

# Extract features
X = df[['External_Comm', 'Internal_Comm']]

# DBSCAN: eps=15, min_samples=2
dbscan = DBSCAN(eps=15, min_samples=2)
df['Cluster'] = dbscan.fit_predict(X)

# Server_D is labelled -1 (noise/anomaly) — it makes 120 external comms
# vs the cluster of normal devices making 3–15 external comms.

In Mattei's example, Server_D is the anomaly: 120 external communications vs the 3–15 range of normal devices. For your firm, DBSCAN can be applied to:

  • Per-user login behaviour: cluster users by (login_hour, geographic_region, devices_used); outliers are likely compromised accounts.
  • Per-asset network behaviour: cluster servers by (egress_volume, destination_diversity, protocol_mix); outliers are likely compromised servers or exfiltration attempts.
  • Per-entity financial behaviour: cluster entities by (transaction_volume, transaction_size, counterparty_diversity); outliers are likely fraud patterns.
DBSCAN's parameter problem. The algorithm's two parameters — eps (max cluster distance) and min_samples (min cluster size) — are chosen by the analyst. The audit-defensible answer is: "Show me the parameter selection method, the sensitivity analysis, and the validation against known incidents." A model trained with eps=15 that catches a different set of anomalies than eps=20 is not deterministic. Run the analysis at multiple parameter settings, document which setting catches which incident type, and lock the production setting with rationale.

Random forest + SVM for supervised classification

Mattei (chapter 12.3) introduces random forest (ensemble of decision trees) and SVM (support vector machine) for classifying events as malicious or benign. Both require labelled training data: a dataset of confirmed-benign events and confirmed-malicious events. The model learns the boundary and classifies new events.

ModelStrengthWeaknessCybersecurity application
Random Forest Handles many features; resist overfitting; gives feature importance Less interpretable than single tree; can be slow on huge datasets Phishing-email classification (many features: sender, subject, body, links)
SVM Effective in high-dimensional spaces; works well on small-to-medium datasets Doesn't scale to millions of samples; kernel choice matters Malware classification (binary: malicious/benign from feature vector)
The labelled-data bottleneck. Random forest and SVM are only as good as the labelled dataset. For a 9-entity Vietnamese securities firm with limited historical incident data (fewer than 100 labelled phishing incidents per entity), the model may overfit. The audit-defensible answer is: "Show me the dataset size, the labelling protocol, the train/test split, the cross-validation result." A model trained on 50 samples with 80% accuracy is not audit-defensible; a model trained on 5,000 samples with 95% accuracy is.

Generative AI patterns — four use cases

Mattei (chapter 13) introduces four generative-AI patterns for cybersecurity. Each has a clear use case and a clear guardrail.

Pattern 1 — incident-response recommendation (Mattei 13.8)

For each incident in the queue, generate an immediate-response recommendation. Mattei's example iterates over incident rows, prompts the LLM with the incident type + severity, and appends the recommendation to the dataset. The output is a draft for the SOC analyst to review.

from openai import OpenAI

# Local LLM (LM Studio, Ollama) recommended for PDPD compliance
client = OpenAI(base_url="http://localhost:1234/v1", api_key="lm-studio")

for index, row in incident_data.iterrows():
    prompt = (
        f"Incident type '{row['Incident Type']}', "
        f"severity '{row['Severity']}'. "
        "Provide an immediate-response recommendation (3 lines max)."
    )
    completion = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="TheBloke/Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2-GGUF",
        messages=[
            {"role": "system", "content": "You are a cybersecurity incident handler."},
            {"role": "user", "content": prompt}
        ],
        temperature=0.7
    )
    incident_data.at[index, 'Recommendation'] = (
        completion.choices[0].message.content.strip()
    )

# SOC analyst reviews and approves before publishing.

The guardrail: the analyst must review the recommendation before acting. The LLM's output is a draft, not an instruction. Mattei (chapter 13.10) is explicit: "AI should serve as a decision-support tool rather than an autonomous decision-maker."

Pattern 2 — automated reporting (Mattei 13.7)

For weekly SOC dashboards, quarterly board packs, and incident reports on demand, use generative AI to transform raw metric data into a structured summary. Mattei's chapter 13.7 example uses the LLM to generate JSON data + summary + visualisation. The output replaces hours of analyst work.

The hallucination risk. LLM outputs are not deterministic; they can fabricate numbers, summarise events that never happened, or contradict the source data. The guardrail is required: every AI-generated number must trace back to the source dataset. Mattei (chapter 13.10): "Check generative AI outputs for accuracy and reliability; be aware of the data quality used." The audit-defensible answer is: "Show me the source dataset, the prompt, the model's output, and the human reviewer's sign-off." Without all four, the AI-generated report is unverified.

Pattern 3 — scenario generation for tabletop exercises (Mattei 13.6)

For the IRP tabletop exercises (lesson 0007 R14), generative AI can draft scenario variations: instead of using the same phishing scenario for every quarterly exercise, the LLM generates 5 variations tailored to the firm's threat landscape. The IR Lead reviews the variations and selects the final scenario.

Pattern 4 — trend analysis and executive briefing (Mattei 13.7)

For the quarterly CISO briefing, generative AI drafts the executive summary from the metric dataset. The output is a 1-page narrative with trend statements (e.g., "MTTC-I has improved 40% quarter-over-quarter, driven by the SOC playbook rollout in February"). The CISO reviews and edits before the board sees it.

The privacy + locality decision — local vs cloud LLM

Mattei (chapter 13.10) is direct on privacy: cloud-based GenAI APIs process data on third-party servers, which "can introduce privacy risks." For a Vietnamese securities firm subject to PDPD, the firm should default to local LLMs (LM Studio, Ollama) and reserve cloud APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic) for non-PDPD-scope data.

Data classRecommended LLM deploymentReason
PDPD-scope data (customer PII, employee records, financial transactions) Local LM Studio / Ollama No third-party processing
Internal security telemetry (SIEM events, SOC alerts) Local or private cloud (Llama-3-70B fine-tuned) May include PDPD-scope data in logs
Public threat intelligence (CVE feeds, MITRE ATT&CK) Cloud API acceptable No sensitive data; processing on third-party servers is OK
Synthetic / test data Cloud API acceptable No real data; no compliance risk
The audit-defensible answer for GenAI deployment. When the UBCK examiner (or the PDPC) asks "where is your security data processed?", the answer is: "PDPD-scope data is processed by local LLMs on our own infrastructure. Non-PDPD data may use cloud APIs with anonymised inputs and zero-retention configuration. All AI-generated outputs are reviewed by humans before acting on them. The model + prompt + review chain is logged for every AI-assisted decision." This is the answer the 13.10 chapter is preparing the reader for.

Self-check

Why must every AI-assisted decision have a human accountable, and how is that enforced at the audit table?

Because AI outputs are not deterministic and not legally accountable. A model can fabricate numbers (hallucinate), summarise events that never happened, or recommend actions that contradict business context. Mattei (chapter 13.10) is explicit: "Decisions involving breach response, regulatory compliance, or insider threat detection require human expertise." The enforcement mechanism is the log: every AI-assisted decision must produce (1) the AI's output (model + prompt + raw output), (2) the human reviewer's sign-off (who, when, what was changed), (3) the resulting action (what was actually done). Without all three, the audit-defensible answer is unavailable — the firm cannot show who made the decision, on what evidence, and with what accountability. The three-log pattern is the discipline that keeps AI assistive and the firm in compliance with PDPD + UBCK + the firm's own governance policy.

Why default to local LLMs for PDPD-scope data, and what is the fallback when local is not feasible?

Because Vietnamese PDPD (Decree 13/2023) regulates the processing of personal data, including data processed by third-party AI services. Sending PDPD-scope data to a cloud API (OpenAI, Anthropic) means the data leaves Vietnamese jurisdiction and is processed on infrastructure controlled by a foreign entity — a clear PDPD compliance gap. Local LLMs (LM Studio, Ollama) keep the data on the firm's own infrastructure, eliminating the third-party-processing risk. The fallback when local is not feasible: (1) anonymise data before sending to cloud (strip PII, redact names); (2) use a private-cloud deployment with a Vietnam-resident vendor (VinAI, FPT.AI, etc.); (3) accept the residual risk and document the compensating controls. The audit-defensible answer is the chosen option + the rationale + the documented controls. There is no AI value that justifies processing PDPD-scope data on unverified third-party infrastructure.

Why is DBSCAN's parameter selection a documentation problem, not a modeling problem?

Because DBSCAN's two parameters (eps and min_samples) are chosen by the analyst. Two reasonable analysts can pick different values and detect different anomaly sets. The model itself is sound; the parameter choice is the documentation gap. The audit-defensible answer is: (1) document the parameter selection method (rule of thumb, k-distance graph, sensitivity analysis); (2) show the sensitivity analysis (which eps catches which incident type); (3) lock the production parameter with rationale; (4) validate against known incidents. A model trained with eps=15 that catches X% of known incidents is defensible if the eps was chosen by a documented method. The same model without the documentation is unverified — the analyst picked a number and the firm trusted it. The parameter choice is the auditing problem.
