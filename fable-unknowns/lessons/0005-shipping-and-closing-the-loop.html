Shipping and Closing the Loop

L5 During + post · ~12 min · Lesson 5 (final) of the Finding Your Unknowns series

You've spent the four prior lessons turning a fuzzy problem into a plan. Now you're going to ship the thing. This lesson covers two halves of the same discipline:

Every surprise during execution deserves a log line; every shipped change deserves an artifact the next person can pick up.

The first half — implementation notes — keeps execution honest by surfacing surprises while you still have the context. The second half — pitch, explainer, quiz — closes the loop by handing off the result to everyone who wasn't in the room.

Why the two halves share a lesson
They share a unifying claim: surprises and shipped changes deserve artifacts. The claim only works if the two halves live together — in isolation, the implementation-notes lesson would feel like process hygiene and the post-ship lesson would feel like marketing.

1. Implementation notes — the during-execution log

Wood keeps a notes file open during execution. New entries when you make a decision that wasn't in the plan, hit a wrong assumption, or take a path the plan didn't foresee. The notes are appended, not edited. The file isn't structured prose — it's a log.

The format

A literal implementation-notes.md file in the project root (or in a notes/ directory for larger projects). Sections are loose and may evolve. Two sections deserve to be there:

  1. Surprises and decisions

    Per-execution entries. Date + topic + what happened. "We thought OAuth was already wired; it wasn't. Added auth.ts with the standard config." Fable can write these during execution if you ask, but you write the load-bearing ones yourself because they're judgment.

  2. Deviations from the plan

    What changed vs. the plan and why. "Plan said Postgres triggers; switched to a worker because the trigger would block the request path." Deviations are tracked because the next time someone asks "why isn't this a trigger?" the answer is in the log.
What goes in the notes (and what doesn't)
In: surprises, judgment calls, deviations, architectural decisions that landed during execution. Out: routine progress ("wrote tests"), bug-fix bookkeeping, things already in the plan. The notes file is a *trail of decisions*, not a journal.

A live example: this very series

The implementation of L1–L5 in this workspace is itself a worked example. The notes file is at implementation-notes.md at the workspace root. Browse it: every lesson ships with a notes entry that captures the deviations and surprises that happened during its build.

What's in there is small but load-bearing: the "no forward links to lessons that don't exist yet" rule discovered during L2, the "blindspot pass can backfire with strong priors" inference flagged in L3, the plan-vs-execution drift test in L4. None of these are in the lessons themselves — they're in the notes, where the next session can pick them up.

The drift detector
Wood's notes function isn't just a deviation log — it's a drift detector. When L5's plan said "notes as a literal .md file" and L5 shipped with notes as a literal .md file, the drift was zero. That's a win for the plan, not the notes. When implementation diverges from plan, the notes show the seam.

2. Pitch — the artifact for the next person

When the implementation is done, you produce a pitch: a small document someone who wasn't in the room can read and understand what shipped and why. Wood keeps these as HTML pages in the project's reference/ folder — separate from the work, not buried inside it.

The template

A pitch has a recognizable shape. Four blocks, in order:

  1. The lede

    One or two sentences. What is this, for whom, and why does it matter. If the lede doesn't make someone want to read the next paragraph, the lede is wrong.

  2. The problem it solves

    What was the user pain or the missing capability? Ground in observable behavior, not in feature lists. "Users churn after onboarding because we don't show them what's missing" not "We added a progress bar."

  3. What makes it different

    The 2–4 properties that distinguish this work from the obvious solution. Keep them concrete. If you can't articulate the difference, the work isn't differentiated.

  4. What you'd push back on if challenged

    Pre-empt the strongest objections. "Five lessons is a lot" / "The quizzes test the lesson not the next prompt" / "The live rehearsals are a technical risk." Honesty about the limits makes the assertions about the strengths credible.

A live example: the pitch for this lesson series

The pitch for the Finding Your Unknowns series is at reference/ship-and-close-pitch.html. It demonstrates all four blocks on the work you just spent five lessons learning. Useful as a concrete ref — especially the "what I'd push back on" section, which is the block most pitches skip and most pitches need.

Template + real artifact, not either alone
This is the L4 plan's "template + real artifact" decision, executed. The lesson teaches the template; the artifact demonstrates the template used on the work the lesson ships. The cross-link between the two is the technique.

3. The quiz — judgment under memory

Wood's discipline: "I only merge after I pass the quiz perfectly." A post-implementation quiz is the test that the user understood what shipped, not just that they shipped it. Under memory pressure, the things the user remembers are the things they'd defend in a code review. The quiz probes exactly those things.

What the quiz tests

Not vocabulary. Not "what did the plan say." The quiz tests the user's judgment on the next prompt they'll write. Wood's example: after launching a color-grading feature video, the post-quiz asks "what would you want the next loop to feel like?" — not "what was the prompt phrase?"

For implementation work, three categories of questions are usually enough:

  1. What surprised you

    The user lists the surprises they logged. The quiz validates that the user can recall and rank them — if they can't, they probably don't own the lessons the surprises taught.

  2. What would you change in the next loop

    The user describes the next iteration. The quiz validates that the user has a forward-looking model of the work — if they don't, they're not done, they're paused.

  3. What would you push back on if the work were challenged

    The user names the limits. The quiz validates that the user can defend the work honestly — if they can't, the pitch above is lying and the user knows it.
The quiz is for the user, not for the team
Wood passes the quiz *before merging*. The audience is the user in a future moment, not a reviewer. The questions are uncomfortable by design — they surface things the user hoped they'd forgotten. That's the test.

4. How the four artifacts of a shipped change relate

A shipped change produces four artifacts in Wood's loop:

Artifact When Who reads it
The plan (L4) Before Fable during execution; you, two weeks later, wondering why you decided what you decided
Implementation notes (implementation-notes.md) During You, when you wonder "why isn't this a trigger?"; the next session; a teammate reviewing the diff
The pitch (ship-and-close-pitch.html) After The next person in Slack; reviewers; teammates who weren't in the room
The quiz After You, before merging; the future you defending the work in a code review

These are four different artifacts for different audiences. The mistake is collapsing them — the plan becomes the notes, the notes become the pitch, the pitch becomes the quiz. Each one needs to be written in its own voice for its own reader.

5. How the five lessons compose

A complete loop, in five lessons:

  1. L1 — Map vs. Territory

    Mental model. The 2×2 of unknowns. The iterate-don't-plan loop.

  2. L2 — Blindspot Pass + Interview

    Populate Unknown Unknowns. Broad pass, then narrow questions.

  3. L3 — Brainstorm + Prototype + Reference

    Populate Unknown Knowns. See something before you commit.

  4. L4 — The Implementation Plan

    Synthesize everything. A doc Fable can execute and you can re-read.

  5. L5 (this) — Shipping and Closing the Loop

    Log surprises. Hand off the work. Quizzes the user on what they shipped.

The first three lessons are pre-implementation. L4 closes pre-implementation. L5 covers during + post. The curriculum is complete.

Knowledge check

Three quizzes, each testing judgment on what the user's next loop should look like. Pass them before you consider the lesson finished.

You're shipping a small bug fix and wondering whether it deserves an entry in implementation-notes.md. What's the right call?
You wrote a pitch and someone on the team asks "what's the strongest objection to this work?" You can name one. What does that tell you about the pitch?
After shipping, you fail the post-implementation quiz on "what would you change in the next loop?" You have a real answer. What now?
