The Map vs. The Territory

L1 Mental model · ~10 min · Lesson 1 of the Finding Your Unknowns series

There's an old idea — usually attributed to the cartographer Alfred Korzybski — that the map is not the territory. The map is a representation; the territory is what's actually there. A map of Paris is not Paris. A perfect map is still less than the city.

When you work with a strong model, the same gap opens up. The map is what you give Claude — the prompt, the skills you load, the context, the chat history. The territory is where the work happens — the codebase, the production environment, the real-world constraints of whoever will read what you ship.

Fable 5 is the first model I find where the quality of the output is bottlenecked by how well my map matches the territory. The model is good enough to do most things. What it can't do is read my mind. Every place the map falls short of the territory, Fable has to guess. Every guess can be wrong.

The gap between the two is what I call unknowns

An unknown is the difference between the map I gave Fable and the territory Fable has to traverse. The more work being done, the more unknowns. The longer the chain, the more guesses Fable makes on my behalf. The cumulative cost of those guesses is why the same task can come back perfect once and broken the next time, with what feels like the same prompt.

The name for what I just described is "surprise". Surprise is the unit of unmapped territory. If Fable returns something and I'm not surprised, my map was good enough. If I'm surprised, my map had a hole.

Re-frame
Working with Fable is not "write a good prompt and get output." It's "write the best map you can, watch where Fable is surprised, update the map." This is the loop. There's no fixed endpoint.

The 2×2 of unknowns

Before I send a task to Fable, I split my own ignorance into four buckets. Reading my own prompt through this lens takes about a minute. It's the diagnostic step every technique in this series builds on.

I know I know it I know I don't
Fable knows Known Knowns. Things I've put in the prompt. The "do X, then Y" part. The file paths, the file content, the constraints. This is the visible map. Known Unknowns. Things I've deliberately left for Fable to figure out — but flagged as gaps. "You decide the schema." "I don't know which library is best; pick one."
Fable doesn't Unknown Knowns. Things I know so deeply I'd never write them down — but I'd recognize the wrong output instantly. House style, the codebase's de facto conventions, the user's actual need behind a vague request. Unknown Unknowns. Things I haven't even considered. What I don't know that I don't know. History in the codebase, potholes that "everyone here knows about," how good the work could be if I knew what good looked like.

Read any prompt through this lens and you'll find most of it sits in Known Knowns — that's the part you typed. The interesting parts are the other three cells.

Where the best agentic coders sit on this 2×2

Wood describes watching senior practitioners like Boris Cherny and Jarred Friedman prompt. The visible difference is that they have very few unknowns, in any of the four cells. They know what they want, in detail, before they ask. They're deeply in-sync with both the codebase and the model's behavior. Their map is good.

That part can be learned. The part that's harder to copy is the second one: they also assume their map will have holes. They plan for unknowns. They don't try to write the perfect prompt first; they treat the prompt as an artifact that gets revised. Every surprise is a hole they can name and patch.

The intuition Wood is pointing at, I think, is this: reducing and planning for your unknowns is the skill of agentic coding. It's a learnable skill. You build it by working with Fable — by getting surprised, naming the hole, and updating the map.

A useful target
Don't aim for "no unknowns." Aim for a working map. The map is good enough when the surprises are small, low-stakes, and within your tolerance for rework.

Iterate, don't plan

Most prompt-advice assumes the question is "how do I write a better prompt up front?" That's the wrong question. Fable is good enough that the prompt doesn't need to be perfect the first time — it needs to be revisable. The cheap thing isn't a perfect map, it's a fast loop.

Here's the loop, applied to a familiar task — adding a new slash command to CLAUDE.md that compresses long sessions.

Loop 0 — my Known Knowns

I know the file path (CLAUDE.md). I know the rough structure of other slash commands in there. I know Claude Code loads the file at session start. I write a draft prompt and pass it to Fable.

Loop 1 — the surprise

Fable returns a working command but it triggers on the wrong prefix. I didn't specify the conflict. That's a Known Unknown I forgot to flag — I knew other slash commands existed in this file but didn't disclose them.

Loop 2 — a new Unknown Known surfaces

Reading the diff, I notice my "compress" command doesn't match the tone of the other commands in the file. I would have recognized the wrong tone, but I'd never think to write it down. I add "match the tone of /context-summarize above" to the prompt. The next pass is correct.

Loop 3 — a smaller surprise

Fable's output is now correct in tone and trigger. There's one more thing I didn't ask for but want — a way to test it. That's an Unknown Unknown surfaced by the work itself: I didn't know I needed a test until I saw the output. Worth capturing in a future loop.

Three maps, three loops. The first map wasn't wrong. It was the best I could produce cold, with no surprises to work from. Each surprise named a hole I could patch. The work was good at every loop, just increasingly good. That's the pattern.

Common misread
"Iterate, don't plan" does not mean "skip planning." It means planning is the first loop, not a complete deliverable. The map is revisable. Planning is the start of the loop, not a substitute for it.

The lesson's only durable artifact

Everything above is preamble for this:

Before sending a task to Fable, scan your map through the 2×2. The cells aren't equal. Known Knowns take thirty seconds to write. The other three cells are where your prompts will surprise you, and where the rest of this series spends its time.

That's the lesson. The rest is vocabulary. Wood's seven techniques — blindspot pass, brainstorm and prototype, interview, reference, implementation plan, implementation notes, pitch/explainer/quiz — are all moves to populate the second row of the 2×2. We'll cover each in turn.

Knowledge check
