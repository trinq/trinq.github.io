Blindspot Pass and Interview

L2 Pre-implementation · ~12 min · Lesson 2 of the Finding Your Unknowns series

Lesson 1 left you with a 2×2 of unknowns. Most of your prompts will be loaded into the Known Knowns cell before you send them — that's the part you typed. The interesting work happens in the other three cells. This lesson teaches the two techniques Wood pairs for the cell that matters most when you're entering new territory: Unknown Unknowns.

Two techniques because they fire at different moments. Blindspot pass runs early, when you're staring at a problem and don't even know what you don't know about it. Interview runs after that, once you've done some scanning and need to land the remaining ambiguities one question at a time.

1. Blindspot pass — the early, broad pass

A blindspot pass is a request for Fable to enumerate the unknowns you should worry about before you start. You are deliberately not asking for the answer. You are asking for the shape of the territory.

The trigger phrase
Use the literal words "blindspot pass" and "unknown unknowns". Wood is specific about this. The phrase is a trigger — Fable has been trained on content where these words cluster with a particular behavior. Rephrasing ("explain my blindspots," "what am I missing") produces a thinner pass.

The shape of a good blindspot prompt

Wood's worked example: 

I'm working on adding a new auth provider but I know nothing about the auth
modules in this codebase. Can you do a blindspot pass to help me figure
out my relevant unknown unknowns and help me prompt you better.

Three things to notice:

  1. It discloses the user's level.

    "I know nothing about the auth modules in this codebase" tells Fable how much it has to explain from scratch. Without this line, Fable assumes you know the average amount for someone who asks about auth — usually wrong.

  2. It names the task, not the answer.

    "Adding a new auth provider" is the territory. Fable is being asked to map the unknowns of the territory, not to design the auth.

  3. It frames the output as input to the next loop.

    "Help me prompt you better" makes the pass a draft of the next map, not the final map. That's the iterate-don't-plan discipline from Lesson 1, made explicit.

A second worked example — learning a new domain

I don't know what color grading is but I need to grade this video. Can you
teach me to understand my unknown unknowns about color grading, so that I
can prompt better?

Same shape. Disclose, name the task, frame as input to the next loop. Notice this prompt is asking for teaching, not for the answer — that's the right framing when the Unknown Unknowns are about a domain you don't yet operate in.

Disclosure is part of the prompt
The first sentence ("I don't know what color grading is") is doing real work. Without it, Fable assumes you've heard of color grading and will give you a partial pass. With it, Fable treats you as a beginner and gives a fuller one. Calibrate the disclosure to your actual state.

2. A real blindspot pass, run live

I just ran one on this very lesson — asking myself what unknowns I have about teaching you the blindspot technique. The pass surfaced three findings, each one a real risk I'd have shipped a thinner lesson without.

Finding 1: the literal phrase is the trigger

I'd assumed "do a blindspot pass" and "explain my blindspots" were interchangeable. The pass forced me to check Wood's exact words. They're not interchangeable. "Blindspot pass" is a memorized trigger; paraphrases dilute it.

Risk this lesson would have shipped without the pass: a softer warning that said "you can ask Fable about your blindspots" — true, but useless, because you wouldn't know which words actually move the answer.

Finding 2: disclosure of skill level changes the answer

I knew disclosure mattered in general (every Lesson 1 example pre-stated user knowledge). I didn't realize how much it changes the depth of the pass. Saying "I'm a senior dev" vs. "I'm new to this codebase" vs. "I know the system but not this module" produces three different lists of unknowns.

Risk this lesson would have shipped without the pass: teaching disclosure as a side note instead of as a load-bearing part of the prompt.

Finding 3: blindspot pass can backfire when you have strong priors

This one I did not have. It came out of the pass.

If you already half-believe the answer to a problem — if you have a guess about which unknowns matter — then asking Fable for its list of unknowns can pull you toward Fable's stock list of plausible unknowns. Fable's plausibility and your plausibility are different things. The pass gives you a polished, confident list of the things everyone worries about — and a quieter list of the things you should worry about.

How to defend against it: when you read the pass output, look for items that don't fit your priors. Items you think "that's obvious, why is it on the list" are often the ones you most needed to see. If nothing in the pass feels uncomfortable, the pass wasn't deep enough.

When the pass is too comfortable
A blindspot pass that confirms your priors isn't a pass — it's a press release. The sign of a useful pass is at least one item that makes you stop and think "oh, I'd missed that."

3. Interview — the late, narrow pass

Once you've done a blindspot pass, you have a wider map but a still-noisy one. The interview technique cleans up the noise by handing Fable a single ambiguous question at a time and letting it pick the most consequential one to ask you first. 

Interview me one question at a time about anything ambiguous, prioritize
questions where my answer would change the architecture.

Three parts, each one discipline:

  1. One question at a time

    The most important part. A list of questions — even a numbered list — is an interrogation, not an interview. Fable answers lists differently: it produces generic clarifications. One question at a time forces Fable to surface its best question first, the one with the most leverage.

  2. "Anything ambiguous"

    No constraint on topic. Fable's job is to find the ambiguity, not to decide which area it belongs to. Constraining the topic ("interview me about the API shape") cuts out the ambiguities you didn't know to ask about.

  3. "Where my answer would change the architecture"

    The prioritization heuristic. Fable has a long list of things it could ask. This filter tells it to rank by architectural impact. A question about a variable name ranks below a question about whether two services should share a database.

When to stop the interview

The interview has a cost — you're spending turns of your session to get Fable to ask you questions instead of answering them. Stop when the cost of asking the next question exceeds the value of its answer. Two signals that you've hit the stop condition:

  • Fable's question is about something you've already decided (Known Known).
  • Fable's question is about something you've decided to defer (a deliberate Known Unknown, not an ambiguity).

In both cases the answer doesn't change the map. Stop and start the next pre-implementation step — usually the brainstorm-and-prototype loop in Lesson 3, or the implementation plan in Lesson 4.

Interview vs. brainstorm
Interview narrows: it removes ambiguities one at a time. Brainstorm widens: it surfaces options you haven't considered. They feel similar but the modes are opposite. Run them in that order — narrow first, then widen, or you'll drown in options before you've fixed the framing.

4. How the two techniques compose

In a typical pre-implementation loop, the order is:

  1. Blindspot pass

    A broad sweep. Fable enumerates unknowns in the territory. You read the list, mark which items are real (most should be), discard which aren't, and patch the items that genuinely change your map.

  2. Interview

    A narrow pass. Fable asks one question at a time about remaining ambiguities. You answer; Fable asks the next one. You stop when questions stop changing the architecture.

  3. Brainstorm / prototype — Lesson 3

    Once you've narrowed, you widen: explore options, mock layouts, sketch the unknown-knowns. This is the next lesson.

The two techniques are deliberately cheap. A blindspot pass costs one turn. An interview is five to ten turns at most. Together they're roughly the cost of one medium-quality implementation attempt. That's the trade Wood is pointing at: pay cheaply now, or pay expensively later.

Knowledge check

Each quiz targets a single cell of the 2×2 (Unknown Unknowns is the focus) or a trade-off inside one of the two techniques.

You paraphrase Wood's prompt: "Fable, explain what I might be missing about auth in this codebase." What's the likely failure mode?
Your blindspot pass output is a clean list of eight plausible unknowns, none of which surprise you. What's the right move?
You start an interview with Fable using a numbered list of seven questions instead of "one at a time." What changes?
Fable has asked five interview questions; the sixth is "should the route handler live in users.ts or users-admin.ts?" When should you stop?
