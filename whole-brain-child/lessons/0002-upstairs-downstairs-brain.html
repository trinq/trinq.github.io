Lesson 0002

The Upstairs Downstairs Brain

The thinking brain and the survival brain — and why your toddler lives mostly downstairs

Remember from Lesson 1: your toddler's brain has a left side and a right side. Now let's look at it from top to bottom.

Imagine your child's brain as a two-story house. This mental image will change how you see every tantrum, every "I can't" moment, and every time your toddler seems completely unreasonable.

The House Analogy

Your toddler's brain is literally divided into two parts:

Upstairs Brain

The Thinking Brain — Prefrontal Cortex

Decision making, planning, empathy, self-control, moral reasoning

Downstairs Brain

The Survival Brain — Brain Stem & Limbic System

Basic instincts, strong emotions, fight-flight-freeze, breathing, blinking

Why Your Toddler Is "Downstairs"

The Key Fact

The upstairs brain doesn't finish building until your mid-20s. In toddlers and young children, it's like a second floor that's still under construction — full of scaffolding, exposed beams, and patches of sky showing through.

This is crucial to understand:

  • Your toddler physically cannot always access their upstairs brain
  • They can't "just calm down" on command — the thinking part isn't fully online yet
  • This isn't bad behavior. It's brain development.

The Baby Gate: Meet the Amygdala

Here's something that makes toddler behavior even more challenging: the amygdala.

The amygdala is a tiny almond-shaped structure in the downstairs brain. Its job? Watchdog. It's always scanning for danger, and when it senses threat — real or perceived — it can:

  • "Flip the lid" on the upstairs brain
  • Lower the baby gate that blocks access to upstairs
  • Take over completely — no thinking allowed

When your toddler has no orange Popsicles left, their amygdala sees a crisis. Massive energy floods the downstairs brain. The upstairs brain goes offline. No matter how many times you explain, they literally cannot access logic.

Two Types of Tantrums

This is one of the most practical insights in the whole book. Not all tantrums are the same:

Upstairs Tantrum

Intentional manipulation

  • Child chooses to act out
  • Upstairs brain is working
  • Could stop if they wanted to
  • Testing boundaries

Downstairs Tantrum

Amygdala hijack

  • Child cannot control it
  • Upstairs brain is offline
  • Literally incapable of stopping
  • Overwhelmed by emotion

Strategy #3: Engage, Don't Enrage

Whole-Brain Strategy #3

Appealing to the Upstairs Brain

When your child is struggling, ask yourself: Am I triggering the downstairs brain, or engaging the upstairs?

Commands and threats activate the survival brain. Questions and collaboration engage the thinking brain.

Triggering Downstairs (Bad): "Stop that right now or no TV for a week!"

Engaging Upstairs (Better): "I can see you're frustrated. What's something we could do instead of hitting?"

The second approach:

  • Acknowledges the feeling
  • Invites problem-solving
  • Uses the word "we" — connection
  • Gives practice at thinking, not just obeying

Strategy #4: Use It or Lose It

Whole-Brain Strategy #4

Exercising the Upstairs Brain

The upstairs brain is like a muscle. It grows stronger when you use it. Give your toddler daily practice.

1 Offer choices (even small ones)

"Red cup or blue cup?" "Banana or apple?" This builds decision-making skills.

2 Ask questions about feelings

"Why do you think your brother is crying?" Builds empathy and self-understanding.

3 Let them solve problems

"You and Sophie both want the swing. What could we do?" Resist fixing it for them.

Strategy #5: Move It or Lose It

Whole-Brain Strategy #5

Moving the Body to Avoid Losing the Mind

Physical movement changes brain chemistry. When upstairs goes offline, get the body moving to bring it back.

Research shows: changing your physical state changes your emotional state.

  • Frowning can make you sad; smiling can help you feel better
  • Shallow breathing comes with anxiety; slow breathing calms
  • Running, jumping, dancing can release the energy that amygdala hijack creates

Toddler melting down over spilled milk? Instead of just saying "it's okay," try: "Let's jump up and down five times together, then clean it up!"

Your Practice This Week

🏠
Picture the house
The next time your toddler flips out, visualize: Is the upstairs brain accessible? Is the baby gate down?
🤔
Ask instead of demand
"What should we do?" or "How could we solve this?" engages the upstairs brain better than "Stop it!"
🏃
Move it to lose it
When emotions are high, try physical movement: jumping, running in place, or even a silly dance to reset.

Integrating Yourself

Here's the hard truth: you have an upstairs brain too. And sometimes, your amygdala takes over.

When You Flip Your Lid

When you find yourself yelling, saying something cruel, or feeling completely out of control — you've been hijacked. Your toddler's behavior triggered your downstairs brain.

What to do:

  1. First: Do no harm. Close your mouth. Put your hands somewhere safe.
  2. Remove yourself. "Mommy needs to take a breath." That's not rejection — it's regulation.
  3. Move your body. Jumping jacks, stretching, deep breaths. Get back in your upstairs brain.
  4. Repair quickly. Reconnect and apologize if needed. Your child is watching how adults recover.
