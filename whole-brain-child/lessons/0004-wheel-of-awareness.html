Lesson 0004

The Wheel of Awareness

Teaching your toddler they have choices in how they feel

Here's a scene that plays out in homes everywhere: Your toddler is having a meltdown over something small. You know — logically — that this isn't the end of the world. But your toddler doesn't know that. They ARE the frustration. The anger. The fear.

When a toddler feels something, they often feel like they ARE that feeling. "I am sad" instead of "I feel sad." This isn't just semantics. It's a fundamental difference in how their brain processes emotions.

The good news? You can teach your child to create space between "feeling" and "being" — and it starts with a simple image: the wheel of awareness.

The Wheel of Awareness

Imagine a bicycle wheel. In the center is the hub. Extending outward are spokes leading to the rim.

Hub
(You)
Thoughts Images Feelings Body Sensations
  • The rim = All the things you can notice: thoughts, feelings, images, body sensations, sounds, the outside world
  • The hub = The place inside you that notices all these things
The Key Insight

The hub is you — the part that can step back and say: "I notice I'm feeling angry. But that's just ONE part of me. I'm also feeling curious. And hungry. And loved."

Why "Feel" ≠ "Am"

Here's what happens when a child gets "stuck on the rim":

Instead of "I feel frustrated," they say "I am frustrated." The temporary state becomes a permanent identity.

When your toddler throws a tantrum and says "I HATE you!" — they mean it. In that moment, they ARE the hate. They can't see past it.

But here's the powerful truth:

The Truth

Feelings are like clouds. They pass by. The sun is always there — even when clouds cover it. Your child IS the sun. The feelings are just clouds.

Strategy #8: Let the Clouds Roll By

Whole-Brain Strategy #8

Feelings Come and Go

Teach your toddler that emotions are temporary — they come, they go, and they're not in charge.

For toddlers, you can use very simple language:

"That big mad feeling is here right now. But it will go away. Just like when the rain stops and the sun comes back."

You can also use physical metaphors:

  • "The mad is like a big wave. It goes up, up, up... and then it goes down."
  • "Feelings are like visitors. They come to visit, and then they leave."

This helps your child understand: this moment is hard, but it won't last forever.

Strategy #9: SIFT

Whole-Brain Strategy #9

Paying Attention to What's Inside

Help your child notice the different parts of their inner world: Sensations, Images, Feelings, and Thoughts.

S
Sensations
"My tummy feels wobbly." "My hands feel tight."
I
Images
"I see the dog in my head." "I picture the playground."
F
Feelings
"I feel happy." "I feel scared." "I feel mad."
T
Thoughts
"I think I want milk." "I think the monster is under the bed."

For toddlers, this is mostly about building awareness:

  • Pointing out sensations: "Your tummy is rumbling — that means you're hungry!"
  • Naming feelings as they happen: "I see your face getting red. You feel really mad, don't you?"
  • Noticing images in the mind: "You're picturing the dog, aren't you? That's why you're scared."

Strategy #10: Exercise Mindsight

Whole-Brain Strategy #10

Getting Back to the Hub

When your child gets "stuck on the rim" — fixated on one feeling — help them return to the calm center.

This is essentially a mini mindfulness practice for toddlers:

1 Get present

"Let's take a pause. Let's be still together."

2 Focus on breath

For toddlers: place a small toy on their belly. "Watch the boat go up and down on the ocean."

3 Notice what's here now

"What do you hear right now? What do you feel in your body?"

4 Come back to the hub

"You're the one watching the waves. You're the one who notices the feelings. That's the real you."

Toddler having a meltdown before nap? Try: "Let's lie down together. Put your stuffed bunny on your tummy. Watch him go up and down with your breathing. Breathe in... and out... Just like bunny. Now what does your body feel? Are your shoulders tight? Let's relax them. There. Better."

The Science: Focused Attention Changes the Brain

Here's something amazing: where you focus your attention actually changes your brain.

This is called neuroplasticity. When your child practices returning to their hub — over and over — they're literally building neural pathways that will help them self-regulate for life.

Neurons that fire together, wire together. The more your child practices noticing their feelings, the easier it becomes.

This is why the practices in these lessons aren't just "nice ideas" — they're building your child's brain.

Your Practice This Week

☁️
Name the clouds
When big emotions come, say: "Here's the mad cloud. Here's the sad cloud. They'll pass by." Simple, but powerful.
🔍
Play SIFT together
During a calm moment, ask: "What does your body feel? What pictures are in your head?" Start building awareness early.
🚣
Try the breathing boat
When overwhelmed, lie down with your toddler. Put a small toy on their belly. Watch it rise and fall with breathing.

Your Own Wheel

Here's a question worth asking yourself:

What rim points am I focused on right now? What am I missing?

When you're stuck in frustration with your toddler — that's one rim point. But you're also tired (rim point), you love your child (rim point), you're doing your best (rim point).

When you return to your hub, you can see the bigger picture. You're not just a frustrated parent — you're a whole person with many feelings, and so is your child.
