Lesson 0003

Memory and Healing

How past experiences shape present behavior — and how to help

Here's a scenario that might sound familiar: Your toddler screams in terror at the doctor's office — even though they've been there before. Or refuses to go near the dog after a single scary encounter. Or suddenly falls apart at daycare drop-off after months of smooth goodbyes.

Sound irrational? It isn't. The answer lies in how memory actually works in the brain.

The Truth About Memory

Forget everything you think you know about memory. It's not a filing cabinet. And it's not a video recording.

The Rule

"Neurons that fire together, wire together."

Every experience causes brain cells to connect. Those connections ARE memory. The more two things happen together, the stronger the connection.

Think about it:

  • Every time you picked up your baby after work, the neurons for "Daddy comes home" connected with "Getting a hug." Now your toddler expects that hug the moment you walk in.
  • One scary moment with a dog = the neurons for "dog" and "danger" got linked. Now even friendly dogs trigger fear.

Memory isn't stored. It's encoded as connections. And when one neuron fires, it pulls all its connected friends along with it.

Two Types of Memory

Here's something most parents don't know: there are two kinds of memory working in your child's brain right now.

Explicit Memory

Conscious recollection

  • Things you know you remember
  • Stories you can tell
  • "Remember when we went to the beach?"
  • Develops after ~18 months

Implicit Memory

Unconscious influence

  • You don't know it's happening
  • Body sensations, emotions, habits
  • "I don't know why I'm scared" (but your body does)
  • Forms even before birth

The problem? Implicit memory shapes behavior without your child knowing why.

A scary doctor visit doesn't just stay in memory — it creates a mental model: "Doctor = bad." Now every white coat triggers fear, even without conscious thought.

The Hippocampus: Your Child's Memory Detective

There's a part of the brain whose job is to make sense of all these scattered memories: the hippocampus.

Think of it as a puzzle master. Implicit memories are scattered puzzle pieces. The hippocampus pulls them together into a coherent picture — making implicit memories explicit so your child can understand what happened.

The Key Insight

When implicit memories stay hidden, they act like buried land mines — exploding in unexpected moments. When we make them explicit, we give our kids power over them instead of being controlled by them.

Strategy #6: The Remote Control

Whole-Brain Strategy #6

Replaying Memories with Control

Help your child revisit scary or upsetting experiences by giving them a "remote control" for their internal DVD player.

Kids can imagine a movie playing in their head. With a remote, they can pause scary parts, fast-forward past overwhelming moments, or rewind to the happy ending.

1 Tell the story once, simply

"So the dog came over and scared you, and you ran away."

2 Introduce the remote

"Now let's watch it again in your head. But this time, you have the remote. If it gets too scary, just hit pause."

3 Let them control the pace

Pause, fast-forward, rewind — whatever they need to get through the story.

4 End with the resolution

Always return to the part where things turned out okay.

This works because:

  • It gives control back to your child
  • The hippocampus can do its job and integrate the memory
  • Scary becomes story — something happened, and it ended

Strategy #7: Remember to Remember

Whole-Brain Strategy #7

Making Recollection Part of Daily Life

Tell stories together. The more kids practice remembering, the stronger their memory integration becomes.

Memory is like a muscle — the more you use it, the stronger it gets. And for toddlers, this practice starts with simple questions.

For toddlers (1-3):

  • "What did you do at the park today?"
  • "Who did you play with? What did you play?"
  • "Remember when we went to the zoo? The monkeys were so silly!"

For building positive associations:

After a potentially scary experience (like the doctor), deliberately recall the positive parts: "Remember when the doctor gave you the sticker? You loved that one!"

This creates new neurons-to-fire connections — building positive memories that can balance out the scary ones.

For Your Toddler: Practical Applications

Here's how this applies to your everyday life with a 1-3 year old:

1 When they fall apart at daycare drop-off

Don't just rush through it. Later, at home, recall it together: "This morning you cried when Mama left. But then what happened? Ms. Sarah gave you a hug and you played with the trucks. And then I came back!"

2 After a scary moment

Once calm, briefly tell the story: "That loud noise scared you. It was just the garbage truck. It went away. You're safe."

3 Build positive expectations

"We're going to Grandma's house today! Remember last time? She made pancakes and you fed the ducks."

Your Practice This Week

🔍
Look for the pattern
Notice when your toddler reacts strongly to something. Ask: "Is this about now, or about something from before?"
📺
Try the remote control
If your child had a scary or upsetting experience, offer to retell the story. Let them hit "pause" if it gets too much.
🌟
Build positive memories daily
Take 2 minutes before bed to recall something good from the day. "Remember when we made sandcastles? That was so fun!"

Your Own Hidden Memories

Here's a question worth asking yourself:

"Is my reaction making sense? Or is something from my past being triggered?"

Your implicit memories affect your parenting too. A scary medical experience from your childhood might make you anxious at the pediatrician. A memory of your own parents yelling might make you over-react to normal toddler behavior.

When you notice your own disproportionate reactions, that's an opportunity to ask: "Where is this coming from? What am I remembering?"
