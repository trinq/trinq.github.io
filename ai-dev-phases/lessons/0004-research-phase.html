The Research Phase — When Exploration Earns Its Keep

Idea names the work. Research does the hard exploration that makes execution AFK-able. This lesson covers when to write a research.md, what to put in it, and why the manager's job is to make sure research.md is findable, not just well-written.

Source: Matt Pocock — "The 7 phases of AI-driven development", AI Hero / YouTube, 8:26, 2026-03-03. Builds on lessons 0001–0003.

How to use this lesson. The Research phase is where most teams either over-invest (documenting what Slack can answer) or under-invest (rediscovering every hard problem in every session). Read the "expensive filter" section first. Then open your team's last 3 features and ask: "did a research.md exist for the hard part? Was it findable?" If not, you have a Research phase problem hiding inside a velocity problem.

What Pocock Actually Said

The full quote, in context:

"The second phase is research. So you might explore the library, you might look at a codebase, you might do some reading. You might want to do some experimentation. If that exploration is difficult, you might want to cache it in a research.md asset." — Matt Pocock

Twelve seconds. The word "might" appears three times — he is deliberately non-prescriptive. Research is the optional phase. The question this lesson answers: when is research.md worth writing, and when is it overhead?

The Agent Context Window Problem — Why Research Exists

For human teams, research is often kept in someone's head or discussed in a Slack thread. That's fine when the explorer and the executor are the same person. When they're not — or when an AI agent is the executor — the research disappears at the end of the session.

The Research phase exists because of a specific engineering problem: context windows have a limit, and exploration has a cost.

  • A human can spend 2 hours exploring the Stripe API, remember the key gotcha ("webhooks retry 72 hours, not 24"), and apply it on day 14 of the project.
  • An AI agent in session 14 has no memory of session 1's exploration. If the gotcha wasn't written down, it gets rediscovered — or it causes a bug.

Research.md is not documentation for humans. It's context injection for future sessions. The person who writes it isn't necessarily the person who benefits from it. That's the manager's insight: research.md is a team asset, not a personal note.

Manager instinct. When you see a team re-making the same technical decision twice, you don't have a talent problem. You have a Research phase problem. The first exploration wasn't cached — so the second session paid for it. That's a process cost, not a people cost. Fix the Research phase and the re-decisions stop.

The Expensive Filter — When Does Research Earn Its Keep?

Pocock says research.md is for "difficult" exploration. Here's a practical filter:

Rule of thumb: if answering the question "why did we do it this way?" would take more than 10 minutes of re-exploration for someone who wasn't in the room, write a research.md. If the answer lives in a code comment, that's fine too — just make sure the comment has a timestamp and a link to the source.

Anatomy of a research.md

Not all research.md files need all sections. Use what's relevant. But every research.md should answer these questions:

research/[area]-[question].md — minimum viable template

  • Question: What hard problem does this exploration answer? (One sentence.)
  • Context: Why does this question matter for the idea? What's at stake?
  • Sources: Links to docs, PRs, Stack Overflow, internal threads. (With dates.)
  • Findings: What you learned. Be specific. Include counter-intuitive things.
  • Decision: What are we doing, and why? (Not "we'll use X" — "we'll use X because A, B, C.")
  • Gotchas / Watch-outs: What will bite someone who hasn't read this doc? (Be honest.)
  • Code snippets: The 3 lines that would have taken 2 hours to write from scratch.
  • Last verified: YYYY-MM-DD — dependencies, not just the doc. Link to the version checked.
  • Owner: Who to ping if this goes stale. (Usually the explorer.)
  • Linked idea: Which idea (from phase 01) is this research for?

Research.md naming convention. File name should answer the question, not the answer. research/stripe-webhook-retry-behavior.md is findable. research/api-research.md is not. The agent needs to find it when the question is "what happens when Stripe retries a webhook?"

The Lifetime Problem — When Does research.md Expire?

Every research.md has a half-life. The moment a dependency ships a breaking change, the research.md becomes misleading. The moment an internal API is refactored, the code snippets are dangerous.

The most expensive failure mode: stale research.md causes confident wrong decisions. The explorer knew it was dated. The next person didn't.

The version-linking rule. Every research.md should link to the exact version of the external dependency it was verified against. "Verified against Stripe SDK v14.2.0, 2026-01-15." When Stripe ships v15, the research.md needs re-verification. Without the version link, nobody knows when to re-check.

Three Team Scenarios

Scenario A — No research.md for a hard problem

Engineer A spends 3 hours figuring out that the Turso local emulator has a bug that conflicts with Turso Studio. She discovers the workaround on a GitHub issue. She tells Engineer B verbally in standup: "yeah, just use the CLI, not Studio."

What happens next: Week 2, Engineer C joins the team. She spends 3 hours on the same problem. She finds a different workaround that introduces a subtle bug. The bug ships to production. Research.md would have prevented this.

Diagnostic: A 3-hour exploration without a durable artifact. The cost: 3 hours × N sessions of re-exploration, compounding.

Scenario B — Vague research.md (too broad, no decision)

Engineer B writes: research/auth-strategy.md — 8 pages covering OAuth, JWT, sessions, every auth library they considered, trade-offs, and a final line: "TBD — need to discuss as a team."

What happens next: The doc is too broad to be actionable. The agent asks "which auth approach should I use?" and the research.md can't answer because it was never decided. Research.md without a decision is a meeting agenda, not research. Enter the Prototype phase from Research, and the team builds two approaches in parallel.

Diagnostic: Research phase ran, but the Decision section is empty. The 4-function model from lesson 0003 applies here — even research.md needs to name a decision.

Scenario C — Well-scoped research.md, findable location

Engineer B writes: research/stripe-webhook-idempotency-retry-storm.md with sections: Question, Context, Sources (Stripe docs + 2 GitHub issues + 1 internal Slack thread), Findings, Decision (use Stripe's Idempotency-Key header with 24-hour window, plus DB-level deduplication), Gotchas (SDK retry logic fires before your idempotency handler), Code snippets (the 10-line idempotency check), Last verified (Stripe SDK v14.2.0, 2026-06-10), Owner (Engineer B), Linked idea (Idea #042 — subscription renewal webhook reliability).

Why it works: Scoped to one hard question. Decision is explicit. Code snippets are copy-paste ready. Version is linked. Location is findable. Phase 06 (Execution) can run AFK from this doc.

Diagnostic: Research.md is a durable, findable, version-linked decision artifact. The Idea → Research → Prototype chain is clean.

Where research.md Lives — The Findability Problem

The best research.md in the world is worthless if the agent can't find it. Three rules:

  1. Location convention. research/[domain]-[question].md in the repo root. Not in a Google Doc. Not in Notion without a link from the repo. In the repo, where the agent operates.
  2. Index it. A single research/README.md that lists all research.md files with one-line summaries and linked ideas. The agent reads this on every new session. Without it, the agent has to know to run find . -name "*.md" | xargs grep "stripe".
  3. Link from the idea. The Idea phase artifact (lesson 0003) should link to the research.md for that idea. Bidirectional links: idea → research, research → idea. This closes the loop for phase review.

The manager's findability checklist. Before a long-running agent session, ask: "does research/README.md exist? Does it index the right files? Does the active idea link to its research?" If any answer is no, the Research phase is broken downstream — and phase 06 will pay for it in context-window re-explanation.

Research and the Downstream Phases

The Research phase has the tightest coupling to both neighbors:

Research ← Idea

Phase 01 names the work. Phase 02 does the hard exploration that the named idea requires. If the idea isn't well-named (lesson 0003: 5/5 on the naming test), the research scope is unclear. Research is bounded by the idea's scope.

Research → Prototype

If the research phase is skipped or half-done, the Prototype phase becomes a research spike — you're building to learn, not building to validate. Prototype should validate a decision already made in Research, not make the decision.

Research → Execution

Research.md is what makes phase 06 (Execution) AFK-able. The agent needs the decision, the gotchas, and the code snippets to run without re-explaining. Without research.md, every execution session is a new exploration session.

The Manager Ritual for Research

Three questions, applied at three moments:

Research phase ritual — 3 questions × 3 moments

At idea intake (phase 01 → 02 transition)

  • "What's the hard exploration in this idea?"
  • "Is the answer already in a research.md somewhere?"
  • If yes: link it. If no: is the exploration expensive enough to warrant a research.md?

Before phase 06 execution (any long-running session)

  • "Does a research.md exist for this idea?"
  • "Does research/README.md index it?"
  • "Is the last-verified field current?"

Monthly / quarterly review

  • "Which research.md files haven't been verified in over 90 days?"
  • "Which ones should be archived vs. re-verified?"
  • "Are we re-discovering things that are already documented?"

Research vs. the Other Phases — A Quick Distinction

Teams often confuse Research with:

  • Research vs. spike. A spike is time-boxed exploration (usually 1–3 days) that ends in a yes/no decision. Research.md is the artifact of the Research phase; a spike branch is the artifact of a time-boxed exploration that may be a different process. In Pocock's framework, research and spike overlap — but spike implies a time limit, research does not.
  • Research vs. design doc. A design doc argues for a direction. A research.md documents what you found. Design docs live in PRD territory (phase 04). Research.md lives here, in phase 02.
  • Research vs. code comments. Code comments document what the code does. research.md documents what the code can't tell you — the reasoning, the gotchas, the alternatives considered. Code comments are for readers of the code; research.md is for explorers of the decision.
Recall exercise: audit your last sprint's research.md hygiene

Go to your team's repo. Run:

find . -name "research.md" -o -name "research" -type d | head -20

Then for each file found, answer:

  1. Does it have a Question section? (One sentence.)
  2. Does it have a Decision section? (Not "TBD" or "needs discussion.")
  3. Does it have a Last verified field with a date?
  4. Does it have a version link to the dependency it was checked against?
  5. Is it indexed in research/README.md?

Score: each yes = 1 point. Below 3/5 → the Research phase is leaking downstream. Above 4/5 → your team has good research hygiene. Neither is a judgment — it's a measurement.

Final note on this lesson. The Research phase's job is deceptively simple: make hard exploration durable. But durability requires three things most teams skip — a scoped question, an explicit decision, and a version link. Without those three, research.md is a note. With them, it's a team asset that compounds in value across every session that doesn't have to re-ask the question. The manager's job: make research.md findable. The engineer's job: write the decision. Separate responsibilities, one artifact.
