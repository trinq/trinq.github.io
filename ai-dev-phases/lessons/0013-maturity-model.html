Team Maturity Model — Where Is Your Team and How to Level Up

The 7-phase framework doesn't work the same way at every team. A team of 2 and a team of 12 require different setups. A team shipping 3 features a week has different process needs than a team shipping 1 a quarter. This lesson gives you the tools to assess where your team actually is, where you need to be, and the path between them.

Source: Synthesis of all lessons 0001–0012. This lesson is the capstone — it maps every concept in the curriculum to a team maturity level and tells you which concept to prioritize next based on where your team is.

How to use this lesson. Start with the Team Self-Assessment — 12 questions that score your team on the 5 maturity dimensions. The score tells you your current level. Then read the Level Detail section for your level and the level above it. Finally, use the Advancement Roadmap to identify the 2–3 specific actions that will move you to the next level fastest.

The 5 Maturity Levels: Overview

The 7-phase system has 5 maturity levels. Each level is defined by a specific set of behaviors — not team size, not tooling, not headcount. Two-person teams can be Level 4. Ten-person teams can be Level 1. The level is about how the team operates, not who they are.

LevelNameCore behaviorShip confidenceLoopback cost
1 Ad-hoc No phases. Build fast, ship, debug. Unpredictable 100% of feature cost
2 Phases exist Team knows phases. Artifacts are inconsistent. Low–Medium 60–80% of feature cost
3 Phases formalized Artifacts are deposited. Handoffs have rules. Medium–High 30–50% of feature cost
4 Phases optimized HFMs tracked. Loopback detector runs proactively. High 10–20% of feature cost
5 Self-correcting Process learns from each iteration. R3 implemented before next feature. Very High 0–5% of feature cost

The most common error in maturity assessment: Teams believe they are 1–2 levels above where they actually are. The reason is simple: the behaviors that define Level 3 are easy to claim and hard to sustain. "We have artifacts" is a Level 2 claim. "Our artifacts are always in the right place, always read by the next phase owner, and always contain an assumptions section" is a Level 3 reality. Read the Level Detail section carefully before assessing your team.

Team Self-Assessment

Answer each question honestly. Score: 0 = never / 1 = sometimes / 2 = usually / 3 = always. Add your total.

Question 0123
1. Every feature starts with a written idea that names a user and a measurable problem.
2. Research artifacts are in a standard location and include a freshness date.
3. The PRD has non-goals and testable requirements before implementation starts.
4. Every impl plan step is completable in 2–4 hours and maps to a PRD requirement.
5. Each execution step has an explicit supervision tier (Tier 1 / 2 / 3) before it starts.
6. Deviations in execution are logged with root cause, not just outcome.
7. QA verdict is binary: ship or don't ship. No "ship with known issues."
8. Every phase has an explicitly named owner (Manager / Engineer / Agent).
9. Handoff failure modes (HF-01 to HF-07) are tracked across features.
10. The loopback pattern detector (lesson 0010) runs proactively during each feature.
11. A retrospective runs within 24 hours of every QA verdict, with R3 action items written.
12. R3 items from the last retrospective were implemented before the current feature started.

13–20 = Level 2 (Phases exist)

34–36 = Level 5 (Self-correcting)

Level Detail

Level 1 — Ad-hoc

Score 0–12

What it looks like: The team builds features without a predictable sequence. Someone has an idea, someone builds it, it gets tested if there's time, it ships. When it breaks, it gets fixed. There's no document called a PRD. There's no plan called an impl plan. The team's memory is the process.

What to do first: Stop trying to optimize. Start with the minimum viable structure: write the idea down before building it. That's it. One sentence: "Who has the problem, what is the problem, what does success look like." Everything else is secondary.

What not to do: Don't implement all 7 phases at once. Don't write a 40-field PRD template. Don't hire a process coach. Level 1 teams that try to leap to Level 3 usually collapse back to Level 1 within 2 features.

Typical team: 1–3 people, early stage product, or high-urgency environment where speed feels more important than quality.

Level 2 — Phases Exist

Score 13–20

What it looks like: The team knows there are phases. They talk about "the PRD" and "the plan." Artifacts exist but they're inconsistent — sometimes in Notion, sometimes in a Google Doc, sometimes in Slack. Loopbacks are surprises. The team is always reacting to something that went wrong in a previous phase.

What to do first: Standardize artifact locations. Pick one place for all artifacts. Use the naming convention: features/[name]/phase-[number]-[name].md. Enforce it.   3. Assign one owner per phase (fixes HF-04)

Typical team: 3–6 people, some process awareness, has shipped features that worked and features that didn't, starting to feel the cost of rework.

Typical team: 5–10 people, has strong individual discipline, struggling with systemic improvement. This is where most "mature" teams are — and where they get stuck because they've confused "following the process" with "the process is working."

Level 5 — Self-Correcting

Score 34–36

What it looks like: HF-01 and HF-02 are eliminated. Loopbacks fire early and are brief. Retrospectives produce exactly 1 R3 item per feature that is implemented before the next feature starts. The team's process is measurably improving every iteration. The rate of improvement is faster than the rate of change in the environment.

What to do at Level 5: Maintain. The risk at Level 5 is complacency and process bureaucracy — adding rituals that feel productive but don't improve outcomes. At Level 5, the measure of a good change is: does it reduce loopback cost or increase ship rate? If yes, implement it. If no, don't.

What to do first: Teach other teams. Level 5 teams that don't transfer knowledge become fragile — dependent on specific people who know the system. Documenting the team's specific R3 learnings into a team playbook is the highest-value activity at Level 5.

Typical team: 10–20+ people, consistently ships features on time, low production incident rate, process is a recruiting asset ("this is how we work").

The Advancement Roadmap

The fastest path between levels is not to implement everything at once. It is to find the single highest-leverage action at each level and do that until it is automatic.

Level 1 → Level 2

One action: Write the idea down before building it. One sentence. The rest follows.

Level 2 → Level 3

One action: Standardize artifact location. Everything goes in features/[name]/. No exceptions. No "I'll send it to you." Enforce it for 3 features. By feature 4 it will be a habit.

Level 3 → Level 4

One action: Run the loopback pattern detector at the start of every execution phase. Not after things go wrong — proactively, before things go wrong. The detector costs 5 minutes. A late loopback costs 2 weeks.

Level 4 → Level 5

One action: Implement R3 item follow-through. Track which R3 items from the last retrospective were implemented before the next feature's Idea phase. If they're not implemented, they don't count. The gap between "we decided this in retro" and "we did this before the next feature" is the difference between Level 4 and Level 5.

The single most important metric for your team's maturity: Are the R3 items from your last retrospective implemented before your next feature started? Everything else in this lesson is preparation for that question. If you can answer "yes" consistently, your team is Level 5. If you can't, your team is somewhere between Level 2 and Level 4 — and you know exactly which level by how consistently you're doing it.

Level 5 in practice: The team that ships without surprises

A Level 5 team starts a feature. Idea is written and scored. Research answers the hard questions and deposits evidence with freshness dates. PRD has 3 non-goals and 8 testable requirements. Impl plan has 14 M-sized steps, each mapped to a requirement. Execution runs with Tier 2 trust on 10 steps, Tier 1 on 4. The loopback detector fires a Mid-tier signal at step 6. The team loops back to impl plan, revises step 6, and continues. No late loopback. No surprise. QA finds 2 issues, both fixed before ship. Retrospective runs 18 hours after QA verdict. R3 item written: "Add evidence freshness date to all research artifacts — 2 features last quarter had stale evidence." It is implemented before the next feature's Idea phase starts. The team is Level 5.
