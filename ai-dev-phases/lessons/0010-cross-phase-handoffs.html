Cross-Phase Handoffs — The Invisible Work Between Phases

The phases don't end when the next phase begins. Every phase transition carries a package of assumptions, decisions, and unknowns. Most process failures happen in the handoff — not inside any phase. This lesson maps the 7 failure modes of handoffs, assigns phase ownership, builds a loopback pattern detector, and closes with a retrospective taxonomy.

Source: Integration lesson. Builds on lessons 0001–0009 (Matt Pocock — "The 7 phases of AI-driven development," AI Hero / YouTube, 2026). This lesson synthesizes the 7-phase sequence into a cross-phase system view.

How to use this lesson. Read the "phases are a system" section first — it establishes the handoff concept. Then read the 7 failure modes — each maps to a specific phase transition and has a fix. The phase ownership matrix clarifies who does what. The loopback pattern detector is the most actionable tool in this lesson: it gives you the early warning signals for when the process is about to fail. The retrospective taxonomy is the template to run after every feature, regardless of outcome.

The Core Idea: Phases Are a System, Not a Checklist

Lessons 0001–0009 described each phase individually. This lesson describes what happens between phases — and why that's where most process failures actually live.

Each phase produces an artifact. Each artifact is the input to the next phase. A phase transition (handoff) is the act of passing that artifact forward. The failure modes of handoffs are different from the failure modes of phases:

  • Phase failure: A phase produces the wrong artifact (wrong PRD, wrong plan, wrong execution log)
  • Handoff failure: The right artifact is produced but the receiver can't use it — wrong format, wrong assumptions, missing context, wrong owner

Most teams optimize for phase quality (they care about good PRDs, good plans, good code). Few teams optimize for handoff quality. That's the gap this lesson addresses.

Forward flow: Idea → Research → Prototype → PRD → Impl Plan → Execution → QA → Ship

QA is the only phase that can loop back to any earlier phase. The three valid loopbacks from QA:

  • QA → Idea: Feature shipped but doesn't solve the user's problem. Loopback C (kill). Lesson 0009, §The Three Loopbacks.
  • QA → PRD: Build fails at the requirement level. Loopback B (rollback). Lesson 0009, §The Three Verdicts.
  • QA → Impl Plan: Build fails at the implementation level. Loopback Fix path. Lesson 0009, §Verdict 2.

Impl Plan can loop back to Research (when the approach is wrong) or to PRD (when requirements are wrong). Research can loop back to Idea (when the idea needs refining before evidence is gathered). Prototype can loop back to Idea (when the prototype reveals the idea is wrong). The loops are not failures — they are the system's correction mechanism.

The 7 Handoff Failure Modes

Each handoff failure mode has a name, a description of what goes wrong, the phase transition it affects, and a fix. The fix is always a document-format or process requirement at the sending phase — not a check at the receiving phase.

HF-01: Artifact Drop

High frequency

What goes wrong: The sending phase produces an artifact, but it never arrives at the receiving phase in a usable form. The artifact exists in the team's memory, in a Slack thread, or in a file that's not in the standard location. The receiving phase starts from scratch or from incorrect assumptions.

Affected transitions: Any. Most common: Idea → Research, PRD → Impl Plan, Execution → QA.

Why it happens: No artifact deposit rule. The team assumes "everyone knows" because the conversation was recent.

Fix: Every phase must deposit its artifact in a standard location before the next phase begins. Standard path: features/[name]/phase-[number]-[name].md. The next phase's first action is to read the prior artifact — not to ask for it.

Signal in retrospective: "Phase 3 started without reading Phase 2 output" or "The impl plan didn't reference the PRD".

HF-02: Assumption Inheritance

High frequency

What goes wrong: The sending phase makes assumptions that the receiving phase inherits without realizing it. The receiving phase treats these assumptions as facts. Later, when the assumption turns out to be wrong, every downstream phase has to redo work.

Affected transitions: Research → Prototype (assumptions about what the prototype needs to prove), PRD → Impl Plan (assumptions about what the plan needs to cover), Impl Plan → Execution (assumptions about what the execution steps need to handle).

Why it happens: Assumptions are invisible. They don't appear in the artifact because they were so obvious to the sender that they weren't worth writing down.

Fix: Every artifact must contain an explicit # Assumptions section. The receiving phase must read the assumptions section and flag any that are wrong before proceeding. This is not optional — it's the assumption review gate.

Signal in retrospective: "We assumed X was true but it wasn't" — where X appears nowhere in the prior artifact.

HF-03: Granularity Mismatch

High frequency

What goes wrong: The sending phase produces an artifact at the wrong level of detail for the receiving phase's needs. The receiving phase either has to work harder (artifact is too coarse) or gets paralyzed (artifact is too fine).

Affected transitions: Prototype → PRD (prototype proves concept but PRD needs requirements; gap between proof and spec), PRD → Impl Plan (PRD has user stories but impl plan needs M-sized steps), Impl Plan → Execution (plan has checkpoints but execution needs action-level detail).

Why it happens: Each phase has its own granularity ladder (lesson 0006). The ladder doesn't align automatically between phases. The sender doesn't know what level of detail the receiver needs.

Fix: Phase contracts. Before sending an artifact, the sender asks: "What level of detail do you need from me?" The receiver defines their minimum artifact requirements. This is a 5-minute conversation, not a document.

Signal in retrospective: "The impl plan had 3 steps but each step took 2 days" or "The PRD had 40 requirements but only 8 were buildable".

HF-04: Owner Ambiguity

Medium frequency

What goes wrong: Two people believe they own the same phase, or no one believes they own it. The phase starts late, runs in parallel with the wrong scope, or produces an artifact with no clear owner to defend it.

Affected transitions: Research → Prototype (both engineer and manager think the other is defining the prototype scope), Prototype → PRD (no clear owner for writing the PRD), PRD → QA (no clear owner for running the test traceability check).

Why it happens: Ownership is assumed, not assigned. The team is small so "everyone owns everything." The manager thinks the engineer is driving; the engineer thinks the manager is driving.

Fix: Explicit owner assignment at the start of each phase. One owner per phase. The owner is responsible for artifact quality and for the handoff. Ownership is not about authority — it's about accountability. See the Phase Ownership Matrix below.

Signal in retrospective: "We weren't sure who was running the PRD phase" or "Both of us thought the other was writing the impl plan".

HF-05: Evidence Decay

Medium frequency

What goes wrong: The research phase produces evidence. Weeks later, the impl plan phase uses that evidence to make architectural decisions. But the evidence has decayed — the landscape changed, the user need shifted, the technology evolved. The impl plan makes decisions on stale evidence.

Affected transitions: Research → Impl Plan (most common), Prototype → PRD (user need shifts between prototype and spec), Idea → Research (stale problem statement).

Why it happens: Research artifacts don't include a freshness date. The impl plan doesn't check whether the evidence is still valid. Evidence decay is invisible until the build is shipped.

Fix: Every research artifact must include a # Evidence Freshness section with: (1) date collected, (2) conditions under which it's valid, (3) expiry date or trigger. The impl plan phase must review freshness before using research evidence.

Signal in retrospective: "The architecture decision was based on research from 6 weeks ago" or "User needs shifted between research and build".

HF-06: Scope Creep in Transit

Medium frequency

What goes wrong: Between phases, new information arrives — a user interview, a competitor release, an internal stakeholder request — and gets incorporated into the next phase's artifact without going through the idea gate. The feature scope grows in transit, invisibly.

Affected transitions: Research → Prototype (new user data mid-research), PRD → Impl Plan (stakeholder adds requirements after PRD is signed off), Impl Plan → Execution (engineer adds polish items mid-implementation).

Why it happens: The team is excited and responsive. New information feels valuable. There's no gate between phases that says "this new information goes back to Idea, not straight into the build."

Fix: Inter-phase gate rule: any new requirement discovered between phases goes back to Idea for evaluation. Not to the current phase's artifact. Not to the next phase's artifact. To Idea. Idea decides whether the new requirement changes scope.

Signal in retrospective: "We added 5 features that weren't in the PRD" or "The scope grew by 40% between sign-off and ship".

HF-07: Premature Forward Motion

Low frequency but high damage

What goes wrong: The current phase hasn't finished, but the next phase starts anyway — either because of schedule pressure, or because the team is excited, or because the phase boundary was never formally checked. The next phase builds on an incomplete artifact. Errors compound.

Affected transitions: Research → Prototype (prototype starts before research is conclusive), Prototype → PRD (PRD written before prototype is done), PRD → Impl Plan (plan written while PRD is still being revised), Impl Plan → Execution (execution starts before plan is reviewed), Execution → QA (QA starts before execution log is complete).

Why it happens: Schedule pressure. Enthusiasm. The belief that "we know enough to move forward." The absence of a formal phase-end check.

Fix: Phase-end gate. Before the next phase begins, the owner of the current phase formally closes it: (1) artifact is complete and deposited, (2) all assumptions are documented, (3) the receiving phase owner acknowledges receipt and confirms the artifact meets their minimum requirements. This is a 10-minute ritual. It prevents days of rework.

Signal in retrospective: "We started execution before the impl plan was fully reviewed" or "QA found gaps that should have been caught in execution".

Frequency and Damage Map

High frequency doesn't always mean high damage. HF-07 (premature forward motion) is low frequency but can invalidate an entire build. HF-01 and HF-02 are the most common and should be eliminated first — they compound every other failure mode.

Phase Ownership Matrix

Three roles: Manager (M), Engineer (E), Agent (A). "Both" means two roles share ownership. The owner is accountable for artifact quality and the handoff to the next phase. Non-owners participate but don't drive.

PhasePrimary OwnerAccountable forNon-owner role
01 Idea Manager Buildable question test; traceable to user problem Engineer advises on feasibility signal
02 Research Manager + Engineer Evidence chains; hard questions answered; freshness dates Agent synthesizes research notes
03 Prototype Engineer Proof of concept; throwaway artifact; clear outcome statement Manager defines what the prototype must prove
04 PRD Manager Buildable spec; non-goals; success metrics; interface contracts Engineer reviews for buildability
05 Impl Plan Engineer M-sized steps; checkpoints; assumption section; plan traceable to PRD Manager reviews for PRD coverage
06 Execution Engineer (with Agent) Supervised autonomy; execution log; deviation types documented Manager monitors trust calibration; reads execution log
07 QA Manager Test traceability to PRD; verdicts; retrospective input Engineer runs tests; Agent helps write test cases

The impl plan is the engineer's phase, even when the agent does the work. The engineer owns the impl plan artifact. The agent is a tool that executes within the impl plan. The engineer reviews every step before it runs. This is not optional: the impl plan is where the engineer thinks through the architecture, and the agent executes it. If the engineer doesn't own the impl plan, the architecture is whatever the agent happened to choose.

The Loopback Pattern Detector

The most valuable skill in AI-driven development is recognizing the loopback before the loopback fires. The loopback pattern detector gives you early warning signals — symptoms that appear 1–2 phases before the loopback would be necessary. Catch the signal, fix the root cause, avoid the loopback.

Three signal tiers:

  • Early — 2 phases before loopback would fire. Actionable: investigate now.
  • Mid — 1 phase before loopback would fire. Actionable: escalate to phase owner.
  • Late — Loopback is necessary. Cannot be avoided; manage it well.
SignalTierIndicates loopback to...What to do now
Research evidence conflicts with itself or the idea Early Idea Refine the idea before writing the prototype spec
Prototype outcome is ambiguous — can't tell if it succeeded Early Research Redefine the prototype's success criteria; re-run
PRD has < 3 user outcomes or no non-goals Early Research Return to research; insufficient problem definition
Impl plan step cannot be traced to a PRD requirement Early PRD Add the missing PRD requirement; don't build without it
Impl plan step has > 3 assumptions listed Early Research Each untested assumption needs evidence before execution
Execution deviates more than twice on the same step Mid Impl Plan Pause execution; the plan step is wrong; revise before continuing
Agent asks the same question twice about the same step Mid Impl Plan The plan step isn't specific enough; clarify before agent continues
Execution log has a Type 3 deviation (wrong tool/approach) Mid Impl Plan Type 3 means the plan chose wrong approach; revisit before more steps run
QA cannot write an A-test for a PRD requirement Mid PRD PRD requirement is vague or non-testable; refine before verdict
QA finds missing PRD non-goals (behavior that should have been excluded) Late PRD (rollback) Feature was built but with wrong scope; rollback or fix depending on severity
QA verdict is rollback; root cause is in the PRD Late PRD Loopback to PRD; archive current artifact; start fresh
Feature ships; no users adopt it within 2 weeks Late Idea (kill) Loopback C — idea was wrong; not execution; research why before next iteration

The single most actionable rule from this lesson: When you see a Mid-tier signal, stop the current phase. Mid-tier signals mean the current phase is working with a flawed upstream artifact. Continuing costs more than stopping. The instinct is to push through. The discipline is to loop back early. The cost of a loopback at phase 3 is 10% of the cost of the same loopback at phase 7.

Which Phases Can Be Skipped?

Each phase has a skip cost — the probability-weighted cost of skipping it versus running it. Some phases have zero-skip situations: conditions under which skipping them is never acceptable.

PhaseCan skip if...Never skip when...
Idea Feature is a known commodity with established user problem (e.g., adding OAuth to a known user base) Feature is new to the team, the user, or the domain. "We've done this before" is not a valid skip condition.
Research Evidence exists from a prior related feature, and freshness date has not expired. The related feature's research must be traceable and dated. Any hard question in the idea phase was marked "unanswered." Research must answer it before PRD.
Prototype Technology is proven, user need is well-understood, team has done this exact thing before. Run prototype anyway if timeline allows — it's cheap insurance. First time the team is using a new framework, library, or architectural pattern in this domain. Prototype validates the approach, not just the idea.
PRD Feature is a minor change to an existing feature with a current PRD. Update the existing PRD rather than writing a new one. Any new user-facing behavior. Non-goals section is missing. Success metrics are missing. Interface contract is missing.
Impl Plan Change is < 1 day of work AND doesn't touch shared systems. Still write a 3-line plan: what, why, how to verify. Anything that touches auth, payments, data pipelines, or shared infrastructure. These require M-sized steps and checkpoints.
Execution Cannot skip. The feature doesn't exist without execution. "Auto-execute" is not a skip — it's running execution without a plan. —
QA Cannot skip. "Shipped without QA" is not a feature — it's a production incident waiting to happen. Even automated test runs count. —

The "We've done this before" trap. Teams that skip phases on familiar features tend to skip them on unfamiliar features too. The skip habit is contagious. If a phase is skippable on one feature, document why — and require explicit sign-off before skipping on the next similar feature. The documentation itself is the check.

Retrospective Taxonomy

Run a retrospective after every feature, regardless of outcome. The retrospective is not about blame — it's about pattern detection. Three categories of retrospective findings:

Category R1: What the process caught well

R1.1 — Loopback detected at which phase? Did it fire at the right time or later than optimal?
R1.2 — Which handoff failure modes appeared? Rate each: HF-01 through HF-07. Were any eliminated this iteration?
R1.3 — Which phase produced its best artifact yet? What made it good?
R1.4 — PRD non-goals: how many actual scope-creep items were caught by non-goals vs. slipped through?
R1.5 — Execution log quality: how many deviations were Type 1 / Type 2 / Type 3? Did the impl plan improve over time?

Category R2: What the process missed

R2.1 — Which PRD requirement was missing or wrong? Did QA catch it or did it reach production?
R2.2 — Which assumption was inherited without being flagged? What was the downstream cost?
R2.3 — Which evidence decayed between research and impl plan? How old was it?
R2.4 — Which phase-end gate was skipped or informal? What entered the next phase without being checked?
R2.5 — What was the cost of the worst loopback? Phase where it started, phase where it was detected, days of rework.

Category R3: Process improvements for next iteration

R3.1 — One artifact format change to make the next handoff cleaner (per HF-01 fix)
R3.2 — One assumption to always surface in the next artifact (per HF-02 fix)
R3.3 — One phase that needs a longer or shorter timeline next time
R3.4 — One retrospective finding to carry into the pattern library for the team

The retrospective is not done until R3 is written. R1 and R2 are observation. R3 is action. A retrospective without R3 is a debrief, not a retrospective. The R3 items become the pre-flight checklist for the next feature's Idea phase. The team's process improves by 1 item per retrospective — over time, that's a fundamentally better system.

The Process Maturity Ladder

Teams move through 5 levels of process maturity on this system. Most teams start at Level 2. Most teams believe they're at Level 4.

LevelNameBehavior
1 Ad-hoc No phases. Build fast, ship, see what breaks. No PRD, no plan, no QA. Production incidents are the QA.
2 Phases exist Team knows the phases. Artifacts exist but aren't consistent. Handoffs are verbal. Loopbacks are surprises.
3 Phases formalized Artifacts are written and deposited. Handoffs have a location rule. Loopbacks are detected but late.
4 Phases optimized Handoff failure modes are tracked. Loopback pattern detector runs proactively. Retrospectives produce R3 items.
5 Phases self-correcting Process learns from each iteration. R3 items from retrospectives are implemented before the next feature starts. HF-01 and HF-02 are eliminated. Loopbacks fire early.

Level 3 is where most "mature" teams believe they are. Level 5 is where the 7-phase system is a genuine competitive advantage — not because the phases run, but because the feedback loop between phases runs faster than the team's competitors can ship features.

What the 7-Phase System Looks Like at Level 5

Level 5: The feature that looped back early and shipped on time

The team starts a feature. Idea is written. Research answers 3 of 4 hard questions. The 4th — "will this approach scale to 10x current load?" — is answered with "we think so, prototype will tell us."

Prototype runs. The early signal (Mid-tier in the loopback detector) fires: "Prototype outcome is ambiguous — can't tell if it succeeded." The team doesn't push through. They return to Research with a precise question: "Does this approach scale to 10x?"

Research answers: "No, not without a caching layer." The idea is refined: add caching layer to scope. Research loops back to Idea; Idea loops forward to Research; PRD is written with the caching layer as a non-goal for v1.

Impl Plan includes the caching layer decision. Execution runs. QA runs. Ship date is 3 days later than the Level 2 version of this team would have shipped — because the Level 2 team would have shipped, discovered the scale problem in production, and spent 2 weeks on incident response.

The Level 5 team shipped on time with the right scope. The loopback saved them, not cost them.

Final note on cross-phase integration. The 7 phases are not a waterfall. They are a system with feedback loops. The most important feedback loop is the loopback — the mechanism by which the system corrects itself. Teams that fear loopbacks have a quality problem. Teams that expect loopbacks have a process. The goal is not to eliminate loopbacks. The goal is to catch them early enough that they cost nothing.
