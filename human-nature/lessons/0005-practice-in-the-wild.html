Practice in the Wild

L5 Practice · ~18 min · Lesson 5 of the Human Nature meta-skill series

L5 is the closing lesson. No new levers, no new quizzes. Three real ads, each read through the full five-lever framework, with the honest verdicts about what each one does well and where the ethics seam is showing.

The point of the series was always recognition, not persuasion. L5 is where that distinction gets tested: the framework should let you read persuasion with clearer eyes, not turn you into a better persuader. The three ads below are chosen to make that visible — one good, one mixed, one where the seams are showing.

How to read this lesson
For each ad, do this in order:
  1. Read the ad.
  2. Score each lever (1–5) using the rubric below.
  3. Note where the ethics seam is showing, if anywhere.
  4. Compare your read to the worked diagnostic.
The diagnostics are mine — they could be wrong, and if your read differs, that's the lesson.

The scoring rubric

For each lever, score 1–5. A 5 means the lever is being used well — it would land on a careful reader. A 1 means the lever is missing or so clumsy it would actively repel that same reader.

  • 5 — Skilled. The lever fires cleanly. The careful reader feels the tension the writer intended, in the way the writer intended.
  • 3 — Mixed. The lever is partially present; some readers feel it, others don't, or the lever sends mixed signals.
  • 1 — Missing or broken. The lever is absent, or present in a way that would lose a careful reader's trust.

Ethics-seam notes are separate. A lever can score a 5 and still have the seam showing — because the lever is well-executed but the promise it serves can't be backed up.

Ad 1: Basecamp — "We're not for everyone"

Source: Basecamp product pages (basecamp.com), in particular the "Why Basecamp?" section. Verifiable independently as of mid-2025.

Basecamp is built for small teams who want less software, not more. If you're after a tool with 200 integrations and a dedicated success manager, look elsewhere — we're going to keep saying no to those things. We make software for the way small teams actually work.

Worked diagnostic

  • L1 Name the Threat — 3. The threat is implicit: "more software" is the unnamed enemy. Strong enough for a problem-aware reader; weak for an unaware one. Schwartz Level 2–3 target, not Level 1.
  • L2 Mirror the Identity — 4. "Small teams who want less software" is specific. Anyone who's felt drowned in tooling recognizes the description. The mirror is honest and verifiable.
  • L3 Exclude People — 5. The classic honest exclusion. The exclusion names a real trade-off Basecamp has committed to (no 200 integrations, no success managers), and the excluded reader can verify the exclusion against the product itself. This is the L3 litmus test passing clearly.
  • L4 Paint the Transformation — 3. "The way small teams actually work" gestures at a future but doesn't paint it sensorily. A careful reader can imagine it but doesn't get the neural simulation firing. Acceptable for a category-leader page; would be a 2 for a new entrant.
  • L5 First Step — 4. The page's implicit CTA ("try Basecamp") has a real first step behind it: the free trial, no credit card, a workspace you can set up in ten minutes. The first step exists; it just isn't written as a specific step in this copy.
Verdict
Strong, honest stack. The L3 lever is the standout — it's the textbook honest exclusion, and it's the move that makes the rest of the page land for the right reader. The L4 lever is the weakness, but Basecamp has earned enough trust over twenty years that the L4 deficit doesn't cost them the read. Ethics seam: clean. The exclusion is real, the transformation is modest, the first step is verifiable. Nothing to call out.

Ad 2: A $2,000 freelance course landing page (representative)

Source: representative of the "Become a 6-figure freelancer" genre common in 2024–2025 cohort-based course launches. The diagnostic applies to the genre, not a specific named course. The structural patterns are stable across many examples.

Most freelancers are stuck at $5k/month and don't know why. Six months from now, you could be at $20k/month — booked solid, raising rates, turning clients away. This is the system that got me there. It's the same one my students use to hit $30k/month in under a year. Book a free call to see if you qualify.

Worked diagnostic

  • L1 Name the Threat — 4. "Stuck at $5k/month and don't know why" is a sharp problem-aware threat. The reader who matches it feels addressed immediately. Loses a point for not naming the cause — "and don't know why" is doing the work of a real diagnosis that the copy doesn't supply.
  • L2 Mirror the Identity — 3. Implicit. The mirror is "freelancer who's serious about their career" — broad enough to catch a lot of readers, narrow enough to feel specific. Could be sharper.
  • L3 Exclude People — 1. Missing. The page wants to maximize the addressable audience; there's no honest exclusion anywhere. The "see if you qualify" framing gestures at one but inverts the move: instead of saying who the offer isn't for, it says the offer might not be for you. That's flattery, not exclusion.
  • L4 Paint the Transformation — 5. "Booked solid, raising rates, turning clients away" — three sensory beats in one sentence. The neural simulation fires cleanly. This is where the page is doing its real work, and the writer knows it.
  • L5 First Step — 1. "Book a free call to see if you qualify." A free call is a non-step — it's a commitment to consider, not a step into the transformation. The page asks the reader to invest time in a sales conversation before producing any visible state-change. This is the lever that fails the three-criteria test on all three counts.
Verdict — ethics seam showing
The L4 lever is excellent. The L5 lever is a non-step that hides the L4 promise behind a sales conversation the reader has to survive to evaluate. The combination is the canonical pattern L4's ethics-seam section warned about: a strong L4 with a vague L5 is a signal that the L4 promise can't survive a real first step. If a course could honestly produce "$20k/month in six months," its first step would not be a sales call — it would be something specific the reader could do in ten minutes that produces visible state-change. The seam is showing.

Ad 3: A clear, decent newsletter welcome

Source: representative of the "weekly essay-style" newsletter genre on Substack and similar platforms. The diagnostic applies to the genre, not a specific named newsletter. The structural patterns are stable across many examples.

You're getting this email because you signed up for [Newsletter]. Every Tuesday I send a 5-minute essay on one idea I've been thinking about — usually something between technology, work, and attention. Last month: why meetings fill calendars, what autocomplete is doing to writing, the case for fewer tools. Next Tuesday: why most "productivity" advice fails within six weeks.

Worked diagnostic

  • L1 Name the Threat — 2. The threat is implicit in the topic list ("autocomplete is doing to writing," "most productivity advice fails") but the welcome email doesn't press any single one. The threat is bibliographic, not felt. The reader is being told what the writer thinks about, not what they should worry about.
  • L2 Mirror the Identity — 5. "If you signed up for this, you already match." The mirror is the act of subscribing. Every reader of the welcome email is, by definition, the mirrored reader. The lever fires at maximum strength.
  • L3 Exclude People — 1. Missing. The email is trying to keep everyone on the list. No honest exclusion. For a welcome email, this is fine — exclusion is a wrong move at this stage of the relationship. But it's worth naming the absence.
  • L4 Paint the Transformation — 3. "5-minute essay on one idea I've been thinking about" is a soft future: a weekly ritual, intellectual companionship. The neural simulation is mild because the transformation is small. A 3 is appropriate for the genre — most newsletter welcomes don't over-promise, and the lower L4 score reflects honest restraint, not weakness.
  • L5 First Step — 4. "Next Tuesday: why most 'productivity' advice fails within six weeks" is a real first step. The reader has a specific thing to do (read next Tuesday's essay) and a specific payoff (understand a claim they're being primed to want to understand). The lever fires cleanly without overclaiming.
Verdict
A clean, decent welcome email. The L2 lever is the standout — the act of subscribing is the mirror, and the writer makes that explicit without belaboring it. The L5 is unusually well-formed for the genre: a specific future essay with a specific claim. The L1 is the weakness, but a welcome email doesn't need to press a threat. Ethics seam: clean. The promise is small and specific; the first step is real.

What the three diagnostics tell you about the framework

Each ad had different strengths and weaknesses. None of them used all five levers well. That's the framework's first lesson when you start applying it to real work: no real ad is a perfect five-out-of-five stack. Real writers are constrained by context, brand, audience, channel. The framework is a diagnostic tool, not a checklist to optimize against.

The framework's second lesson is that the ethics seam is diagnostic too. Ad 1 and Ad 3 had clean seams. Ad 2's seam was showing — and the seam was visible from the L5 alone, before you had to trust or distrust the L4. You can spot the seam from the first-step test. A non-step L5 next to a strong L4 is the canonical dishonest-pattern signature.

What the framework is for

  • Reading persuasion with clearer eyes. You can see the levers in real copy now. That's recognition.
  • Finding the seam. When a transformation promise feels too good, you can check the first step and see whether it's specific enough to expose the promise, or vague enough to hide it.
  • Writing your own copy more honestly. You can run your own L4 against your own L5 and see whether they fit. This is the persuasion skill, and it's a useful skill — but the workspace teaches recognition and honesty, not persuasion for its own sake.

What the framework is not for

  • Manipulation. The five levers are neutral techniques. Using them to extract value from a reader the levers have hoodwinked is exploitation, not persuasion. The workspace's claim has always been recognition — and the recognition runs in both directions.
  • Optimization. The framework doesn't tell you that more levers is better. Ad 1 used four levers well; Ad 2 used two levers well and one catastrophically; Ad 3 used two levers well and three were appropriately absent. A real stack is a real judgment, not an optimization problem.
  • Replacement for taste. A lever can score 5 and still be wrong for the brand. Basecamp's L3 lands because it's Basecamp; the same exclusion would land differently from a startup trying to look bigger than it is. Framework plus taste; never framework alone.

Final exercise: run the framework on something you wrote

The closing exercise isn't a quiz. It's an invitation.

The exercise
  1. Pick something you wrote in the last month — a Substack post, a launch email, a product page, a tweet thread. Something you're willing to look at with new eyes.
  2. Score each of the five levers 1–5, in the same format as the diagnostics above.
  3. Note the ethics-seam status. If you're not sure, look at the L5 specifically — is it a real first step, or a non-step?
  4. Find the single highest-leverage change. Not the lowest score; the highest-leverage change. That's usually the lever whose improvement would do the most work for the least effort.
  5. Make that change. Read the result with the framework again.

That's the full loop. Recognition, then application. The workspace's mission — recognition, not persuasion — completes here, in the act of looking at your own work and seeing the levers clearly enough to know which one to pull next.
