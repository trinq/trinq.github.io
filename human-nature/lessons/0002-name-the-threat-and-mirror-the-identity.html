Name the Threat + Mirror the Identity

L2 Techniques · ~13 min · Lesson 2 of the Human Nature meta-skill series

L1 taught you to see the three tensions — Survival, Identity, Progress — in any piece of content. L2 teaches the first two of the five levers that press them. Both levers are about awareness: making the reader see themselves in a picture you've drawn. That's why both target the moment before the message lands.

Lever 1: Name the Threat

Name the Threat is the lever for pulling Survival Tension. You do it by describing a threat the reader already half-senses — and naming it more precisely than they could on their own. The mind rewards precise threat-naming with attention, because the wiring evolved to do exactly this.

Notice the order: name the threat before you offer the solution. Reversed order kills the lever. "Our product stops data leaks — buy now" is a feature. "Your data leaks every week and you don't see it — our product stops this" is the lever.

Schwartz's 5 levels of awareness

Eugene Schwartz named this lever in *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966). Before you can name a threat, you have to know how aware the reader is of it. Schwartz split awareness into five levels:

Level What the reader knows What the lever does
1. Unaware Doesn't even recognize they have the problem You can't name the threat — they have no map for it. You have to create the problem first. Anything else bounces off.
2. Problem-Aware Knows the problem exists, doesn't know solutions Name the threat precisely. This is the lever's sweet spot. Most successful ads live here.
3. Solution-Aware Knows solutions exist, doesn't know yours The threat is already in the reader's head. You name a sharper version of it, then move quickly to mechanism.
4. Product-Aware Knows your product, hasn't decided Threat-naming here feels pushy. Switch to Lever 4 (Paint the Transformation) or Lever 5 (Give the First Step).
5. Most Aware Knows your product, ready to buy Don't name any threat — they already feel it. Just close. "Enroll now."
Common mistake
Writing for Level 2 (Problem-Aware) when your reader is actually Level 1 (Unaware). The threat names bounce off — the reader thinks "what threat?" and the message fails. First diagnose the level, then pull the lever.

Worked example — threat named, then threat missed

Reads as Level 2 (good):

Your laptop fan has been spinning harder every month. You thought it was the apps — but it's the OS quietly indexing your files for ad auctions in the background. Every minute it runs, your battery dies faster.

The reader feels the symptom (fan spinning). The threat is named precisely (OS indexing for ad auctions). The reader can now verify the threat against their own laptop. That's the lever working.

Reads as Level 1 (the lever fails):

Your laptop is being used to track you.

True, but the reader has no map for the threat. There's no symptom to verify against, no mechanism to anchor. It sounds like a conspiracy post. The lever skipped a step.

Lever 2: Mirror the Identity

Mirror the Identity is the lever for pulling Identity Tension. You do it by describing a person the reader already is — in language they would use to describe themselves. The reader's mind recognizes itself in the description and locks in.

This is different from flattering the reader. Flattery says "you're amazing" — the reader is suspicious. Mirror says "you've noticed X and Y, and you're the kind of person who notices things like that." That's specific, verifiable, and far more powerful.

The "If you're..." opener

The most common mirror pattern is "If you're...":

  • "If you've ever started a SaaS and felt the launch was the easy part..."
  • "If you're the person who keeps a Notion template for everything..."
  • "If you find yourself rereading your own code from a year ago and cringing..."

Each of these mirrors a specific kind of person. People who match the description think "yes, that's me, keep going". People who don't match it scroll past — and that's fine. Mirror narrows attention on purpose.

The danger of vague mirrors
"If you're a high achiever..." mirrors nobody. The reader can't verify the description against themselves, so the lever doesn't fire. Mirrors work when they're specific enough to exclude someone. If your mirror could fit everyone, it's a flattery, not a mirror.

Worked example — mirror that works, mirror that doesn't

Specific, verifiable (works):

If you've got 14 tabs open and three of them are a doc you keep rewriting because the structure still feels wrong — you're not procrastinating, you're drafting.

The mirror names tab count, doc, rewriting, structure — all things the reader can verify against their own screen. Reader locks in.

Vague (fails):

If you're a creator who's always looking to grow...

Every creator matches this. There's nothing to verify against. The mirror is a flattery in disguise.

Why these two go together

Name the Threat and Mirror the Identity target the same step of the reader's experience — the moment of attention. Threat says "this matters". Mirror says "this is for someone like you". Used together, they hand the reader two reasons to keep reading. Used alone, each works; together, they're the standard opening of most persuasive ads.

This is also why the two levers are in the same lesson, not separate ones. In practice you'll rarely pull one without the other.

Live exercise: rewrite a flat announcement

Here's a flat announcement — the kind your company might send, the kind your newsletter opens with, the kind you almost always scroll past. Your job is to rewrite it by pulling exactly the two levers from this lesson.

The original
"We just shipped v2 of our analytics dashboard. New charts, faster queries, dark mode. Try it out."

  1. Diagnose the awareness level

    Existing users are Level 4–5 (Product-Aware / Most Aware). New prospects are Level 2 (Problem-Aware) at best, often Level 1 (Unaware). Pick an audience — and stick with them.

  2. Name a threat they'd recognize

    For existing users: "Your dashboards have been getting slower as your data has grown, and you've started opening two tabs to compare numbers side by side." For new prospects: "You're making decisions on data that's already two days stale by the time you look at it."

  3. Mirror an identity

    Existing users: "If you've started keeping a side spreadsheet because the dashboard's filters aren't enough..." New prospects: "If you've ever caught yourself exporting to CSV just to slice one chart differently..."

  4. Stack them in one paragraph

    Threat first, then mirror, then the announcement. Three lines. Pick whichever order feels less like an ad to your specific reader.

Example rewrite (existing users):

Your dashboards have been getting slower as your data has grown — and if you've started keeping a side spreadsheet just to slice one chart differently, you know the pain. v2 ships faster queries, the filters you were about to build yourself, and dark mode because you've been complaining. It's live now.

Compare that to "We just shipped v2 of our analytics dashboard." Same news. Different lever stack. The reader who matches the mirror will read the rest of the message.
