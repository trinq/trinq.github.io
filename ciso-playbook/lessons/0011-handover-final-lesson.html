Lesson 0011 · CISO playbook · Arc 3: The handover that outlives the CISO

Handover — The Final Lesson

The CISO role is a stewardship. The handover package is what you leave behind. Build it as if you will not be there tomorrow — because one day, you will not.

Why this lesson eleventh

Lessons 0001–0010 built the substantive content + the strategic plan. Lesson 0011 builds the discipline that makes the programme survive the CISO's departure. Every CISO leaves — promotion, lateral move, retirement, contract end, the unexpected. The handover package is the difference between a programme that continues to mature and a programme that resets to zero.

Chapter 18 closes Volume 2 with a thematic summary, but the substantive content of handover lives across the volume — the Stamper hospital scenario in Chapter 16 (the new CISO who inherited nothing), the strategic plan in Chapter 18 (the artefact that organises the function), and the recurring "document the obvious" discipline from lesson 0007. Lesson 0011 integrates these threads into the handover discipline.

For your 9-entity VN securities firm, four specific weights. First, the average CISO tenure in financial services is 24–36 months — shorter than the strategic-plan horizon. The handover discipline must work on a 12-month turnover cycle. Second, the UBCK examiner expects continuity of programme — a programme that visibly resets after each CISO departure is a programme the examiner will examine more closely. Third, the institutional knowledge (who at UBCK, who at MSSP, who at peer firms, who at insurance carrier, which board member is supportive, which is hostile) is the CISO's most under-appreciated asset — and the most easily lost. Fourth, the relationships with security team members matter for retention — a CISO who disappears without handover damages the team he or she built.

By the end of this lesson you will have drafted your handover package — in Vietnamese — covering the institutional knowledge transfer, the relationship transfer, the documentation audit, the 30-60-90 transition plan, and the post-departure support window.

The handover philosophy: stewardship, not ownership

The handover philosophy that makes 0011 work: the CISO role is a stewardship, not an ownership. The programme belongs to the firm, not to the individual. The institutional knowledge belongs to the firm. The relationships belong to the firm (with the CISO as the temporary custodian). The handover is the act of returning these to the firm in a form the next custodian can use.

The handover that is built with this philosophy has three characteristics:

  1. Built continuously, not at the end. The handover package is updated quarterly alongside the strategic plan tracker. By the time the CISO announces departure, the handover package is 90% complete.
  2. Designed for the next CISO, not for the current one. The handover package is written as if the reader has zero institutional knowledge of the firm, the regulator, the MSSP, the peers. The current CISO cannot imagine being that reader — but the next CISO will be.
  3. Contains the unglamorous. The handover package is not just strategic plan + policy framework + playbook. It is also: which office has the good coffee, which executive assistant actually knows how to book a board meeting, which UBCK examiner is friendly to clarification questions, which MSSP engineer answers the phone on weekends, which board member asks the hardest questions and why, which entity-level CISO is most reliable and which is most at risk.
Stamper, CISO DRG Vol. 2 Ch. 16 These "hypothetical" dynamics likely resonate with a number of us who have worked in healthcare or provided services to this sector. Just to add to the tension, you've been in your role less than a month. The former head of security left under less-than-ideal circumstances. It's now your department. You own it.

Stamper's hospital scenario in Chapter 16 is the handover-from-hell: new CISO, no playbook, no documentation, FBI at the door, predecessor departed under "less-than-ideal circumstances." The lesson: this is what happens when the handover discipline fails. The fix: lesson 0007's "document the obvious" discipline + lesson 0010's strategic plan + this lesson's handover package together ensure the next CISO inherits a functioning programme.

The 8-component handover package

For your firm, the handover package has 8 components. Each is updated on a defined cadence during the CISO's tenure, not assembled in a panic in the final week.

  1. Programme overview — the strategic plan + project schedule. From lesson 0010. What the function is, where it is going, who is accountable for what.
  2. Documentation index — the inventory of policies, procedures, playbooks, runbooks. Where they live, who owns each, last-review date, next-review date.
  3. Relationship map — the institutional knowledge. UBCK examiner contact, MSSP relationship owner, peer-CISO network, board allies, internal stakeholders, external vendors, law enforcement contacts.
  4. Operational status — the day-to-day running of the function. Active incidents, open initiatives, upcoming audits, pending regulatory matters, scheduled tabletop exercises.
  5. Risk register — the current view of where the function is exposed. Top 10 risks, owners, mitigation status.
  6. Team & talent map — the people. Org chart, key personnel, retention risk, succession candidates, hiring plans.
  7. Budget & vendor map — the money. Current fiscal year budget, vendor contracts, insurance carrier, renewal calendar.
  8. Personal transition plan — the 30-60-90 transition. What the CISO will hand over when, who the successor will meet with when, what the post-departure support window looks like.

For your firm, components 1–7 are updated quarterly. Component 8 is updated when departure is announced. The package is stored in the CISO's personal handover folder + the firm's secure document repository (encrypted, WORM-backed, access-controlled).

The "document the obvious" discipline — the Stamper thread

Lesson 0007 introduced Stamper's "document the obvious" discipline (the RACI matrix in the runbook). Lesson 0011 elevates this discipline from a lesson-0007 tactical practice to a handover philosophy: the things that are obvious to the current CISO are the things that will be unknown to the next one.

For the handover package, the "document the obvious" categories:

  • Vendor contacts — who actually answers the phone. Not the published account manager. The actual engineer who picks up at 2 AM on a Saturday.
  • Regulator contacts — who is approachable. Not the published examiner. The deputy who responds to clarification emails within 24 hours.
  • Internal politics — which meetings matter. Not the published organisational chart. Which weekly meeting is where the real decisions get made.
  • Board dynamics — who asks the hard questions. Not the published audit committee charter. Which director will read the strategic plan cover-to-cover and which will skim the executive summary.
  • Team dynamics — who is at risk of leaving. Not the published org chart. Which engineer is being recruited by the competitor and which is loyal to the firm.
  • Taboo topics — what not to raise with whom. Not the published stakeholder list. Which topic will trigger the CIO's defensiveness, which will trigger the CFO's silence.
Documenting the obvious is the discipline that distinguishes the good handover from the bad. The good handover assumes the reader knows nothing about the firm's specific context. The bad handover assumes the reader can read minds. Every "obvious" thing that the current CISO has not written down is a thing the next CISO will spend the first 90 days discovering — the worst possible use of the first 90 days.

The 30-60-90 transition plan

When the CISO announces departure, the 30-60-90 transition plan kicks in. The plan has three phases:

Days 1–30: Orientation

  • Day 1–7: Successor CISO (or interim) receives the handover package. Walks through each component with the departing CISO. Asks questions. Documents gaps.
  • Day 8–14: Successor meets the key internal stakeholders (CEO, CIO, Head of Risk, Head of Compliance, Audit Committee chair, board sponsor). Departing CISO introduces in person where possible.
  • Day 15–21: Successor meets the key external stakeholders (UBCK examiner, MSSP account manager, peer CISOs, insurance broker). Departing CISO introduces where possible.
  • Day 22–30: Successor attends the next regularly scheduled governance meetings (Strategic Cybersecurity Committee, Audit Committee briefing, UBCK update if scheduled). Observes, does not lead.

Days 31–60: Shadowing + early leadership

  • Day 31–45: Successor shadows the departing CISO in all operational activities. Incident response (if any), tabletop exercise (if scheduled), MSSP quarterly review, vendor management meeting.
  • Day 46–60: Successor begins leading selected activities. Departing CISO remains available but not in the lead. Strategic plan updates begin.

Days 61–90: Departing CISO exits; successor takes the lead

  • Day 61–75: Departing CISO available on call (defined hours). Successor leads all activities. Hand-off to MSSP and external stakeholders completed.
  • Day 76–90: Departing CISO reduces availability to on-request basis. Successor fully operational. Final transition review with CEO.

The post-departure support window

The handover does not end at day 90. For your firm, the practical post-departure support window is 6 months:

  • Months 1–3: Weekly check-in (1 hour, video call). Departing CISO available for questions. No commitments beyond the agreed hours.
  • Months 4–6: Monthly check-in (1 hour, video call). Departing CISO available on request. Focus on the strategic decisions the successor is facing.
  • After month 6: Departing CISO available on request, at standard consulting rates, with explicit pre-agreement. No informal obligation.

The post-departure support window is the safety net. The successor inherits the programme; the post-departure support is the parachute if something the handover package missed surfaces in the first months.

The relationship transfer — the most under-appreciated handover

The relationship transfer is the most under-appreciated part of the handover. The CISO has invested years in building relationships — with the UBCK examiner, with the MSSP account team, with the peer-CISO network, with internal stakeholders. These relationships do not transfer automatically. They transfer by deliberate introduction.

For your firm, the relationship transfer checklist:

The relationship transfer that is rushed produces a CISO who is "in the role" but not "in the network." The relationship transfer that is skipped produces a CISO who has to rebuild the network from scratch — the worst possible use of the first 6 months.

The documentation audit — the handover-quality check

Before departure, the CISO runs a documentation audit. The audit asks: if I were hit by a bus tomorrow, what would the next CISO not be able to figure out from the documentation?

For your firm, the documentation audit categories:

  1. Strategic plan + project schedule. (Lesson 0010) — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  2. Policy framework + standards. — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  3. BIA + risk register. (Lesson 0006) — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  4. IR playbook + runbooks. (Lesson 0007) — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  5. Recovery playbook + persistence checklist. (Lesson 0008) — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  6. Forensics + post-mortem template. (Lesson 0009) — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  7. Awareness training + phishing simulation results. (Lesson 0003) — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  8. Monitoring + metrics dashboard. (Lesson 0004) — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  9. Threat intel programme + peer network map. (Lesson 0005) — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  10. Vendor contracts + service-level expectations. — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  11. Cyber insurance policy + panel counsel. — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  12. Taboo topics + internal politics notes. — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?
  13. Team + talent + retention risk map. — Current state? Yes/No. Gaps?

The audit produces a gap list. Each gap is closed before departure (or explicitly documented as a known gap with a recommended approach to closure).

The handover package as an audit-ready artefact

The handover package is also an audit-ready artefact. The UBCK examiner may ask: "Show me the documentation that demonstrates the security function operates continuously regardless of personnel changes." The handover package is the answer.

For your firm, the audit-ready characteristics:

  • Versioned. Each component has a version number + last-update date + next-update date.
  • Stored in a controlled location. Encrypted at rest, access-controlled, backed up.
  • Reviewed by Audit Committee. Annual review ensures the package is current.
  • Aligned with UBCK expectations. Maps to UBCK's ICT risk management documentation requirements.

The handover in the absence of a successor

The most common handover scenario is not the planned succession but the unexpected departure — the CISO resigns, is terminated, is incapacitated, or simply disappears. For your firm, the handover package must be designed to function in this scenario as well.

The successor-in-absence playbook:

  1. Interim CISO appointed within 7 days. Internal candidate (CIO, Head of IT, Head of Risk) or external interim.
  2. Handover package provided to interim CISO. Component 8 (personal transition plan) is replaced by a "handover-in-absence" annex.
  3. 30-day stabilisation period. Interim CISO stabilises the function. No strategic changes. No vendor changes. No team changes.
  4. Permanent CISO search initiated by day 30. External search firm engaged. Internal candidate evaluated.
  5. Permanent CISO onboarded by day 90–120. 30-60-90 transition plan resumes (without the departing CISO's involvement).

The handover-in-absence scenario is the test of whether the handover package has been built continuously. If the package is 90% complete at the moment of unexpected departure, the stabilisation period works. If the package is 10% complete, the stabilisation period does not work.

The handover as a programme-management discipline

The most important reframing in this lesson: the handover is not an end-of-ten activity. It is a programme-management discipline that runs throughout the CISO's tenure.

For your firm, the discipline:

  • Quarterly review: Each component is reviewed for currency. Gaps documented. Updates made.
  • Annual audit: Audit Committee reviews the package as part of the annual governance review.
  • Tabletop drill: Once per year, assume the CISO is unavailable for 30 days. Can the function operate? What breaks?
  • Succession planning: Identify potential successors (internal candidates, peer-CISO contacts who could serve as interim). Build relationships with them.

Self-check

Why is the handover a programme-management discipline, not an end-of-ten activity?

Because the handover package that is built continuously (updated quarterly, reviewed annually, audited periodically) is the package that works when the CISO departs unexpectedly. The handover package that is built in the final week is the package that produces a Stamper-style hospital scenario for the successor: no playbook, no documentation, FBI at the door. The handover discipline throughout the CISO's tenure is what closes the gap. Every quarterly review of the 8 components is a deposit in the handover package. The CISO who builds the package continuously is the CISO who leaves behind a programme, not a pile of documents.
