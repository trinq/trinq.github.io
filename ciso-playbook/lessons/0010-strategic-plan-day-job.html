Lesson 0010 · CISO playbook · Arc 3: The ongoing operational programme

Strategic Plan — The Day Job

The plan that turns the 17 chapters of work into 1 page the board can read, the UBCK examiner can audit, and the next CISO can inherit.

Why this lesson tenth

Lessons 0001–0009 built the substantive content of the CISO playbook. Lesson 0010 builds the strategic plan that organises all of it — the 1-page-per-objective + initiatives + owners + dates document that operates as the day-to-day manual of the security function.

Chapter 18 is the closing chapter of Volume 2. The framing: "the last key action for the reader to take is to build a strategic cybersecurity plan. We've emphasised that cybersecurity is a team sport, and we come back again to your human network." The plan is not the strategy. The plan is the artefact that documents the strategy so that the board, the executive team, the UBCK examiner, the cyber-insurance carrier, the next CISO, and the security team itself all know what the CISO is doing, why, and when.

For your 9-entity VN securities firm, three specific weights. First, the UBCK expects a documented security strategy as part of the ICT risk management requirements — the strategic plan is the artefact that demonstrates compliance. Second, the board needs a 1-page-per-objective view of what security is doing — not a 300-page strategy document nobody reads. Third, the strategic plan is what makes the security function defensible against budget cuts — the CISO can point to specific objectives, specific initiatives, specific dates, specific outcomes, and ask "which of these do you want to cut?"

By the end of this lesson you will have drafted your firm's cybersecurity strategic plan — in Vietnamese — with mission, vision, governance, SWOT analysis, strategic objectives, key initiatives, and the build-vs-outsource matrix.

The strategic plan structure — the 6 sections

Bonney, Hayslip, and Stamper converge on a single recommended structure. 6 sections, each with a specific purpose:

  1. Mission Statement. Declaration of the organisation's core purpose. Does not change over time.
  2. Vision Statement. Aspirational description of what the organisation would like to achieve in the mid-term or long-term future.
  3. Introduction. Statement describing the business and the environment in which the security program operates. Executive leadership uses this to communicate the cybersecurity program's critical role.
  4. Governance. How the strategic plan will be implemented. Who audits the process. Which committees or personnel assess effectiveness and recommend changes.
  5. Strategic Objectives. How the cybersecurity organisation invests time and resources to manage the security risks discovered in the assessment and SWOT data. Arrayed over 1–3 years. Each objective has initiatives derived from security-gap data.
  6. Key Initiatives. Each initiative states which objectives it satisfies, what security/risk issues it alleviates, and the benefits it brings to the business.

For your firm, the structure adapts to the multi-entity reality: the strategic plan at the group level + entity-level tactical plans that roll up into the group plan. The group's mission/vision/governance/strategic objectives are common. The entity-level initiatives reflect the specific risk profile of each entity.

The plan is concise, not encyclopedic

Bonney's most distinctive contribution is the brevity discipline: "The cybersecurity strategic plan needs to be concise and easy to understand and reflect realistic expectations for funding that are in line with what the organisation can afford. The plan document is not the place to surface a 300% increase in funding. That is a discussion that should already have taken place between you and the management team and, as appropriate, the board."

For your firm, the practical implication: the strategic plan is a 5–15 page document, not a 50-page document. The board reads the executive summary (1 page) + the strategic objectives table (1 page). The executive team reads the full plan. The security team uses the plan + project schedule as the operating manual.

The SWOT analysis — the discovery input

Bonney recommends a Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats analysis as the discovery input for the strategic plan. The 7 areas the SWOT should cover:

  1. Current capacity to deliver security services.
  2. Core competencies — what the security program is doing well.
  3. Business processes the security program supports.
  4. Staffing roles, skills, knowledge strengths and shortfalls.
  5. Currently deployed security assets.
  6. Effective and ineffective security controls.
  7. Financial resources the program currently has available.
The 9 lessons produce the SWOT inputs. Lessons 0001–0009 have each produced a Vietnamese artefact that maps directly to a SWOT category: lesson 0001 (first 90 days plan → financial resources); lesson 0002 (blended-capability team → staffing + competencies); lesson 0003 (awareness one-pager → business processes supported); lesson 0004 (monitoring one-pager → security assets + effective controls); lesson 0005 (threat intel programme → core competencies); lesson 0006 (BIA matrix → business processes supported); lesson 0007 (IR playbook → effective controls + gaps); lesson 0008 (recovery playbook → financial resources + gaps); lesson 0009 (forensics + post-mortem playbook → core competencies + gaps). Aggregating these 9 artefacts produces the SWOT.

Moore's core-vs-context matrix — the build-vs-outsource tool

Bonney references Geoffrey Moore's mission-critical "core vs context" matrix (Volume 1 Chapter 10). The matrix helps group capabilities along two axes: mission-critical vs commodity, and core competency vs outsourced.

Mission-criticalCommodity
Core competency Build & retain in-house (e.g., security strategy, BIA ownership, IR leadership) Differentiate selectively (e.g., threat intel specific to your industry)
Not core Strategic partnership (e.g., 24/7 SOC, MSSP for commodity monitoring) Outsource to commodity provider (e.g., log retention, vulnerability scanning)

For your firm, the practical use: walk through each capability in the security function and place it on the matrix. Mission-critical + not-core = strategic partnership (vendor relationship management becomes the in-house competency). Commodity + not-core = outsource to MSSP. Mission-critical + core = build and retain in-house.

The build-from-scratch playbook

Bonney's most operationally distinctive contribution is the build-from-scratch playbook for the new CISO with no programme:

  1. Inventory required security functions. Use the lessons 0001–0009 artefacts to determine what needs to be protected and what is already in place.
  2. Look internally for overlooked talent. Many systems and network administrators are "chomping at the bit to move into cybersecurity." Coordinate with CIO — do not poach. HR facilitates training.
  3. Audit the existing security architecture. Where are the blind spots? Can you meet regulatory and contractual obligations with current controls?
  4. Surface regulatory exposure directly to C-suite. "If not, surface this concern directly to your colleagues in the C-suite and get their acknowledgment that the organisation has significant regulatory exposure and cannot meet contractual obligations."
  5. Apply the "what cannot I see?" question. "What is it that I cannot see that I should see that will allow me to protect my organisation better?" Take a full-stack, process-driven view. Leverage data flow diagrams for material processes.
  6. Prioritise where to implement new technology. Knowing which business processes require the most protection drives this prioritisation.
  7. Establish cadence of program reviews with executive management. Security metrics tied to business + organisational initiatives. Validate data with stakeholders + trusted partner.

The outsource playbook — MSSP due diligence

Bonney's outsource playbook is the most operationally detailed in the chapter. The principle: "Validate everything. Leave nothing to chance. The selected MSSP will have a huge impact on the credibility and effectiveness of your security program."

  1. Begin with the BIA. Know which processes are material. Use the core-vs-context matrix to determine which security processes can be safely outsourced.
  2. Expect organisational momentum to outsource. "You will likely experience significant organisational momentum to outsource key elements of your security program (e.g., the use of a managed security services provider or an MSSP)."
  3. Work with candidate MSSPs during evaluation. Review tools and services. How will the service be staffed? Communications protocols? Measurements?
  4. If it isn't spelled out in the contract, it doesn't get done. "If it isn't spelled out in the service contract, it doesn't get done without a change order. Change orders are where service providers make their profit." Have a trusted third party review the service contract before signing.
  5. Visit the MSSP's SOC after hours. "If the services require 7x24 monitoring and support, validate services and capabilities by going to the MSSP's SOC after hours, preferably late at night or early in the morning."
  6. Ask the two questions. "How will I know they are delivering as contracted? And how can I take this process back in-house if I need to?"
Bonney, CISO DRG Vol. 2 Ch. 18 In a scenario where outsourcing has been the primary modus operandi of the organisation, you may also have inherited significant security debt — not necessarily internal — that is tied to immature vendor oversight and vendor risk management practices on other departments' outsourced business processes.

For your firm, the security-debt-inherited-from-vendors observation is the chapter's most underappreciated warning. Outsourcing does not outsource risk — it transfers operational accountability to a vendor but leaves the firm accountable for the outcome. Vendor risk management (lesson 0005 vendor-touchpoint analysis) is the discipline that closes this gap.

The vendor materiality model

Bonney's recommendation for vendor materiality: "Work with your colleagues within the organisation to create a model that defines the materiality of your vendors. You can base this on the type and volume of information shared (e.g., PHI, PII, or intellectual property) as well as the criticality of the vendors' services to your organisation's operations."

For your firm, the materiality model uses:

  • Data type: Customer PII, trading data, custody records, financial reports.
  • Data volume: Number of records (customers × records per customer).
  • Criticality of service: OMS vendor, KYC vendor, custody system vendor, market-data vendor.
  • Compliance overlap: PDPD exposure, UBCK regulatory exposure, contractual exposure.

CISO govern thyself — the annual review discipline

The chapter's most operationally important governance move is the annual review discipline: "The vision, goals, and objectives of your plan should be reviewed at least annually with senior leadership. This review will ensure that the plan's projects, policies, and roadmap are relevant, current, and applicable to the organisation's current technology and application portfolios and strategic business objectives."

For your firm, the annual review produces a 1-page update to the board: what changed in the threat landscape, what changed in the business, what objectives are complete, what objectives are in progress, what objectives are at risk. The update is the artefact that demonstrates the CISO is governing the function — not just running it.

Transparency as a governance principle

Bonney's most distinctive contribution is the transparency principle: "Another critical aspect of governance is transparency, both from the perspective of creating visibility about the plan and the resources being assigned to achieve it. This is an essential driver for the engagement of the entire organisation and the ability of everyone — you, your team, your management peers and the organisation as a whole — to held accountable for the outcomes you collectively agreed to achieve."

For your firm, the transparency artefact is the quarterly security dashboard (lesson 0004) + the strategic plan tracker (this lesson) + the annual board update. Three artefacts together demonstrate that security is being run as a disciplined function, not a hidden one.

The three concluding themes — and your firm's response

The chapter closes with three themes that anchor everything in Volume 2:

  1. Many tools are available. With footnotes, bibliography, and references to standards, the two volumes provide a combination of best practices + informative narrative. Use the resources.
  2. Almost every problem can be addressed by preparation + critical thinking. Do homework. Dig in. Learn everything about the organisation, industry, and goals. Apply to plans, staffing, and resource allocation.
  3. Cybersecurity is a team sport. Build a world-class team. Use the human network. Communication is key. Get out into the organisation. Talk to team, peers, management. Meet regularly with colleagues and vendors. Join local, regional, industry groups. Share knowledge. Absorb what you learn.

For your firm, the three themes map to: (1) the 10 lessons + 10 Vietnamese artefacts; (2) the BIA + assessment + planning discipline; (3) the human network of peer CISOs, MSSPs, law enforcement, UBCK examiners, and security team members.

Where Bonney, Hayslip, and Stamper actually agree

The three authors converge on a single principle: the strategic plan is the artefact that turns the security function from a hidden cost centre into a visible, accountable, board-ready programme. The plan is concise, SWOT-informed, objective-driven, initiative-tracked, and annually reviewed.

  • Bonney emphasises the 6-section structure + the SWOT analysis + the build-from-scratch playbook + the MSSP due diligence + the vendor materiality model + the transparency principle.
  • Hayslip emphasises the strategic plan as proof of business value + the human network as the CISO's primary asset + the project schedule as the operating manual.
  • Stamper emphasises the SWOT integration with the assessment findings + the core-vs-context matrix as the build-vs-outsource tool + the cadence of program reviews with executive management.

For your CEO briefing on the strategic plan, lead with Bonney (the 6-section structure + the SWOT + the build-vs-outsource), support with Stamper (the cadence of program reviews), close with Hayslip (the human network + the project schedule as the operating manual).

Self-check

Why does Bonney insist the strategic plan be concise (5–15 pages, not 50)?

Because the strategic plan has three distinct audiences with three distinct reading patterns: (1) the board reads the executive summary (1 page) + the strategic objectives table (1 page); (2) the executive team reads the full plan; (3) the security team uses the plan + project schedule as the operating manual. A 50-page plan serves none of these audiences — the board does not read 50 pages, the executive team does not absorb 50 pages, and the security team cannot use 50 pages as an operating manual. Bonney's discipline: the plan is concise (5–15 pages), realistic (no 300% budget increases surfaced for the first time), and aligned with current business functions. "The plan document is not the place to surface a 300% increase in funding. That is a discussion that should already have taken place between you and the management team and, as appropriate, the board."

What is the build-vs-outsource decision framework and how does Moore's core-vs-context matrix help?

The core-vs-context matrix groups capabilities along two axes: mission-critical vs commodity, and core competency vs not-core. Mission-critical + core = build and retain in-house (security strategy, BIA ownership, IR leadership). Mission-critical + not-core = strategic partnership (24/7 SOC, MSSP for commodity monitoring). Commodity + not-core = outsource to commodity provider (log retention, vulnerability scanning). Commodity + core = differentiate selectively (threat intel specific to your industry). The matrix is not just for talent management — it is the operational map for the entire security function. For your firm, the practical use: walk through each capability in the security function, place it on the matrix, and the build-vs-outsource answer is structural rather than political.

What is "what is it that I cannot see that I should see that will allow me to protect my organisation better?" and how does it work?

Bonney's framing question for the new CISO with no programme. The point: the security architecture has blind spots, and the most dangerous blind spots are the ones the CISO does not know about. The discipline is to take a full-stack, process-driven view. Leverage data flow diagrams for material processes. Understand where security controls should be deployed and evaluate the status of these controls. Walk through each critical business process end-to-end and ask: what data does this process handle? Where does the data flow? What controls are between each step? Which controls are missing or degraded? For your firm, the answer for the OMS process is probably: customer identity → KYC → order routing → execution → settlement → custody → reporting. Walk through each step. Identify the controls. Identify the gaps. The gaps are the strategic plan's initiatives.
