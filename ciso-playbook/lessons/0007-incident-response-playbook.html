Lesson 0007 · CISO playbook · Arc 2: Run the program when something goes wrong

Incident Response — The Playbook

The chapter that decides whether the 2 AM phone call is a 6-hour incident or a 6-month crisis.

Why this lesson seventh

Lessons 0001–0006 built the prevention + recovery programme. Lesson 0007 builds what you actually do in the moment when something triggers. The BIA + RTO/RPO (lesson 0006) tells you what to recover. This lesson tells you how to recover — the team, the decisions, the communications, the chain of command.

Chapter 15 opens with the framing line every CISO should tattoo on their brain: "Incident response is the most visible function for a typical CISO. For good or for ill, it is the primary way CISOs are judged." The lesson is not "incident response in theory." The lesson is "the playbook that turns a 2 AM incident into a documented, rehearsed, executed routine — not a hunt for information."

For your 9-entity VN securities firm, three specific weights. First, UBCK will judge you on whether your IR programme is documented, tested, and integrated with the regulator's own notification expectations. Second, the trading window (9:00–14:30 HOSE / 9:00–15:00 HNX) is your hardest constraint — an incident during trading has minutes, not hours, to be contained or escalated. Third, the PDPD 72-hour breach notification clock starts when you become "aware" of a personal-data breach — and your playbook must define when "aware" begins.

By the end of this lesson you will have drafted the IR playbook for your firm — in Vietnamese — with the virtuous cycle, the RACI matrix, the pre-staged communications, the runbooks, and the test cadence.

Bonney's virtuous cycle — the framework

Bonney's 6-phase "virtuous cycle" is the canonical structure: prepare, detect, contain, eradicate, recover, improve. Each phase has its own communication requirements. The cycle loops back into preparation because each incident produces lessons that update the playbook.

PhaseWhat happensWho leadsCommunication
Prepare Training, documentation, tools, runbooks, pre-staged communications, tabletop exercises. CISO + IR lead Internal — sell the programme to board.
Detect SIEM alerts, user reports, threat intel feeds, MSSP triage. SOC (MSSP or in-house) + on-call engineer Internal — IR team + CISO.
Contain Isolate affected systems, preserve evidence, stop lateral movement. IR lead + sysadmin / network engineer Internal — CISO + senior management + legal.
Eradicate Remove malware, close attack vector, reset credentials, patch vulnerabilities. IR lead + engineering Internal — same as contain.
Recover Restore from clean backup, validate, monitor for re-infection. Engineering + business owner Internal + customer / market operator.
Improve Post-mortem, lessons learned, playbook update, board briefing. CISO + IR lead + post-mortem team Board + regulatory (if required) + customer (if affected).

The chapter's framing: "While it's helpful to break the entire incident response discipline into a series of discrete phases so that each can be described individually to assist with training and the command and control of response activities, it is rarely clear-cut when one process ends, and the next begins. There is often significant overlap." The virtuous cycle is a teaching tool, not a strict sequence. In real incidents you will move back and forth.

Bonney's 5 governance activities — the board's job

Bonney's most distinctive contribution is the board-level framing. Five governance activities the board must perform during preparation:

  1. Adequately staff, equip, and train. Balancing resource needs is a critical function of the board. The board validates the preparations made for business continuity and recovery.
  2. Provide guidance for communication plans and escalation paths. Avoid the cursory review. Ensure communication plans are adequate. Involve legal in escalation. Know when to notify law enforcement, regulators.
  3. Signal the importance of the preparation work. Senior leaders participate personally in planning and drills. Tone at the top.
  4. Marshal external resources. Partners, suppliers, key customers. Joint drills with multi-partner integrations. Communication plans for enterprise customers.
  5. Dedicate time to prioritise improvements. Every drill and every live incident produces gaps. Act on them.

For your firm, the CISO move is to make sure the board has performed these 5 activities in the last 12 months and can produce evidence (board minutes, tabletop attendance lists, drill reports). Without that evidence, "reasonable care" is harder to demonstrate when an incident happens.

Stamper's 5 critical assumptions — the worst-case framing

Stamper's essay is the most operationally bracing in the chapter. He opens with: "There are three things we can predict with certainty: death, taxes, and that our organisations will be the subject of cyber-attacks." Five assumptions to plan around:

  1. An incident will happen. The CISO's role is not "if," it is "when."
  2. An incident will happen when you are least prepared. After hours. On vacation. During a holiday. When the most experienced engineer is unreachable.
  3. An incident will involve infrastructure you do not directly protect. Shadow IT. Service provider. Third-party platform. The CISO is accountable for the consequences regardless.
  4. An incident will impact a high-value, material business process. Not the low-priority server. The trading system. The custody ledger. The regulatory reporting system.
  5. The response will have life-impacting and career-ending consequences if mishandled. Stamper's exact words: "Cybersecurity incidents can be life-impacting and career-ending if not handled well."
The first four assumptions drive the playbook design. The playbook must work at 2 AM with a primary unavailable and a backup who has never run the playbook end-to-end. The playbook must cover shadow IT and third-party infrastructure. The playbook must prioritise by the BIA's critical processes (lesson 0006). The playbook must be tested quarterly, not annually. Stamper's recommendation: "while many standards suggest annual incident response exercises, I strongly recommend that you change the cadence to quarterly."

Stamper's IT/OT convergence + negligence doctrine — the legal frame

Stamper's most forward-looking argument is that IT/OT convergence is creating a negligence doctrine in cybersecurity. Establishing negligence requires four elements:

  1. Duty. Regulations (PDPD, UBCK circulars, ISO 27001) create the legal duty of care.
  2. Breach of duty. Frameworks (NIST, ISO) define specific obligations. Discretionary practices no longer suffice.
  3. Causation. When harm results from an unpatched system or a misconfiguration, causation is established.
  4. Damages. Liability and settlement awards follow.

For VN securities firms, this maps directly to: PDPD creates the duty for personal data. UBCK circulars create the duty for ICT risk management. ISO 27001 controls (where certified) define the breach-of-duty standard. Customer records at $158 each (lesson 0006) define the damages.

Stamper, CISO DRG Vol. 2 Ch. 15 If incident response has been a process you have put off until next quarter or delegated to other members of your team, it's time to bring incident response front and center and make it a top, if not the top, priority of your security programme.

Stamper's "document the obvious" — the most underrated move

Stamper's 8-category "document the obvious" list is the most operationally useful single artefact in the chapter. The framing: "It's the middle of the night, and you get one of those calls. You stumble to your home office, fire up the laptop and try to figure what's happening. It is precisely the wrong time to look for fundamental information." The 8 categories:

  1. Contact details of key participants. Home phone, mobile phone, alternative email — not just office. Embed in the IR plan.
  2. Contact details and account numbers for key vendors. ISP, MSSP, EDR vendor, cloud provider, forensic firm, crisis comms. Know who is authorised to request changes at 3 AM.
  3. Escalation matrices and conditions. Circumstances for escalation. Alternate contacts.
  4. Signal-to-noise definitions. The difference between an event, an alert, an incident, and a crisis. When does each escalate?
  5. Pre-drafted communiques. Corporate comms drafts basic templates for data breach, DDoS, ransomware. Legal reviews in advance.
  6. Pre-established authorisations. Who can approve shutdown of a system? Who can approve payment of a ransom (which the chapter explicitly discourages)? Who can approve external comms?
  7. Process workflows and documentation. Workflow diagram understandable by both technical and non-technical colleagues.
  8. Runbooks for common attacks. DoS/DDoS, ransomware, phishing/spear-phishing, malware, compromised vendor.

For your firm, this is the 1-page-per-category document you build in the next 30 days. Hard copies in the IR team's homes. Soft copies on a tablet in the SOC. Quarterly review and update.

Hayslip's "it's about the business" — the value-model argument

Hayslip's opening framing is the chapter's most quoted line: "Incident response isn't about technology, it is really about business." His value argument for an in-house IR programme (vs pure outsourcing) is the rebuttal to the "let's just outsource it" instinct:

  1. The business is accountable for reasonable care, regardless of who does the work. Outsourcing IR does not outsource accountability.
  2. The business needs its own IR plan. The plan covers communications, resource activation, leadership responsibility, reporting to executive leadership. This is not the vendor's job.
  3. The IRP provides a framework for fast decisions. Without it, the team is improvising at 2 AM.
  4. The IRP provides clarity on priorities. Which system, which data, which stakeholder, which comms.
  5. The IRP defines the business priorities and interdependencies. The mapping you need to triage is the BIA's output (lesson 0006).

Hayslip's 2016 SANS stats frame the maturity gap: "76% of those security professionals had dedicated internal IR teams... 21% said their time to detect malware in their networks, or 'dwell time,' was two to seven days, while 40% indicated that they could detect an incident in less than one day... malware remains the underlying cause of most reported breaches, at 69%... 65% of the security professionals surveyed were still dealing with a shortage of skilled personnel, and only 58% of organisations admit to regularly reviewing and updating their IR processes."

For your firm, the median dwell time target should be <24 hours. The 2016 leader target (40%) was <1 day. The 2016 laggard (21%) was 2–7 days. The lesson's practical implication: invest in detection engineering (lesson 0004 monitoring + lesson 0005 intel) to drive dwell time down, and run quarterly IR exercises to drive response time down.

Stamper's "incident response is a team sport" lists 5 functions that must participate in the IR plan. This is your RACI matrix's rows:

Threat modelling — the proactive IR move

Stamper's most operationally distinctive recommendation: "CISOs should have their teams conduct threat modelling exercises on the more critical or high-priority operations, processes, applications, and infrastructure within their organisation. Threat modelling will indicate where there are inherent weaknesses in these systems that the organisation values most. Further, threat modelling will also provide contextualised indicators of compromise (IOCs) that can inform the security and IT staff."

STRIDE (Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege) and DREAD (Damage potential, Reproducibility, Exploitability, Affected users, Discoverability) are the two frameworks Stamper names. For your firm, the practical move is to run STRIDE against the top 3 BIA processes per entity, producing a threat model + IOCs that feed the SIEM use-case library (lesson 0004).

The "what would happen if..." question

Stamper's most underrated practical recommendation: "Bring up the incident response process in your day-to-day discussions with your team and other colleagues. One simple way to do this is to ask the following question: 'What would happen if adversaries compromised this system?'"

For your firm, this is a 5-minute weekly question you raise in your leadership meeting. The first answer is always partial. The fourth answer is detailed. By the twelfth week, your team is doing the threat-modelling instinctively.

Where Bonney, Stamper, and Hayslip actually agree

The three authors converge on a single principle: incident response is a team sport, requires pre-staged artefacts, must be tested quarterly, and is judged by the board on whether the firm can demonstrate reasonable care.

  • Bonney emphasises the virtuous cycle + the 5 governance activities for the board + the importance of pre-staged communication templates.
  • Stamper emphasises muscle memory + the 5 critical assumptions + "document the obvious" + threat modelling + the IT/OT negligence doctrine.
  • Hayslip emphasises the business value framing + the 2016 SANS stats as the maturity gap + the argument for in-house IRP even when outsourced operationally.

For your CEO briefing, lead with Hayslip (the SANS stats + the business-value framing), support with Stamper (the 5 assumptions + "document the obvious"), close with Bonney (the virtuous cycle + the 5 governance activities).

5. Pre-staged communications (5 mẫu)

  • Template A — Data breach (customer-facing): Thông báo cho khách hàng về việc dữ liệu cá nhân có thể bị ảnh hưởng. Bao gồm: scope, dữ liệu nào, hành động của công ty, quyền của khách hàng, liên hệ.

  • Template C — System outage (market operator + UBCK): Báo cáo cho UBCK + HOSE/HNX về gián đoạn hệ thống giao dịch, thời gian dự kiến khôi phục, tác động.

  • Template E — Board briefing: Báo cáo cho HĐQT về sự cố — scope, impact, response, lessons learned.

Self-check

Why does Stamper insist on quarterly IR exercises instead of annual?

Because of muscle memory. "Incident response benefits from muscle memory. In this case, even high-level discussions about incident response will improve overall organisational preparedness and resiliency." Annual exercises are too infrequent — the playbook changes substantially between exercises, key people leave or rotate, and the lessons learned are not practiced enough to become reflexive. Quarterly exercises produce a different operational outcome: when an incident happens at 2 AM, the team executes rather than improvises. Stamper's recommendation is the practical floor for any firm that takes reasonable-care doctrine seriously.

What is "document the obvious" and why does Stamper argue for it?

It is the practice of pre-staging the basic information you need at 2 AM — contact details (home phone, mobile, alternative email), vendor account numbers, escalation matrices, signal-to-noise definitions, pre-drafted communiques, pre-authorisations, workflow diagrams, runbooks for common attacks. Without it, your first hour of an incident is spent hunting for information that should have been pre-staged. Stamper's argument is that this hunting-for-information time is "for the benefit of the adversary and to the detriment of your organisation." For a securities firm, where trading windows have minutes of tolerance, the difference between pre-staged and improvised can be the difference between a contained incident and a regulatory disclosure.

What is the "team sport" framing and why is it important?

Incident response is not just a security/IT function. It requires HR (insider threats, employee comms, training programmes), Legal Counsel (regulator notification, law enforcement liaison, PDPD 72-hour clock, attorney-client privilege for forensic work), Corporate Communications (pre-drafted templates, customer + media statements), Line of Business (ground truth on operational impact, shadow IT knowledge, vendor relationships), and Executive Management (tone at the top, risk-tolerance decisions, public communicator for major incidents). Without explicit RACI coverage of all 5 functions, the IR plan is structurally incomplete. Stamper's framing: "Too frequently, we think of incident response exclusively as a cybersecurity or IT discipline. Nothing could be further from the truth."
