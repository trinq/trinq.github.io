Threat Intel as a Program

Most CISOs buy threat-intel feeds. The book's argument: build the program around what you actually have to protect, then subscribe to feeds that map to that exposure.

Why this lesson fifth

Lessons 0001–0004 built the inside-out programme: 90-day plan, team, awareness, monitoring. Lesson 0005 turns the camera outward — to the threats your monitoring programme has to detect.

Chapter 13 is the most underrated chapter in Volume 2. Most CISOs read it as "we should buy a threat-intel feed." The book argues something sharper: threat intel without context is just a laundry list that produces fear, uncertainty, and doubt — or "boy who cried wolf" syndrome. The chapter's central move is Stamper's "Six Keys" — a structured way to make threat intel relevant to your environment rather than to the global threat landscape in general.

For your 9-entity VN securities firm, the chapter lands with two specific weights. First, the financial-services sector has its own intel-sharing infrastructure (FS-ISAC, national CSIRTs, sectoral ISACs) that you should be plugged into — but the VN equivalents are smaller, less mature, and harder to find. Second, your monitoring programme (lesson 0004) needs threat intel to populate the SIEM use-case library. Without intel, the SIEM is rules you wrote yourself; with intel, it is rules informed by what adversaries are actually doing against your sector.

By the end of this lesson you will have drafted the threat-intelligence programme outline for your firm — in Vietnamese — that maps intel sources to the Six Keys, defines internal vs external feeds, and names the specific actions intel triggers.

Bonney's situational awareness frame — the "look outside" move

Bonney opens with the most quoted definition of threat intel in the book: "evidence-based knowledge, including context, mechanisms, indicators, implications and action-oriented advice about an existing or emerging menace or hazard to assets." The key word is context. A feed that tells you "ransomware group X is targeting financial services" is not actionable. A feed that tells you "ransomware group X is targeting financial-services firms in APAC using phishing emails impersonating UBCK notifications, with the initial-access technique matching your trading-desk email profile" is actionable.

Bonney's seven tactical questions are the situational-awareness checklist. Every CISO should answer them out loud once a quarter:

  1. What is our current working relationship with law enforcement? (For VN: A05 / Ministry of Public Security, VNCERT/CC, the VNPT ISC and Viettel CSIRT teams. These are the closest equivalents to FBI InfraGard.)
  2. What are our sources of international cyber threat intelligence? (FS-ISAC, regional CERTs, vendor feeds that include APAC coverage.)
  3. What organizations are we sharing cyber threat knowledge with, and what are we learning from them? (Your peer network — peer CISO roundtables at VN Security Summit, the SecurityWorld community.)
  4. What is our working relationship with the most high-profile firms who have had breaches? (For VN: not formalised. The closest is informal post-incident sharing through trusted-agent networks.)
  5. Do we track social media sites and blogs referencing us for clues about our vulnerabilities? (Brand monitoring — cheap and high-value. Often catches early signals of phishing campaigns or activist chatter.)
  6. When we hear of a breach in another organization, what do we do? (Bonney's standard: validate if the threat applies, assess nature of asset under attack, scan your environment, patch if needed.)
  7. As we look at the data for intrusions, what has been the primary category of threat actors? (Track this. Over 12 months it tells you who is actually interested in your firm.)
Bonney, CISO DRG Vol. 2 Ch. 13 Effective threat intelligence programs should make you aware of threats that are specific to your industry before you read about them in the national news.

That last sentence is the bar. If you are reading about a threat in the news after it has hit your peers, your intel programme is failing. The 24-72 hour lead time Bonney describes is what makes threat intel valuable.

The human network — Bonney's structural argument

Bonney's most distinctive contribution to the chapter is the human-network framing. He argues that the most valuable threat intel comes not from feeds but from peer relationships — with law enforcement, with peer CISOs, with vendors, with partners. The mechanisms:

  • InfraGard (US). FBI public-private partnership. VN equivalent: A05 / MPS liaison through VNCERT/CC.
  • ISACs (US). Sector-specific Information Sharing and Analysis Centers. Financial Services ISAC (FS-ISAC) is the financial-sector model. VN equivalent: emerging; check VNISA sectoral working groups.
  • LECCs (US). Law Enforcement Coordination Centers. VN equivalent: provincial-level police cybercrime units.
  • Chatham House Rule. The standard ground rule for peer sharing: "share what you've learned, but do not attribute specific comments to a person or organisation." This is what makes peer sharing safe.

For your firm, the practical move is to build a 6-person peer circle: 2 peer CISOs from other VN securities firms, 1 from a VN bank, 1 from a VN insurer, 1 from a regional financial-services CISO (Singapore or HK), 1 from your MSSP's senior SOC staff. Quarterly meeting under Chatham House Rule. Two-hour agenda: what each of you is seeing, what worked, what failed. This is the highest-ROI threat intel you will get — because it is contextualised to your actual peers, in your actual jurisdiction.

Stamper's Six Keys — the contextualisation method

Stamper's essay is the chapter's most under-appreciated contribution. He gives you a structured way to make threat intel relevant to your environment. The Six Keys:

  1. Know your industry. Which sector are you in? What are the sector-specific threat actors? What recent attacks have hit peer firms? For VN securities: phishing campaigns impersonating UBCK, ransomware targeting trading desks, abuse of customer onboarding flows for AML evasion.
  2. Know the profile of your business. Board and executive profiles (are they targets? do they have unusual public visibility?), revenue model (where does income come from?), client profile (HNW individuals? institutional? retail?). Each profile shapes who is interested in attacking you.
  3. Know the critical business processes. Trading, custody, settlement, customer onboarding, KYC/AML, market-data ingestion. For each, what is the CIA impact if availability is interrupted?
  4. Know which applications support which processes. OMS, custody ledger, KYC platform, market-data feed handler. For each: manufacturer, version, custom or off-the-shelf, source-code location if in-house.
  5. Know your infrastructure. CMDB. For each material application: OS, server/VM, storage, network, backup dependencies. Hardware manufacturers, firmware versions, OS patch status.
  6. Know your vendors and suppliers. Map vendors to specific business processes. SaaS providers: which data centers? Which sub-processors? Contract clauses: right-to-audit, breach notification, minimum security practices?
Stamper's load-bearing insight. Threat intel without the Six Keys is a feed that produces noise. Threat intel with the Six Keys is a feed that produces action. The workflow: (1) build the Six Keys inventory; (2) for each Key, identify the 3–5 threat-actor categories most likely to target it; (3) subscribe to intel sources that cover those categories; (4) when intel arrives, triage against the Six Keys to determine relevance; (5) trigger a specific action (SIEM rule update, vulnerability scan, awareness campaign, vendor review). Without the Six Keys, step 4 is impossible. With them, it is a 10-minute triage.

For your 9-entity firm, the Six Keys inventory is the bridge between Stamper's framework and your monitoring programme. Build it once at the group level. Refine it at the entity level for the highest-risk business processes.

Hayslip's strategic framing — the operational spine

Hayslip's essay is the most operational of the three. He starts with Gartner's definition (which Bonney also uses), then makes the move that drives the rest of the chapter: threat intel must be actionable to be valuable. A feed that informs your team about a threat they cannot act on is wasted.

His key operational framework: internal vs external sources.

Source typeWhat it gives youHow to use it
Internal Your own attack history: IOCs, attack patterns, vulnerabilities exploited, business processes that were targets, legacy issues that keep recurring. Feed your SIEM with your own IOC history. Run quarterly review of recurring patterns. Use the history to identify business processes with structural exposure.
External — commercial feeds Sector-specific IOCs, threat-actor profiles, dark-web monitoring, vulnerability disclosures, exploit availability. Tier by relevance to your Six Keys inventory. Subscribe to 2–3 high-quality feeds; ignore the rest.
External — industry sharing FS-ISAC alerts, peer CISO sharing, sectoral ISACs. Highest-ROI for contextualised intel. Join at least one industry sharing community.
External — law enforcement VNCERT/CC alerts, MPS cybercrime notices, international CERT feeds. Free, often timely, sector-agnostic. Use as baseline coverage.
External — crowdsourced OTX (AlienVault), MISP community feeds, threat-crowdsourced platforms. Free or low-cost. Useful for community-sourced visibility.
External — vendor / MSSP Vendor-specific threat intel (Microsoft, Cisco, your EDR vendor, your SIEM vendor, your firewall vendor). Tied to the technology you have deployed. Often under-used. Build into your MSSP contract.

The polymorphic-malware / 24-hour phishing-site / ransomware-as-a-service reality

Hayslip opens his essay with three observations that explain why traditional intel is breaking:

  • 94% of malware observed in 2016 was being seen for the first time (Webroot data). Polymorphism — automated file modification — means signature-based detection is structurally losing.
  • 84% of phishing sites live less than 24 hours. Blocklists are structurally losing.
  • Ransomware-as-a-service has lowered the entry barrier for would-be attackers. Anyone with a grudge and a few hundred dollars can launch a credible attack.

The implication for your programme: signature-based intel is no longer enough. You need behavioural intel (what attackers do, not what their tools look like), contextual intel (what is being targeted in your sector), and TTP-based intel (tactics, techniques, procedures — mapped to MITRE ATT&CK). The OSINT catalogue Hayslip gives is biased toward TTP-based intel. That is the right bias.

The 11 questions to ask before subscribing to any commercial feed. Hayslip's due-diligence checklist — use it the next time a vendor pitches you: (1) How many feeds, what types? (2) File formats — CSV, XML, STIX/TAXII? (3) API available? (4) Can you ingest? (5) Sources — beware "confidential." (6) Currency — real-time or near real-time? (7) Reports for executive briefings? (8) Cost structure? (9) Tiered service? (10) Multi-year discount? (11) Customer support — 24/7, response-time SLA? If a vendor cannot answer all 11 cleanly, the feed is not ready for your programme.

What threat intel actually triggers — the action chain

The most under-discussed part of threat intel is what happens after a feed surfaces something. Most programmes collect but do not act. The action chain Hayslip describes:

  1. Triage. Apply the Six Keys inventory. Does this threat target assets we have? Does it use techniques we are vulnerable to? Is the threat actor relevant to our sector?
  2. Indicator ingestion. Push relevant IOCs into the SIEM, EDR, firewall, and email gateway. Automated where possible.
  3. Vulnerability scan. If the threat exploits a specific CVE, scan the environment for that CVE.
  4. Historical search. Search past logs for the IOC. Have we already been targeted?
  5. Defence update. Patch, configure, disable, segment, or back up — whatever is appropriate.
  6. Awareness update. If the threat uses a phishing pattern, update the awareness programme and run a targeted simulation.
  7. Stakeholder communication. Brief the CISO, the relevant entity leadership, the board if material.
  8. Sharing. Anonymised sharing back to the community. Chatham House Rule applies.

Without the action chain, threat intel is noise. With it, threat intel is a programme.

Where Bonney, Stamper, and Hayslip actually agree

The three authors converge on a single principle: threat intel without context and action is wasted.

  • Bonney emphasises the human network — peer sharing, law-enforcement relationships, Chatham House Rule. His argument: feeds tell you what is happening; the human network tells you what it means for you.
  • Stamper emphasises the Six Keys — contextualise to your industry, business profile, processes, applications, infrastructure, vendors. His argument: intel without context is a laundry list.
  • Hayslip emphasises the action chain — internal vs external sources, the 11 vendor questions, the OSINT catalogue, the polymorphic-malware reality. His argument: intel without action is wasted spend.

Where they diverge: emphasis. For your CEO briefing, lead with Stamper (the Six Keys are the bridge between intel and your actual business), support with Hayslip (the action chain shows operational discipline), close with Bonney (the human network is the long-term asset).

Self-check

Why does Stamper say threat intel without context is a "laundry list"?

Because most intel feeds report threats in the abstract — "ransomware group X targeting financial sector," "APT group Y exploiting CVE-Z." Without context, you cannot tell whether the threat is relevant to your environment. Does your firm use the vulnerable software? Are you in the targeted sector geography? Does your attack surface include the technique being used? Stamper's Six Keys is the structured way to answer these questions, so that when intel arrives, you can triage it in 10 minutes against an inventory you have already built. Without the Six Keys, triage is guesswork; with them, triage is a checklist.

What is the Chatham House Rule and why does Bonney argue for it?

Chatham House Rule: "participants are free to use the information received, but neither the identity nor the affiliation of the speaker(s), nor that of any other participant, may be revealed." Bonney argues for it because peer sharing is the highest-ROI threat intel you can get — but only if participants trust that what they share will not be attributed back to them. Without that trust, peer sharing collapses into vendor pitches and press-release quotes. For your firm, the practical move is to convene a 6-person peer CISO circle and explicitly adopt the Chatham House Rule for every meeting. The first meeting will be stiff; the second will be productive; by the fourth, you will have actionable intel no commercial feed can match.

Why does Hayslip argue that polymorphic malware and 24-hour phishing sites make signature-based intel "structurally losing"?

Because 94% of malware observed in 2016 was being seen for the first time, and 84% of phishing sites lived less than 24 hours. Signature-based detection — matching files against known-bad hashes, matching URLs against known-bad blocklists — is structurally outpaced by attackers who automate polymorphism and rotate infrastructure hourly. The implication is that threat intel must shift from "what does the bad thing look like?" to "what is the adversary doing?" — i.e., from signatures to TTPs. MITRE ATT&CK is the indexing scheme for this shift. For your programme, the practical move is to use ATT&CK to organise your SIEM use-case library, so that detection is based on adversary behaviour, not on file hashes that change daily.
