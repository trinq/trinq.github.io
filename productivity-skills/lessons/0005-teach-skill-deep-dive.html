The teach Skill — Deep Dive

Master the Feynman Technique for deep learning

The teach skill isn't just "explaining." It's structured learning.

The Feynman Principle If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough. Real understanding comes when you can teach it to a beginner.

The Four Steps

1

Identify the Topic

What specifically are we learning? Narrow it down. "JavaScript" is too broad. "JavaScript closures" is specific.

2

Assess Prior Knowledge

What do they already know? Build on existing understanding. Don't explain what they already know.

3

Bridge the Gap

Connect new concepts to what they know. Use analogies, metaphors, concrete examples.

4

Check Understanding

Ask them to explain it back. Can they teach it to someone else? Find gaps.

The Assessment Questions

Before explaining anything, ask:

  • "What do you already know about this?"
  • "Can you give me an example of what you mean?"
  • "What are you trying to accomplish?"
  • "Where have you gotten stuck before?"

Bridge with Analogy

Closure Analogy

A closure is like a backpack a function carries. When the function is created, it packs up references to variables from its surrounding scope. Even when the function travels elsewhere, it still has its backpack and can access those variables whenever it runs.

Another: Private Variables

Closures let you create "private" variables in JavaScript. Like how a class has private fields, a closure can encapsulate state that only the function can access.

Anti-Patterns in Teaching

Skipping assessment. Jumping straight to explanation because "everyone needs this."

Result: Boring for experts, confusing for beginners.

Using jargon without defining it. "Closures are functions that capture their lexical environment."

Result: Sounds impressive, teaches nothing.

Moving too fast. Covering multiple concepts before checking understanding.

Result: Overwhelm and confusion.

The Check Step

After explaining, ask them to teach it back:

  • "Can you explain closures to me as if I were a junior dev?"
  • "How would you use a closure to solve [problem]?"
  • "What's the difference between closure and scope?"
If they can explain it simply, they understand it. If they revert to jargon or can't answer simply, there's a gap.
Use the "Explain it to a 10-year-old" test. Can they simplify it? That's true understanding.

When Teaching Fails

If the learner can't grasp it, the teacher failed, not the learner. Common causes:

  • Assumed too much prior knowledge
  • Jumped too fast
  • Used unfamiliar terms without definitions
  • No concrete examples
  • No analogy to something they know
