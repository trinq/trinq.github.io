Human-in-the-Loop Design — When and How to Keep Humans in the Decision

"The last mile is always the hardest mile." HITL is not a workaround for unreliable AI — it is a deliberate architectural decision that says: some decisions carry stakes that justify a human's time.

Prerequisites: Lesson 0001 (architecture), Lesson 0002 (supervisor), Lesson 0003 (evaluation). You should understand the accuracy trajectory (0–50% → 80% → 90% → 100%) before starting.

What this lesson teaches. By the end of this lesson you will be able to decide where in a multi-agent system human involvement is architecturally appropriate, how to implement three distinct HITL patterns, and how to calibrate the cost of human involvement against the cost of error.

1. The Reframe — HITL is a Design Choice, Not a Failure Mode

The dominant mental model for human-in-the-loop is compensatory: the AI isn't good enough yet, so humans fill the gap until the AI improves. This framing is backwards for high-stakes domains. The right framing is: some decisions carry stakes that permanently justify a human's time, regardless of how good the AI gets.

Ask David makes this explicit. The system operates in a financial context with billions of dollars of assets. Even if the AI reaches 99% accuracy, the 1% of errors — applied to a billion-dollar portfolio — can be catastrophic. The cost of a human reviewing that 1% is trivially small compared to the cost of the errors going unchecked. HITL is not a sign of weakness; it is risk management made architectural.

"When you apply a general model to a specific domain, usually you will get less than 50% of accuracy... Between 90 and 100 — that's what we call the last mile, and the last mile is always the hardest mile... Ask David still consults with real David whenever needed."
— Zheng Xue & David Odomirok, JP Morgan Chase Private Bank

The practical implication: you design HITL into the system from the beginning, not as a patch when things go wrong. The supervisor knows — from the moment it classifies the user's intent — whether this query type requires human review.

2. The HITL Spectrum — Four Levels of Human Involvement

Human-in-the-loop is not binary — full autonomy or full manual. There is a spectrum of involvement, and the right level depends on the stakes of the specific decision type:

Level 1
Fully autonomous. AI decides and acts. No human involved. Example: routing a general FAQ question.
Level 2
AI decides, human reviews asynchronously. Example: code suggestion in a PR, reviewed before merge.
Level 3
Human approves before AI acts. Example: high-value trade flagged for manager sign-off.
Level 4
Human decides, AI assists. Example: compliance officer reviews flagged transactions.

Most multi-agent systems use all four levels, applied to different parts of the workflow:

  • Level 1 (autonomous): routine queries with low stakes — "What is the current price of fund XYZ?"
  • Level 2 (async review): answers that look correct but need a spot-check — the reflection node catches obvious failures; a human reviews a sample.
  • Level 3 (approval gate): high-stakes actions — the analytics agent generates code for a complex model; a human reviews and approves execution.
  • Level 4 (human-led): novel or ambiguous cases that fall outside the system's training distribution — escalated to a specialist.

The key design question is not "how much human?" but "which decisions get which level?" Most teams get this wrong by applying a blanket human review to everything (too expensive, human becomes a bottleneck) or nothing (too risky for high-stakes domains). The art is calibrated routing — matching decision stakes to human involvement level.

3. Three HITL Patterns — Where and How Humans Enter

Pattern 1: Approval Gate (Human Checks Before Action)

Where it goes: Between the analytics agent's code generation and its execution. Complex analytics queries in Ask David generate Python code before running — and a human reviews that code before it executes against live financial models.

How it works:

  1. Analytics agent generates code to answer the complex query.
  2. System pauses and surfaces the code to a human reviewer with the original question.
  3. Human approves, rejects, or modifies.
  4. Approved code executes; results flow back into the answer.
Approval Gate — When to use
The action being approved carries irreversible consequences or significant cost. Code that moves money, sends communications, or triggers downstream processes should almost always go through an approval gate. The question is the threshold — not whether to gate, but what triggers the gate.

Pattern 2: Escalation Trigger (Supervisor Routes to Human)

Where it goes: In the supervisor's routing logic. The supervisor evaluates confidence signals and routes to a human when signals indicate the AI shouldn't handle this alone.

Common triggers:

Does the query match a known pattern in the evaluation dataset?
Autonomous — proceed to sub-agents
Is confidence below threshold (reflection node failed)?
Escalate — flag for human review
Is the query above the financial threshold (e.g., >$1M implied)?
Approval — human approves answer before delivery
Does the query fall in a flagged category (litigation, compliance, exception)?
Escalate — specialist review
Autonomous — proceed with confidence

Pattern 3: Async Review Loop (Human Reviews After)

Where it goes: As a sampling mechanism on top of Level 1 autonomous answers. Not every answer gets reviewed — a representative sample does, and that sample feeds back into the ground truth dataset.

How it works:

  1. System delivers answer to user autonomously.
  2. A sampling engine selects ~10% of answers for human review based on: edge cases, high-stakes topics, low-confidence queries, or random sampling.
  3. Human SME reviews the answer and provides feedback.
  4. Feedback becomes a new ground truth example in the evaluation dataset.
  5. Periodically: evaluate the reflection node's pass/fail decisions against the human review results — does the LLM-as-judge agree with the human?
Async Review Loop — The evaluation flywheel in practice
This is where Zheng Xue's "once you start doing review, you will accumulate ground truth examples" actually happens. Every async review is a seed for the evaluation dataset. The loop is: review → ground truth → better eval → better system → fewer reviews needed for the same quality. At scale, this dramatically reduces human review burden while maintaining quality.

4. Designing Escalation Triggers — The Core Design Decision

The escalation trigger is the most important HITL design decision. Get it wrong in one direction and you get expensive human bottlenecks. Get it wrong in the other and you miss high-stakes cases. The right trigger is specific to your domain, but the framework is universal:

Trigger dimension What it measures Example signal
Confidence score How certain is the AI? Reflection node failed; RAG retrieved no high-similarity chunks; structured query returned null
Financial threshold What's the dollar magnitude? Fund value > $1M; transaction size > $500K; portfolio change > 5%
Query category Does this fall in a flagged domain? Litigation, compliance, regulatory, exception — anything requiring specialist knowledge
Novelty Is this out-of-distribution? Query doesn't match any pattern in training data; new product type; new regulation
User signal Does the user want human involvement? User explicitly requests human review; user rejected prior AI answer

Start broad, narrow over time. On day one, err on the side of more human review. You can always reduce escalation as you accumulate evidence that the AI handles certain query types well. It is much harder to add escalation after an incident. Each escalation you skip because "the AI handles this fine" is a potential risk event — track those decisions.

5. The Cost of HITL — And How to Minimize It

The cost of HITL is real: human reviewers are expensive, slow, and become a bottleneck. The goal is not to minimize human involvement — it is to get the right human involvement at the right time. Here's how:

Cost 1: Human becomes a bottleneck
Problem: Every query above the escalation threshold waits in a human review queue. Long queues mean slow answers — defeating the purpose of automation.

Solution: Build a triage layer before the human reviewer. The system pre-classifies: "high confidence, low stakes" (proceed autonomously), "medium confidence" (flag for priority queue), "low confidence" (urgent queue). This ensures the human reviewer always sees the cases that genuinely need them.
Cost 2: Human review burden doesn't scale
Problem: As the system handles more queries, the human review queue grows faster than you can review it.

Solution: The async review loop (Pattern 3) — sample intelligently, don't review everything. Target the 10–20% of cases that are most informative (edge cases, low-confidence, novel). As the evaluation dataset grows, use LLM-as-judge as a pre-filter: if the judge is calibrated and confident, it handles the pass/fail; human reviews only the cases where the judge is uncertain.
Cost 3: Human reviewer fatigue
Problem: Reviewing AI-generated answers for hours is cognitively draining. Fatigue leads to inconsistent review quality — the exact problem you're trying to solve.

Solution: Design the review interface for speed. Show the human: the original question, the AI's answer, and the specific flagged reason (not a raw transcript). Make the action a single click: "Approve / Revise / Escalate." Add variance randomization — show cases in random order, not grouped by topic, to maintain attention. Track reviewer accuracy over time.
