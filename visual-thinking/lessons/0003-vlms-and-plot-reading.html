VLMs read plots wrong (even when they're accurate)

A chart-reading benchmark shows GPT-4V, LLaVA, and BLIP-2 trail humans — not on accuracy, but on which errors they make. And a plot-selection experiment shows humans pick charts by question, not by chart-type.
~9 min Lesson 0003 glossary

Part 2 of Fan's talk shifts from drawing to data visualization. The provocation: humans can read charts. Vision-language models can read charts. The two should be benchmarked head-to-head.

What Fan + Verma's team found is that the headline accuracy gap is the wrong thing to look at. The interesting gap is the error pattern.

The benchmark

Six commonly used tests of graph-based reasoning, administered "in as parallel a manner as possible" to both humans and several VLMs (~38:00–44:00):

  • GGR (Graphic Gestalt Reasoning)
  • VLAT (Visual-Literacy Assessment Test)
  • CALVI (adversarial plots — designed to trick with misleading y-axis scales)
  • HOLF / HOLF-multi
  • Chart-QA subset

VLMs tested: two variants of BLIP-2 + FlanT5, three LLaVA variants, Matcha + Pix2Struct, GPT-4V. Human reference: US adults with at least one high-school math class.

"Even though GPT-4V might look to be approaching human-level performance, none of these models, GPT-4V included, generated human-like error patterns."
— Judy Fan, 39:30

The score Fan uses for the error-pattern check is a divergence between the model's wrong-answer distribution and the humans' wrong-answer distribution. The human noise ceiling — the band you'd expect from humans being imperfect — is shown in green. Every model's dots fall below that band. The models make wrong answers humans wouldn't make.

Why this matters: if a model makes wrong answers humans wouldn't make, you can't trust its right answers either, even at high accuracy. The shape of the failure mode is the question, not just the rate. A model that's "human-level on Chart-QA" but wrong in alien ways is a model you'll misread on the cases that matter.

The CALVI finding is the most actionable

CALVI plots are designed to be adversarial: funny y-axis limits, ambiguous units, things designed to make you misread the magnitude. The accuracy gap on CALVI is much larger than on Chart-QA, which is the standard benchmark everyone reports.

"We might have missed [the gap] had we relied exclusively on the chart understanding benchmarks that are currently most popular in the machine learning literature."
— Judy Fan, 39:00

Lesson: if you're evaluating a VLM's chart-reading for any application where the data might be messy (dashboards, ambiguous scaling, real-world reports), Chart-QA is a flattering benchmark. Pick something with adversarial plots, or build your own.

The plot-selection experiment (Huey)

The second half of Part 2 (~44:00–54:00) shifts from reading plots to picking plots. Holly Huey's experiment: present participants with a question (e.g., "what is the average speed of aircraft in overcast skies that encounter bird strikes at 0–5K [typo aside] altitude?") and a menu of candidate plots — bar, line, scatter — at varying levels of aggregation. Let them pick.

Then measure how often their chosen plot, when paired with the question, produced correct answers from a separate group of participants.

Several hypotheses tested:

Hypothesis What it predicts What the data shows
Bar-plot purist Always pick bar plot Rejected — humans don't default to bar
Targeted-plot purist Always pick the most aggregated plot Rejected — humans prefer relevance over aggregation
Show-more-data Pick the plot with the most underlying data shown Partially supported but not the best predictor
Audience-sensitive Pick the plot whose features are needed to answer the question Best fit — predicts the answerer-performance distribution
"I'm excited about that result as an initial validation of a strategy for measuring visualization understanding using more open-ended tasks, and also because it suggests that even non-experts … are sensitive to those features of plots that make some suitable for answering some questions than others."
— Judy Fan, 47:30

The measurement problem (Brockbank)

The third leg of Part 2 (~54:00–57:00) is a quiet bomb. Postdoc Erik Brockbank took two of the most-used graph-reading tests (GGR and VLAT), administered them to ~1,700 US adults across two samples (UCSD campus + Prolific representative), and asked: what are these tests actually measuring?

Two findings:

  1. The two tests correlate — people who do well on one do well on the other. So they're measuring something real, not noise.
  2. But what they're measuring is not the kind of plot, the type of question, or the standard textbook ontology. A four-factor model that doesn't use the standard taxonomy (find-max / identify-cluster / characterize-distribution / retrieve-value) predicts error patterns much better than the textbook categorization.
"Other underlying factors that aren't well described by the ontology are really accounting for error patterns much more efficiently."
— Judy Fan, 56:30

Lesson: your standard taxonomy of "graph-reading skills" is probably not what's actually driving the difficulty. The skill decomposition you've been using to teach (or to write your agent's prompt) might be wrong.

What this lesson leaves you with

Three rules, each tied to a concrete agent-design move:

1. Score error patterns, not just accuracy

If you're benchmarking a VLM on a chart-reading task, accuracy is the first cut, not the last. Compute the divergence between the model's wrong-answer distribution and a human baseline's wrong-answer distribution. If they don't match, the model is failing in ways that won't generalize — and your "human-level" number is misleading.

Heuristic for evaluating an agent's chart-reading: don't accept a chart-QA score alone. Pair it with an adversarial plot set (CALVI-style) and a wrong-answer-shape comparison. Report both.

2. Pick charts by question-relevance, not by chart-type prior

An agent that's asked to "explain the data" should not default to a bar chart. It should pick the chart whose features make the question answerable. The audience-sensitive hypothesis is your rubric.

Heuristic for an agent that picks or generates charts: in the prompt, force the agent to first name the question, then name the features of the chart that answer it, then pick. Three steps, not one. This is the bar-plot-purist trap closed.

3. Treat your "skills taxonomy" as a hypothesis

If your agent's chart-reading prompt is structured around "find the max," "identify the cluster," "retrieve a value," etc., you're using the textbook ontology. Fan's data says that ontology is the wrong factor structure. Watch for chart-reading failures that don't fit your decomposition — they're telling you your taxonomy is wrong.

Heuristic for iterating on chart-reading agents: when the agent fails on a specific chart, ask "what factor was this failure about?" Don't slot it into your existing skill categories by reflex. Let the failures update the taxonomy.

A user asks the agent: "Did the conversion rate drop more in mobile or web last quarter?" The agent has access to: (a) a daily line chart of mobile conversion, (b) a daily line chart of web conversion, (c) a daily line chart of both overlaid, (d) a bar chart of monthly conversion for both, side by side.   3. The audience-sensitive hypothesis says: pick the chart whose features are needed to answer the question. The question is a comparison. The chart should make the comparison answerable directly. The overlay (c) is the right pick — not because lines are better than bars in general, but because the comparison is what the question asks for.

Action items

  1. Audit one chart-reading evaluation you've shipped or relied on. If it was just an accuracy number, note the gap. Fan would say the gap matters more than the number. Add a wrong-answer-shape check next time, even informally.
  2. Find one chart-pick decision your agent made. Look at a chart the agent recently picked (for a dashboard, a report, a recommendation). Walk it back through the three steps: (1) what was the question? (2) what features of the chart answer it? (3) does the picked chart have those features? If not, that's a bar-plot-purist slip.
  3. Read the source. Watch 37:17–57:19 of Fan's talk for the plot-reading benchmark, the plot-selection experiment, and the measurement critique. Plus the Q&A (57:19–end) for misconception diagnosis and mechanistic interpretability.
