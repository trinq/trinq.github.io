Visual abstraction

Humans draw detail that scales with the lineup, not with the object. CLIPasso doesn't. Implications for any agent that renders diagrams.
~8 min Lesson 0002 glossary · taxonomy

Picture two scenes. In both, someone is playing a drawing game: they see a target object and they have to draw it so a partner can identify it.

Scene A. The lineup is five birds. The target is a robin. To uniquely identify the robin, you have to draw it carefully — beak shape, breast color, size. The lineup is full of close alternatives.

Scene B. The lineup is a robin, a teapot, a clock, a car, and a shoe. The target is the robin. To uniquely identify the robin, you can draw it as a small bird-shape with a beak. The lineup is full of distant alternatives.

Same target. Same drawer. Same task. Different drawings.

"We discovered how readily ordinary folks can adjust the way they use depiction to communicate — making more detailed and faithful drawings on those close trials when they needed their drawing to uniquely identify a particular exemplar, but then sparser drawings on the far trials when they could get away with these category-level abstractions."
— Judy Fan, 16:30

This is the first big finding of Fan's talk (the drawing-game studies, ~13:00–21:00). Detail scales with the referential context, not with the object. The drawing isn't a property of the robin. It's a property of the robin-and-its-lineup together.

The frame, restated: a drawing is not a representation of an object — it's a representation of an object given a question. Same for diagrams. Same for charts. Same for any visual artifact an agent produces. The agent that ignores the question will draw the wrong amount.

Dissociable vs. cumulative: how to explain vs. how to depict

The second big finding shifts the question from how much detail to what kind of detail. Holly Huey (Fan's PhD student, now at Adobe Research) ran a study where participants were shown six novel contraptions — each with a clear causal mechanism that turned on a light. Each participant was asked to draw two things for two different audiences:

  • A depiction: "your drawing will be used by someone to identify this object out of a lineup."
  • An explanation: "your drawing will be used by someone to understand how this object works."

Huey tagged every stroke (causal / non-causal / background / symbolic — arrows and motion lines) and ran three downstream tasks. The result, ~24:00:

Question for the viewer Which drawing wins? Why
"What kind of action operates this machine?" Explanation Causal parts and motion lines are emphasized.
"Which of these machines is this?" Depiction Background and surface features preserved.
"Which highlighted part is the causal one?" Tie Explanations drop too much background for the viewer to map parts.

The clean summary:

"Explanations better communicated how the mechanism worked, but depictions were better for communicating object identity."
— Judy Fan, 25:00

What this rules out: the cumulative hypothesis

There were two competing hypotheses going in:

  • Cumulative hypothesis: explanations are depictions + extra mechanism. Same visual style, more arrows.
  • Dissociable hypothesis: explanations and depictions use different visual strategies. Suppress surface detail; emphasize causal structure.

Fan's data rules out the strong cumulative hypothesis. If explanations were just depictions-with-extras, then depictions should be at least as good as explanations on every task. They're not — depictions fail the action task because they don't privilege the causal part.

The hypothesis that survived: dissociable. Explanation and depiction are not the same drawing with extra markup. They are different drawings, shaped for different questions, with overlapping but distinct vocabularies. The vocabulary overlaps (causal parts get drawn in both), but the relative weights are different.

The SEVA benchmark: humans sparsify, CLIPasso doesn't

The third leg of Part 1 (28:00–37:00) is a benchmark. Fan's team collected 90,000 sketches from 5,500 people of 128 visual concepts under four production budgets (think: "you have N seconds to draw"). They then compared human sketches to those produced by CLIPasso, a state-of-the-art neural sketch generator — both at matched recognizability.

The finding:

"Even while human drawings and CLIPasso's drawings were similarly recognizable across the four production budgets, humans and CLIPasso sparsify their drawings differently."
— Judy Fan, 31:30

Specifically: at the generous end (32 seconds for humans, 32 strokes for CLIPasso), the two distributions of meaning (which labels the sketch evokes) are close. At the tight end (4 seconds for humans, fewer strokes for CLIPasso), they diverge. CLIPasso's budget-compressed sketches evoke a different distribution of meanings than human budget-compressed sketches. They're functionally not the same drawing.

Three takeaways for agent design

You're not building CLIPasso. But you might be building (or choosing between) agents that render diagrams, sketches, or visual explanations. Fan's three findings collapse into three rules.

1. Detail scales with referential context, not with the object

An agent asked to draw the difference between Service A and Service B should draw Service A and Service B in detail. An agent asked to draw "what is a service" should draw any one service in minimal form. The lineup is the question, and the drawing is shaped by it.

Heuristic: don't pass the agent just the object. Pass the agent (object, comparison set, question). All three shape what the drawing should look like.

2. Explanation is dissociable from depiction

An agent asked to explain a system is not being asked to draw a depiction + arrows. It's being asked to draw a different drawing that suppresses surface and emphasizes causal structure. If the agent's first instinct is "let me render a faithful picture and then add motion lines," it's already on the wrong strategy.

Heuristic: for explanation tasks, set the agent's instructions to (a) identify the causal structure first, (b) suppress non-causal parts deliberately, (c) privilege arrows and motion lines over shading. The drawing is the causal diagram; the depiction is optional context.

3. Budget-constrained generation is qualitatively different, not just quantitatively

CLIPasso at 4 strokes isn't "a worse version of CLIPasso at 32 strokes" — it's a sketch that evokes a different distribution of meanings. The same will be true for any agent you push toward terseness. "Less detail" is not the same thing as "lower fidelity."

Heuristic: when you budget-constrain an agent's visual output (token cap, stroke cap, time cap), don't measure success with a single "did it look like the unconstrained version?" metric. Measure the distribution of meanings the output evokes, not just the top label. Lesson 0003 takes this further with a chart-reading benchmark.

Action items
