# Reading Nonfiction That Sticks — Glossary

Canonical terms used across lessons in this workspace. A term is added here only after the user has demonstrated they can use it correctly, not at the moment of first exposure. This is a record of compressed knowledge, not a vocabulary list.

## Reading science

**Schema**:
A pre-existing mental structure for organising knowledge in a domain. Activating a relevant schema before reading a text produces measurably better inferential comprehension than reading cold.
_Avoid_: Framework, mental model (overloaded), context (too vague)

**Steelman**:
The strongest, most charitable version of an argument — usually the one you disagree with. Building it before refuting it is the discipline that separates reading from thinking.
_Avoid_: Devil's advocate (overlaps but is more performative), counter-argument (too generic)

**Retrieval practice**:
Pulling an idea out of memory (writing it down, explaining it, testing yourself) rather than putting it back in (re-reading, re-highlighting). The act of retrieving strengthens the memory trace.
_Avoid_: Active recall (alias — fine to use), self-testing (alias)

## Workflow terminology

**Load-bearing idea**:
An idea that, if removed, would cause either the book's central argument to collapse, or two or more other ideas in the book to become un-understandable. Distinguished from *memorable* ideas, which can be vivid and important to the reader's experience without carrying the book's weight.
_Avoid_: Key takeaway, main point, important idea (all too vague to test)

**One-page artifact**:
The single page of outputs from the 4-prompt workflow: central argument in your own words, five load-bearing ideas with test results, strongest hostile-critic objection, one action. The artifact is yours, not the model's, and is the durable residue of having read the book.
_Avoid_: Book summary, book notes, reading notes (none capture that the output is a judgment, not a transcription)

**The 3-part test**:
Three questions applied to a candidate load-bearing idea: (1) Does it carry the central argument? (2) Does it unlock two or more other ideas? (3) Could you defend it against a hostile critic? An idea passes the test if it satisfies at least two of the three.
_Avoid_: Critical reading checklist (too generic), test of importance (the test is structural, not subjective)
