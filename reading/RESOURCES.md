# Reading Nonfiction That Sticks — Resources

## Knowledge

- [Article: "Retrieval-Based Learning: A Decade of Progress" — Roediger, Karpicke et al. (ERIC)](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED599273.pdf)
  The canonical review of why testing yourself beats re-reading. Use for: any claim about why the "explain it in your own words" prompt actually works.

- [Article: "Test-Enhanced Learning" — Roediger & Karpicke, 2006 (Psychological Science)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x)
  The original "testing effect" study showing that taking a test on prose you just read produces dramatically better delayed recall than re-studying the same prose. Use for: the "explain to a 14-year-old" prompt — the explanation *is* the test.

- [Article: "Desirable Difficulty: Theory and application" — Nelson, 2023 (Medical Education)](https://asmepublications.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/medu.14916)
  Peer-reviewed synthesis of the Bjork framework: conditions that feel harder during learning (retrieval, spacing, interleaving) produce stronger long-term retention. Use for: why the workflow feels effortful in the moment and why that is a feature.

- [Article: "Schema Activation for Reading Success" + the broader pre-reading research](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2307/1511053)
  Empirical base for the first prompt (extract the skeleton first, then read). Background knowledge activated before reading produces measurably better inferential comprehension. Use for: justifying the "skeleton before the flesh" idea in the lesson.

- [Article: "Value ratios: a key metric for your book chapters" — Josh Bernoff](https://bernoff.com/blog/value-ratios-a-key-metric-for-your-book-chapters)
  Replaces the "60–70% padding" claim in the original transcript with a sourced, testable metric: a well-structured nonfiction chapter should be >75% stories + advice, ≤25% proof/argumentation. Use for: the second prompt (which chapters are load-bearing).

- [Article: "Steel-manning Can Be More Useful Than Fact-Checking" — Card Catalog for Life](https://cardcatalogforlife.substack.com/p/steel-manning-can-be-more-useful)
  The "hostile critic" prompt is a steelman exercise. Use for: the third prompt — making the strongest possible case against the book before deciding whether to keep the book in your head.

- [Article: "The Feynman Learning Technique" — Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/feynman-learning-technique/)
  Primary, well-cited write-up of the explain-it-simply technique. The "explain to a 14-year-old" prompt is essentially the Feynman technique applied to a book. Use for: the fourth prompt and for designing future lessons on active recall.

- [Tool: NotebookLM official site (Google)](https://notebooklm.google/)
  Source-grounded AI tool — only uses the documents you upload, cites its claims inline. The "single central argument" prompt works *because* the model is bound to your book. Use for: every prompt in the workflow.

- [Book: *Cyberjutsu: Cybersecurity for the Modern Ninja* — Ben McCarty (No Starch Press, 2021)](https://nostarch.com/cyberjutsu)
  Used as the worked example in Lesson 0002. 26 chapters pairing historical ninja tactics with cybersecurity concepts (network mapping, sensors, social engineering, zero days, etc.). The recurring "Castle Theory" exercises and clean one-technique-per-chapter structure make it an ideal case for the 5-ideas exercise. Note: McCarty is a former NSA developer and the U.S. Army's first fully qualified cyber warfare specialist, so the analogy is informed, not decorative.

## Wisdom (Communities)

- [r/BookReviews](https://www.reddit.com/r/BookReviews/) and [r/nonfictionbookreviews](https://www.reddit.com/r/nonfictionbookreviews/)
  Use for: comparing your "one-sentence central argument" extraction against what other readers pulled out. If your compression matches the consensus, your reading is solid; if it doesn't, you probably read a different book than the one most people read.
- [Hacker News — books subgroup / monthly book threads](https://news.ycombinator.com/)
  Use for: hostile-critic practice. The comment culture actively attacks weak arguments; reading a thread on a book you just read is a free steelman exercise.

## Gaps

- **No high-quality empirical study comparing "skim + AI extract" reading vs. full-cover reading on long-term retention.** The 4-prompt workflow is plausible (it's built on real learning science) but the specific claim "30 minutes, retains 6 months later" has no controlled-trial backing. We teach the workflow *as* a habit to test on yourself, not as a proven protocol.
- **No good public source for the "60–70% padding" number.** Bernoff's "value ratio" is the real, testable version. We will not repeat the unverified figure in the lesson.
- **The 14-year-old compression prompt has not been studied as a specific intervention.** It is the Feynman technique in disguise, which *is* well-supported. We frame it that way.
