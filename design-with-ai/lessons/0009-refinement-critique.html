Refinement and Critique

Lesson 0009 · ~8 minutes · Meta-Skill

The first AI output is rarely the best output. The difference between good and great design is the iteration loop — knowing what to keep, what to change, and how to direct the next pass.

The Skill Above the Skills
Refinement is a meta-skill. It sits above prompting, above referencing, above asset generation. Master this and every other skill compounds.

The Critique Mindset

Critique is not criticism. It is structured observation: looking at what is, comparing it to what you wanted, and identifying the specific gap.

The best designers separate observation from judgment. First, describe what you see. Then, evaluate against intent. Only then, decide what to do.

The Critique Framework

When evaluating any design output — yours or AI's — use this four-step framework:

  1. What is it? — Describe what you see objectively. No judgment yet.
  2. What was intended? — Recall the prompt, the reference, the goal.
  3. What's the gap? — Identify specific differences between output and intent.
  4. What's the single next change? — Pick one focused refinement.

Skipping step 1 leads to vague feedback ("it feels off"). Skipping step 4 leads to rework loops that never converge.

The Refinement Ladder

Refinements exist at different levels. Knowing which level you're working at keeps iteration focused.

Level 1: Surface

Color, font, spacing values. Quick to change, often high impact. "Increase headline weight." "Tighten the line height."

Level 2: Structure

Layout, hierarchy, information architecture. "Move the CTA above the fold." "Group these features together."

Level 3: Concept

The fundamental approach or direction. "Try a completely different hero composition." "This isn't the right metaphor."

Level 4: Strategy

The problem being solved or audience being served. "We built for the wrong user." "The product itself needs rethinking."

Most iteration happens at Level 1. When Level 1 stops helping, move to Level 2. When that's exhausted, Level 3. The further up you climb, the more expensive the change — but sometimes necessary.

The "One Change" Rule

When directing AI for refinement, change one thing at a time. This gives you:

  • Clear cause-and-effect: you know what worked and what didn't
  • Faster convergence: small changes compound predictably
  • Easier prompts: each one is focused and unambiguous
  • Better learning: you build intuition about what drives quality

"Make it better" is not a refinement prompt. "Increase the CTA's contrast against the background" is.

The Visual Quality Checklist

Run every design through this checklist before declaring it done. If any item fails, you have your next refinement.

Hierarchy
The most important element is the most visually dominant
Eye flow matches intended reading order
Secondary elements support without competing
Typography
Font sizes follow a clear scale (no one-offs)
Line lengths stay within readable range (45-75 chars)
Line heights are appropriate for size and density
Font weights create clear contrast between levels
Spacing
Related elements are visually grouped (closer than unrelated)
Vertical rhythm is consistent across sections
No element feels cramped or accidentally isolated
Color
Palette is restricted and intentional
Contrast meets accessibility standards
Color use reinforces hierarchy, not decoration
Cohesion
Components feel like part of the same system
Assets match the design's tone and palette
Motion supports the same feeling as the visuals

Spotting "AI-Default" Patterns

AI output has recognizable defaults. Learn to spot them so you can override:

  • Purple-to-blue gradients — Default for tech products. Rarely the right choice.
  • Inter as the only font — Safe but generic. Consider alternatives.
  • Generic stock photo aesthetic — Even with AI generation, outputs can feel templated.
  • Symmetric, centered layouts — Safe but lifeless. Try asymmetric tension.
  • Vague copywriting — "Welcome to the future of productivity." Replace with specifics.
  • Three-column feature grids — Overused. Try other arrangements.
  • Cookie-cutter CTAs — "Get Started" everywhere. Make CTAs specific to the action.

When you see these, that's a signal to push further, not accept them.

The "Five Whys" for Design Problems

When something doesn't feel right but you can't articulate why, ask "why" five times:

  1. Why does this not work? → "The CTA doesn't stand out."
  2. Why doesn't it stand out? → "It's the same size as the body text."
  3. Why is it the same size? → "I didn't specify a CTA scale."
  4. Why didn't I specify? → "I assumed AI would default to a hierarchy."
  5. Why did I assume? → "I've been treating AI as the designer, not as a tool."

The root cause is rarely the visible problem. Going deeper reveals what to actually fix.

The Iteration Loop

Put it all together — this is the loop you run for every design:

  1. Generate with a detailed, complete prompt
  2. Step away briefly (5-10 minutes minimum)
  3. Return and describe what you see (no judgment)
  4. Compare to intent; identify the gap
  5. Pick one focused refinement at the right level
  6. Apply, then return to step 2
  7. Continue until the design passes the checklist

Typical count: 5-10 refinement passes for a single component. For a full page, expect 30-50 passes across components.

The Patience Lesson
Quality is the sum of focused refinements. The designers who ship the best work aren't the most talented — they're the most patient with iteration.

When to Stop

The biggest risk in iteration is never stopping. Knowing when "done" is done is itself a skill.

Stop when:

  • The checklist passes (or you've decided which failures to accept)
  • Further refinements make marginal, not meaningful, improvements
  • You're refining taste preferences, not objective issues
  • The design serves its purpose (launches, ships, gets used)

Perfectionism is expensive. Done and shipped beats perfect and never released.

Exercise: Run a Full Refinement Cycle

Practice · 45 minutes
Take one AI output and refine it through 5 passes.

Pick a component you generated in Lesson 0004 or 0006.

Pass 1: Step away for 5 minutes. Return. Describe what you see.

Pass 2: Apply the checklist. Identify the single biggest gap.

Pass 3: Make one focused refinement at Level 1 or 2.

Pass 4: Apply the Five Whys to anything that still feels off.

Pass 5: Make your final refinement. Run the checklist again.

Save before/after versions. Notice: how much did quality improve? At what level?

Key Takeaways

  • Critique is observation before judgment
  • Refinements exist at four levels: surface, structure, concept, strategy
  • One focused change beats broad rework
  • Spot AI defaults and override them
  • Five Whys reveals root causes
  • Knowing when to stop is as important as knowing what to change
