Shipping the Product

Lesson 0010 · ~6 minutes · Meta-Skill

A design that lives on a Figma canvas affects no one. A design that ships changes something. This final lesson covers the gap between "looks good in review" and "actually works in the world."

The Last Mile
Most design quality is lost between design and shipped product — not in the design itself. The last mile matters more than you think.

The Ship Readiness Checklist

Before declaring any design done, run this final check. Every item must be answered yes, or you have a known debt.

Functional
Every link and button does something
Forms submit and validate correctly
Empty states exist for every list
Error states are designed, not just text
Loading states exist for async actions
Responsive
Mobile layout works (test at 375px width)
Tablet layout works (test at 768px)
Desktop layout works (test at 1440px)
No horizontal scrolling at any size
Touch targets are at least 44x44px
Accessible
Color contrast meets WCAG AA (4.5:1 for text)
All images have alt text
Keyboard navigation works through all interactive elements
Focus states are visible and styled
Reduced-motion preference is respected
Performance
Images are optimized (correct format, sized for display)
Page loads in under 3 seconds on 3G
No layout shifts after initial load
Animations run at 60fps
Content
All placeholder copy is replaced
Spelling and grammar checked
Numbers, dates, and facts are accurate
Legal pages exist (privacy, terms)

Items that fail become known debt. Decide: fix now, fix in next sprint, or accept and document. Never pretend they don't exist.

The "Good Enough" Decision

Every design choice is a tradeoff. Knowing when to stop refining is a strategic skill.

Ship at 90%
When learning from users matters more than polish. When competition is moving.
Lower initial impression; faster feedback
Ship at 100%
When first impression defines the brand. When launch is the marketing moment.
Higher cost; slower learning
Ship, then iterate
When you have traffic. When you can measure impact.
Inconsistent experience during iteration
Kill it
When the design doesn't solve the real problem. When nobody uses it.
Sunk cost; but frees you to build what matters

There is no universal right answer. Match the decision to your context, not to abstract ideals.

The Five Launch Principles

1. Done is better than perfect

The design that ships teaches you. The one you keep refining teaches you nothing. Pick a date, hit it, learn from real use.

2. Real users over design opinions

Your aesthetic preferences matter less than whether users can complete their goals. Watch real people use it.

3. Document the debt, don't ignore it

Every known issue should be written down. Undocumented debt compounds. Documented debt becomes a roadmap.

4. Plan the iteration, not just the launch

The first version is the start, not the end. Before shipping, know what you'll measure and what you'll change based on it.

5. Ship the smallest useful thing

Every feature you don't ship is one fewer thing to test, maintain, and confuse users with. Cut until it hurts, then cut one more thing.

Post-Launch: What to Watch

The first 48 hours after launch tell you the most. Watch for:

  • Where users drop off — Funnels reveal what confused them
  • What they search for — Missing features or unclear language
  • What they message support about — Real pain points vs. imagined ones
  • Where they click that isn't clickable — Affordance failures
  • How long tasks take — Efficiency vs. expected ease

Use these signals to prioritize the next iteration. Don't redesign; fix specific, observed problems.

The Honest Reflection

After shipping, ask yourself three questions — not to judge, but to learn:

  1. What worked better than expected?
  2. What worked worse than expected?
  3. What would I do differently next time?

Capture answers in your learning log. This is how taste compounds. Each project teaches you something the previous one didn't.

Final Exercise: The Whole Loop

Capstone · 2-4 hours
Ship a complete product from scratch.

Apply everything from lessons 0001-0009 to a real project.

Phase 1 (Foundation): Define the product, audience, and taste direction (Lessons 0001-0002).

Phase 2 (Design): Build component-by-component using your reference library and prompts (Lessons 0003-0008).

Phase 3 (Refinement): Apply the critique framework and visual quality checklist (Lesson 0009).

Phase 4 (Ship): Run the ship readiness checklist, deploy, and observe (this lesson).

Phase 5 (Learn): Write a public case study of what you built, what worked, what didn't.

The Closing Thought

Where This Started
Lesson 0001 said AI can't replace your creativity. After ten lessons, you have the tools to prove it: taste, methods, references, prompts, assets, motion, critique, and shipping. The constraint was never the AI. It was always you.

You now have a complete system. The only remaining variable is how often you use it. Design compounds: every project sharpens your taste, every iteration teaches you something, every shipped product earns you the chance to ship another.
