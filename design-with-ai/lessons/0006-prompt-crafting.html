Prompt Crafting

Lesson 0006 · ~8 minutes · Skill

A great reference library doesn't help if you can't translate it into instructions. This lesson teaches you the prompt patterns that turn your taste into AI output.

The Core Principle
The best prompts give constraints, not directives. The more specific you are about what you want, the less AI has to guess — and the more your taste shines through.

The Anatomy of a Good Design Prompt

Every effective prompt contains the same five elements. Use this formula:

Prompt Formula
[Component scope]
+
[References]
+
[Branding context]
+
[Specific constraints]
+
[Feeling/tone]

When all five are present, AI has what it needs to execute well. When even one is missing, it defaults to generic.

The Five Elements in Practice

1. Component scope — Be specific about what you're building.

  • "Hero section" not "landing page"
  • "Pricing card" not "pricing"
  • "Mobile navigation menu" not "nav"

2. References — Show, don't tell. Attach screenshots from your library.

  • Always use multiple references for inspiration
  • Include the "do not copy directly" instruction

3. Branding context — Lock in your visual system.

  • Colors (specific hex codes if you have them)
  • Fonts (specific family names)
  • Tone (minimal, playful, premium, technical)

4. Specific constraints — The details that matter.

  • Spacing values: "generous padding (at least 80px vertical)"
  • Typography scale: "headline 64px, subhead 20px"
  • Interaction: "hover should feel subtle, not bouncy"
  • Content: "headline should be confident, not salesy"

5. Feeling/tone — The emotional target.

  • "Should feel calm, never urgent"
  • "Playful but not childish"
  • "Confident without being aggressive"

Before/After Examples

Bad Prompt
Build me a landing page for my SaaS tool.
Good Prompt
Create a hero section for [product name], an AI tool for content teams. References attached (3 screenshots). Use the typography hierarchy from #1, the spacing rhythm from #2, and the CTA treatment from #3 — but do not copy directly. Adapt to my brand. Branding: - Background: warm off-white (#FAF7F2) - Primary text: charcoal (#1A1A1A) - Accent: muted terracotta (#C4654A) - Font: Inter for body, a confident serif (like Fraunces) for headlines - Tone: warm, considered, intelligent — not cold or corporate Specifics: - Headline: bold but conversational, max 8 words - Subhead: 1-2 sentences explaining the product benefit - CTA: primary action ("Start free trial") + secondary ("Watch demo") - Generous whitespace; section should breathe - Subtle entry animation on scroll The hero should feel like an invitation, not a pitch.

The "Do Not Copy Directly" Rule

Always include this phrase when sharing references. Without it, AI will reproduce screens verbatim — producing something that looks familiar and feels derivative.

With it, AI extracts patterns: the typography rhythm here, the spacing scale there, the structural approach over there. The result is your own synthesis, not a copy.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Vague adjectives

"Modern, clean, beautiful" tells AI nothing. Replace with specifics: "8px border radius, 16px base spacing, sans-serif throughout."

Mistake 2: Too many references

More than 5-7 references dilutes direction. The more you show, the more AI averages everything. Pick 3-5 that share a clear direction.

Mistake 3: Skipping the feeling

Technical specifications without emotional targeting produce technically correct but emotionally empty design. Always include how it should feel.

Mistake 4: Not iterating

First output is rarely the best output. Plan for 2-4 refinement rounds. Each one should add or sharpen one specific thing.

The Iteration Pattern

Each refinement should be focused, not broad:

  • Round 1: Generate the initial design from the full prompt
  • Round 2: Refine typography — "Increase headline weight, tighten line height"
  • Round 3: Refine spacing — "Add more breathing room between sections"
  • Round 4: Refine tone — "The CTA feels too pushy; make it gentler"

Single-focus refinements get better results than "fix everything" prompts.

The Refinement Mindset
Don't ask AI to redo. Ask AI to refine one thing. Focused adjustments compound into quality.

Exercise: Write a Component Prompt

Practice · 25 minutes
Write a detailed prompt for a pricing section.

Use the product idea from previous lessons.

Step 1: Find 3-5 pricing section references from your library (or Mobbin/Awwwards).

Step 2: Apply the formula: scope + references + branding + constraints + feeling.

Step 3: Include the "do not copy directly" instruction.

Step 4: Generate, then iterate with 2 focused refinements.

Save the final result and your prompt. You'll reference it in future lessons.

Key Takeaways

  • Good prompts provide constraints, not directives
  • Five elements: scope, references, branding, constraints, feeling
  • "Do not copy directly" prevents verbatim reproduction
  • Iterate one focused change at a time
  • Vague adjectives produce vague results — always be specific
